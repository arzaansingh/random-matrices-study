\section*{Abstract}
\addcontentsline{toc}{section}{Abstract}

High-dimensional data sets, in which the number of measured variables is comparable to the number of observations, break the classical guarantees of multivariate statistics: the eigenvalues of a sample covariance matrix no longer converge to their population counterparts, and its eigenvectors can lose all contact with the population directions. This report develops the random matrix theory that replaces those guarantees, focusing on Gaussian sample covariance matrices and Johnstone's spiked covariance model. Starting from elementary probability, we follow the sample covariance matrix from the classical regime, where it obeys the law of large numbers and the central limit theorem, into the high-dimensional regime, where its spectrum spreads into the Marchenko-Pastur bulk and the largest eigenvalue exhibits Tracy-Widom fluctuations. Adding low-rank structure produces the spike phase transition: a signal direction is detectable, and its eigenvector informative, exactly when the signal strength crosses an explicit threshold set by the ratio of dimension to sample size. The same machinery is then shown to govern spectral clustering, with the similarity matrix of clustered data acting as a spiked model. The resulting accuracy formula is verified on the MNIST handwritten digits, where prediction and observation differ by 0.4 percentage points on average across all forty-five digit pairs. Monte Carlo simulations, reproducible from the companion Python notebooks, support every stage of the argument.

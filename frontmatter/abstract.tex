\section*{Abstract}
\addcontentsline{toc}{section}{Abstract}

High-dimensional data sets, in which the number of variables is comparable to the number of observations, break the classical limit theorems of multivariate statistics: the eigenvalues of a sample covariance matrix spread instead of converging, and its eigenvectors can lose all contact with the population directions. This report develops the random matrix theory that governs this regime and follows it, step by step, to a working clustering algorithm. For pure noise, the eigenvalues fill the Marchenko-Pastur bulk and the largest one fluctuates at the bulk's edge by the Tracy-Widom law. Adding a low-rank signal gives the spiked covariance model and its phase transition: an eigenvalue escapes the bulk, and its eigenvector aligns with the signal direction, exactly when the signal strength crosses the explicit threshold \(1 + \sqrt{y}\) set by the dimension-to-sample-size ratio \(y\). Spectral clustering is then the same mathematics in disguise: the similarity matrix of clustered data is itself a spiked model, its top eigenvectors carry one entry per observation, and the theory predicts the clustering accuracy in closed form, verified on the MNIST handwritten digits to within half a percent. Monte Carlo simulations, reproducible from the companion notebooks, support every step.

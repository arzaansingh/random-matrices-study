\subsection*{Why random matrices?}

To look for structure in a data set, one computes its sample covariance matrix and reads off the leading eigenvalues and eigenvectors. This is \vocab{principal components analysis}, and when the number of observations \(n\) is large relative to the number of variables \(p\), classical statistics guarantees that it works: the sample covariance matrix is close to the true population covariance \(\Sigma\), and its eigenvalues and eigenvectors converge to those of \(\Sigma\). Many modern data sets do not sit in that regime. When \(p\) is comparable to \(n\), the eigenvalues of the sample covariance matrix are no longer faithful \arzaancomment{is faithful the correct word? lets be engaging yes but prceise. if this is saying that it doesnt converge to the sigma, we need to be clear. i am not sure but you must be.} to \(\Sigma\), and the question this report studies is what they do instead, and when hidden low-dimensional structure can still be recovered from them \arzaancomment{again is it really recovering low dimensional structure or is it still high dimensional structure but its own unique interpretable version. tighten this sentence and make it less vague.}.

The departure from the classical picture is sharp and can be quantified. Consider the simplest case, pure noise: \(n\) independent Gaussian observations in \(\R^p\) whose population covariance is the identity, so every population eigenvalue equals \(1\) and there is no structure to recover \arzaancomment{again wtf is structure here, it seems so vague, idk what it means in this context}. Each entry of the sample covariance matrix is then an unbiased average, close to its true value once \(n\) is moderately large. The eigenvalues, however, do not concentrate at \(1\). As \(p\) and \(n\) grow together with the ratio \(y = p/n\) held fixed, they spread across the interval \([(1-\sqrt{y})^2, (1+\sqrt{y})^2]\); at \(y = 1/2\), realized with \(p = 500\) and \(n = 1000\), this is the interval \([0.09, 2.91]\) shown in Figure~\ref{fig:intro-regimes}. The largest sample eigenvalue then sits near \(2.91\), almost three times the truth, and the spread does not shrink as the sample grows so long as \(y\) is held fixed. \vocab{Random matrix theory} describes this behavior precisely, as a function of the single ratio \(y\) \citep{YaoZhengBai2015, Vershynin2018, CouilletLiao2022}. Its central consequence for this report is a threshold. Suppose a genuine signal is present: one population direction along which the variance is inflated to a level \(\alpha > 1\), with the rest of the spectrum left at \(1\). The sample spectrum reveals that signal only when \(\alpha\) exceeds \(1 + \sqrt{y}\). Above the threshold, an outlier eigenvalue separates from the noise and its eigenvector aligns, to a computable degree, with the true direction; below it, the signal leaves no detectable trace. \subsection*{What this report does, and how}

The report builds from the ground up. The probability and linear algebra it uses are developed in Appendix~\ref{sec:foundations}. Section~\ref{sec:scm} treats the null case \(\Sigma = I_p\): in fixed dimension the sample covariance matrix obeys the classical limit theorems, while in the high-dimensional regime \(p/n \to y\) its eigenvalues \arzaancomment{wdym fill, shouldnt it be follow. this is unclear} fill the \vocab{Marchenko-Pastur law} \citep{MarchenkoPastur1967} and the largest fluctuates at the edge of that bulk by the \vocab{Tracy-Widom law} \citep{TracyWidom1996, Johnstone2001}. This is the baseline any claim of signal must exceed. Section~\ref{sec:spiked} introduces signal one direction at a time: it establishes the detection threshold \(1 + \sqrt{y}\), locates the outlier eigenvalue and measures how well its eigenvector recovers the true direction, and asks what changes when two signals are present. The same section turns to \vocab{spectral clustering} \citep{vonLuxburg2007, NgJordanWeiss2002}, where the similarity matrix of grouped data is again a \arzaancomment{again what does low rank signal in noise mean, this is unclear} low-rank signal in noise and the leading eigenvectors estimate the group labels; the resulting accuracy formula is tested on synthetic data and on the MNIST digits. Section~\ref{sec:conclusion} summarizes and points beyond.

Two conventions hold throughout. The report is a guided reconstruction of known results, not new mathematics: each claim is proved, sketched with a reference to a complete proof, or cited at the precise statement used, and the \arzaancomment{is deepest correctly used here? what does that mean} deepest limit laws are stated and simulated rather than proved. And each theoretical claim is accompanied by a Monte Carlo simulation, reproducible from the notebooks in the accompanying repository; the figures are evidence, not decoration.

\subsection*{How to read this report}

The prerequisites are a first course in probability and one in linear algebra; a reader who can state the central limit theorem and diagonalize a symmetric matrix has enough. All of it is developed from the beginning in Appendix~\ref{sec:foundations}, written to be read in sequence. A reader new to this material should start there; a reader already comfortable with it can begin at Section~\ref{sec:scm} and use the appendix for reference, since the main text cites the exact result it needs at each step. Every symbol is collected, with a single fixed meaning, in the notation table of Appendix~\ref{app:notation}, which can be consulted whenever a symbol is unfamiliar. We begin where the classical theory is exact, with \(p\) fixed and \(n\) growing, and add dimension from there.

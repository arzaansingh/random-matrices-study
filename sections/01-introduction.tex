\section{Introduction}\label{sec:introduction}

\subsection*{Why random matrices?}

Thousands of handwritten digits arrive scanned and unlabeled, and the task is to sort them with no help: every zero into one pile, every one into another, with no idea in advance of what a zero or a one should look like. Each image is a grid of \(28 \times 28\) pixels, a single point in \(784\) dimensions, and two images of the same digit ought to sit near each other there. To find that hidden grouping, the natural move is to look at the directions along which the images vary the most, the leading eigenvectors of a matrix built from the data. This is the idea behind \vocab{principal components analysis}, and it is the engine of the \vocab{spectral clustering} that this report builds toward. High dimension, though, plays a trick that has to be understood first.

Strip the data down to pure noise, independent random numbers whose true covariance is the identity, so that there is genuinely nothing to find. The eigenvalues misbehave anyway. Instead of sitting at their true value of \(1\), they spread across the interval from \(0.09\) to \(2.91\) (Figure~\ref{fig:intro-regimes}, right): a dominant ``component'' nearly three times the average, a graded tail behind it, the very look of hidden structure, all of it an artifact of dimension. The failure is subtle, because nothing is wrong with the underlying numbers. Each entry of the sample covariance matrix is an honest average that, with a thousand observations, misses its mark by only a few hundredths. The eigenvalues are another story: as the dimension and the sample size grow with their ratio held fixed, that spread does not shrink but converges to the very interval shown. The matrix is right in every entry and wrong in every eigenvalue, and no larger sample of the same proportions will fix it.

The trap is a feature of dimension, not of any one data set: it appears wherever the number of variables \(p\) rivals the number of observations \(n\), as in a genomics experiment measuring tens of thousands of genes in a few hundred cells, or a portfolio of hundreds of assets followed over a few years of trading. The object at the center of it all is the \vocab{sample covariance matrix}
\begin{equation}
\Sigmahat_n \;=\; \frac{1}{n} \sum_{i=1}^n \vect{X}_i \vect{X}_i\transpose,
\label{eq:scm-intro}
\end{equation}
where the \(\vect{X}_i \in \R^p\) are the \(n\) observed data points, assumed centered. When \(p\) is small and \(n\) is large, the classical picture holds and the eigenvalues land where they should (Figure~\ref{fig:intro-regimes}, left). In the modern regime they do not, and the eigenvectors fare no better.

\vocab{Random matrix theory} is the framework that explains and predicts what the eigenvalues and eigenvectors of large random matrices actually do \citep{YaoZhengBai2015, Vershynin2018, CouilletLiao2022}. For the questions of this report it delivers a complete answer, organized by a single threshold. A signal direction planted in the population covariance, carrying variance above the threshold \(1 + \sqrt{y}\), where \(y = p/n\), produces an isolated outlier eigenvalue and an eigenvector measurably aligned with the truth; below it, the signal is absorbed into the noise and the eigenvector retains no information. In the limit, detection switches on all at once: a genuine phase transition. The promise of this report is to build that statement from first principles, watch it happen in simulation at realistic sizes, and then collect the payoff: the same mathematics decides when data can be sorted into groups at all. Back at the digits we began with: on twelve thousand of them, the theory's accuracy formula, which has no free parameters, lands within half a percentage point of what we observe.

\subsection*{What this report does, and how}

The route runs from nothing to something, building on the probability and linear algebra collected in Appendix~\ref{sec:foundations}. Section~\ref{sec:scm} studies the null case \(\Sigma = I_p\), where there is nothing to detect: in fixed dimension the sample covariance matrix behaves like the classical average it is. In the high-dimensional regime its eigenvalues spread into the bulk of Figure~\ref{fig:intro-regimes}, the \vocab{Marchenko-Pastur law} \citep{MarchenkoPastur1967}, and the largest one fluctuates at the bulk's edge by the \vocab{Tracy-Widom law} \citep{TracyWidom1996, Johnstone2001}. This is the null baseline any signal must beat. Section~\ref{sec:spiked} then plants signal, one spiked direction at a time: it states and verifies the phase transition, locates the outlier exactly, quantifies how well its eigenvector recovers the true direction, and asks what changes when two spikes compete. The same section turns to its application, \vocab{spectral clustering} \citep{vonLuxburg2007, NgJordanWeiss2002}: data with hidden groups turn out to be a spiked model in disguise, the top eigenvectors act as noisy labels that tell the groups apart, and the theory becomes a formula for how often those labels are right. The formula is tested on synthetic data and then on the MNIST handwritten digits. Section~\ref{sec:conclusion} closes with what was found and what lies beyond.

The report is a guided reconstruction of known results, not new mathematics, and it keeps two promises. Every claim is proved in the text, sketched with a pointer to a full proof, or cited at the precise statement used; the deepest limit laws, Tracy-Widom among them, are stated, simulated, and cited rather than proved. And every theoretical claim comes with a Monte Carlo experiment, a repeated randomized simulation, reproducible from the accompanying notebooks; the figures are evidence, not decoration.

\subsection*{How to read this report}

The prerequisites are a first course in probability and one in linear algebra; a fair self-test is whether you can state the central limit theorem and diagonalize a symmetric matrix. A reader meeting any of these topics for the first time, or wanting a refresher, should start with Appendix~\ref{sec:foundations}, where everything is developed from scratch; a reader comfortable with them can start at Section~\ref{sec:scm} and treat the appendix as a reference: the main text cites the exact result it needs at every step. We begin where classical statistics is strongest, \(p\) fixed and \(n\) growing; by the last section, Figure~\ref{fig:intro-regimes} should read not as a pair of histograms but as a pair of theorems.

\section{Introduction}\label{sec:introduction}

\subsection*{Why random matrices?}

Take one thousand independent samples of a five-hundred-dimensional Gaussian vector whose population covariance is the identity, and compute the sample covariance matrix. Every one of its entries is an honest average, and every one is a good estimate: the true diagonal entries are \(1\), the true off-diagonal entries are \(0\), and a typical entry misses by only a few hundredths. The eigenvalues are another story. Instead of concentrating near \(1\), they spread across the interval from \(0.09\) to \(2.91\), the largest nearly three times the truth and the smallest less than a tenth of it (Figure~\ref{fig:intro-regimes}, right). This is not bad luck that a larger sample could fix: as the dimension and the sample size grow with their ratio held fixed, the spread converges to this interval. The matrix is right in every entry and wrong in every eigenvalue.

The culprit is the regime: five hundred variables measured against only one thousand observations. When the dimension \(p\) is small and the sample size \(n\) is large, the classical picture holds and the eigenvalues land where they should (Figure~\ref{fig:intro-regimes}, left). But modern data live in the other regime. A genomics experiment measures tens of thousands of expression levels in a few hundred cells; a portfolio tracks hundreds of returns over a few years of trading days; a vision system compares image patches in feature spaces of hundreds of dimensions. In each case \(p\) is comparable to \(n\) or larger, and the most basic question one can ask of the data, which directions carry the most variance, is answered by the eigenvalues and eigenvectors of the \vocab{sample covariance matrix}
\begin{equation}
\Sigmahat_n \;=\; \frac{1}{n} \sum_{i=1}^n \vect{X}_i \vect{X}_i\transpose,
\label{eq:scm-intro}
\end{equation}
where the \(\vect{X}_i \in \R^p\) are the \(n\) observed data points, assumed centered. That computation is \vocab{principal components analysis}, and Figure~\ref{fig:intro-regimes} says its raw output cannot be taken at face value in high dimension; the eigenvectors, as we will see, fare no better than the eigenvalues.

\vocab{Random matrix theory} is the framework that explains and predicts what the eigenvalues and eigenvectors of large random matrices actually do \citep{YaoZhengBai2015, Vershynin2018, CouilletLiao2022}. For the questions of this report it delivers something rare: a complete, explicit answer, organized by a single threshold. A signal direction planted in the population covariance, carrying variance above the threshold \(1 + \sqrt{y}\), where \(y = p/n\), produces an isolated outlier eigenvalue and an eigenvector measurably aligned with the truth; below that strength, the signal is absorbed into the noise and the eigenvector retains no information at all. Nothing about this is gradual: in the limit, detection switches on all at once, a genuine phase transition. The promise of this report is to build that statement from first principles, watch it happen in simulation at realistic sizes, and then collect the payoff. The same kind of phase transition, transplanted to the similarity matrix of clustered data, yields a formula with no free parameters for the accuracy of spectral clustering; on twelve thousand real handwritten digits, the prediction lands within half a percentage point of what we observe.

\subsection*{What this report does, and how}

The route runs from nothing to something. Section~\ref{sec:scm} studies the null case \(\Sigma = I_p\), where there is nothing to detect: in fixed dimension the sample covariance matrix behaves like the classical average it is, while in the high-dimensional regime its eigenvalues spread into the bulk described by the \vocab{Marchenko-Pastur law} \citep{MarchenkoPastur1967} of Figure~\ref{fig:intro-regimes}, and the largest one fluctuates at that bulk's edge by the \vocab{Tracy-Widom law} \citep{TracyWidom1996, Johnstone2001}. This is the null baseline that any claim of signal must beat. Section~\ref{sec:spiked} then plants signal, one spiked direction at a time: it states and verifies the phase transition, locates the outlier eigenvalue exactly, quantifies how well the corresponding eigenvector recovers the true direction, and asks what changes when two spikes compete. With the machinery complete, the same section turns to its application, \vocab{spectral clustering} \citep{vonLuxburg2007, NgJordanWeiss2002}. The similarity matrix built from clustered data is again a spiked model, and its top eigenvectors carry the class structure, one entry per observation, fluctuating around scaled-down copies of the class-membership vectors. The resulting accuracy formula is tested on synthetic mixtures and on the MNIST handwritten digits. Section~\ref{sec:conclusion} closes with what was found and what lies beyond.

About rigor and evidence, the report keeps two promises. First, it is a guided reconstruction of known results, not new mathematics: every claim is proved in the text, sketched with a pointer to a full proof, or cited at the precise statement used, and the deepest limit laws, such as Tracy-Widom at the edge, are stated, simulated, and cited rather than proved. Second, every theoretical claim is accompanied by a Monte Carlo experiment at realistic sizes, with every figure reproducible from the Python notebooks in the accompanying repository. The asymptotics of this subject describe finite plots remarkably well, and the reader should treat the figures as evidence, not decoration.

\subsection*{How to read this report}

The prerequisites are a first course in probability and one in linear algebra; a fair self-test is whether you can state the central limit theorem and diagonalize a symmetric matrix. All of it is developed from the ground up in Appendix~\ref{sec:foundations}, written as a self-contained course running from random variables through the limit theorems to the spectral theorem, and everything in it feeds the analysis of \(\Sigmahat_n\). A reader meeting any of these topics for the first time, or wanting a refresher, should read Appendix~\ref{sec:foundations} before Section~\ref{sec:scm}; a reader comfortable with them can start at Section~\ref{sec:scm} directly and treat the appendix as a reference, since the main text cites the specific definition or theorem it needs at every step. Three further appendices collect auxiliary probabilistic results, the simulation methodology, and selected code listings.

We begin where classical statistics is at its strongest: \(p\) fixed and \(n\) growing. By the last section, Figure~\ref{fig:intro-regimes} should read not as a pair of histograms but as a pair of theorems.

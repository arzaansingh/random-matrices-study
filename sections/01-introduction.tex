\section{Introduction}\label{sec:introduction}

\subsection*{Why random matrices?}

In order to reveal structure in a data set, the methodology of \vocab{principal components analysis} makes use of the leading eigenvalues and eigenvectors of sample covariance matrices. When the number of observations \(n\) is large relative to the number of variables \(p\), classical statistical theory guarantees that this idea is sound. In fact, by the classical strong law of large numbers (SLLN), the sample covariance matrix is close to the population covariance \(\Sigma\), and its eigenvalues converge to those of \(\Sigma\), as do its eigenvectors when the population eigenvalues are simple. Nevertheless, many modern data sets do not sit in that regime. In the Big Data era, several data sets are \textit{high-dimensional}, that is, \(p\) is comparable to \(n\).\gdmark{intro-blue} In this setting, the guarantees provided by the SLLN do not hold, and the sample eigenvalues no longer converge to those of \(\Sigma\). What happens instead, and how the information available in the data set can be used, is the subject of this report.\gdmark{intro-filler}

Consider the simplest case: measurements are standard Gaussian noise. Then the population covariance is the identity, and every direction in \(\R^p\) has the same variance \(1\).\gdmark{intro-nosignal} Each entry of the sample covariance matrix is then an unbiased average, close to its true value once \(n\) is moderately large. The eigenvalues do not concentrate at \(1\). As \(p\) and \(n\) grow together with the ratio \(y = p/n\) held fixed, they spread across the asymptotic support \([(1-\sqrt{y})^2, (1+\sqrt{y})^2]\). An example is provided in Figure~\ref{fig:intro-regimes}: there we take \(y = 1/2\), \(p = 500\) and \(n = 1000\), in which case the support is \([0.09, 2.91]\). Then the largest sample eigenvalue sits near \(2.91\), almost three times the population eigenvalue \(1\),\gdmark{intro-truth} and the spread does not shrink as the sample grows so long as \(y\) is held fixed. The entries are accurate; their eigenvalues, taken together, are not.\gdmark{intro-repeat}

\vocab{Random matrix theory} describes this spreading of the sample eigenvalues precisely, as a function of the single ratio \(y\) \citep{YaoZhengBai2015, Vershynin2018, CouilletLiao2022}. Its central consequence for this report is a threshold. Suppose a genuine signal is present: one population direction along which the variance is inflated to a level \(\alpha > 1\), with the rest of the spectrum left at \(1\). The sample spectrum reveals that signal only when \(\alpha\) exceeds \(1 + \sqrt{y}\). Above the threshold, an outlier eigenvalue separates from the noise and its eigenvector aligns, to a computable degree, with the true direction; below it, the signal leaves no detectable trace. The transition is sharp: as the dimension grows, the signal becomes detectable once \(\alpha\) crosses \(1 + \sqrt{y}\), and not before.

The same kind of threshold governs a problem that looks, at first, unrelated: the machine learning task of sorting data into groups. The sample covariance matrix is \(p \times p\), with one entry for each pair of variables. The analogue for clustering is an \(n \times n\) \vocab{similarity matrix}, with one entry for each pair of data points, recording how close the two are.\gdmark{intro-variables} It is the same data viewed the other way around, and the theory carries over to \vocab{spectral clustering} \citep{vonLuxburg2007, NgJordanWeiss2002}. When the data fall into several groups, each group contributes one strong direction that stands out against the noise. The leading eigenvectors of the similarity matrix estimate which group each point belongs to. Just as for a covariance spike, the groups separate only when their signal clears the threshold.\gdmark{intro-shorten} Above it, the theory gives an explicit formula for the accuracy, the limiting fraction of points that spectral clustering sorts correctly. We test this on the MNIST handwritten digits, clustering the images of \(0\) and \(1\) using none of the labels. The achieved accuracy closely matches the formula's prediction (Section~\ref{subsec:mnist}).\gdmark{intro-mnist}

\subsection*{What this report does, and how}

The report builds from the ground up. The probability and linear algebra it uses are developed in Appendix~\ref{sec:foundations}. Section~\ref{sec:scm} treats the null case \(\Sigma = I_p\): in fixed dimension the sample covariance matrix obeys the classical limit theorems. In the high-dimensional regime \(p/n \to y\), its eigenvalues follow the \vocab{Marchenko-Pastur law} \citep{MarchenkoPastur1967}, and the largest fluctuates at the edge of that bulk by the \vocab{Tracy-Widom law} \citep{TracyWidom1996, Johnstone2001}. This is the baseline any claim of signal must exceed. Section~\ref{sec:spiked} introduces signal one direction at a time. It establishes the detection threshold \(1 + \sqrt{y}\), locates the outlier eigenvalue, and measures how well its eigenvector recovers the true direction; a final subsection treats two signals at once. Section~\ref{sec:clustering} then turns to \vocab{spectral clustering}. There the similarity matrix of grouped data carries the same spiked structure, one strong direction per group, and the leading eigenvectors estimate the group labels. The resulting accuracy formula is tested on synthetic data and on the MNIST digits. Section~\ref{sec:conclusion} summarizes the results and points to open directions.

Two conventions hold throughout. The report is a guided reconstruction of known results, not new mathematics. Most claims are cited at the precise statement used or sketched with a reference to a complete proof; some are proved in full, and the most difficult limit laws are stated and simulated rather than proved.\gdmark{intro-proved} Moreover, each theoretical claim is accompanied by a Monte Carlo simulation, reproducible from the notebooks in the accompanying repository; the figures are evidence, not decoration.

\subsection*{How to read this report}

The prerequisites are a first course in probability and one in linear algebra. A reader who can state the central limit theorem and diagonalize a symmetric matrix has enough; Appendix~\ref{sec:foundations} develops the rest from the beginning.\gdmark{intro-longsentence} A reader new to this material should start there. One already comfortable with it can begin at Section~\ref{sec:scm}, since the main text cites the exact result it needs at each step; the notation table of Appendix~\ref{app:notation} resolves any unfamiliar symbol. We begin where the classical theory is exact, with \(p\) fixed and \(n\) growing, and add dimension from there.

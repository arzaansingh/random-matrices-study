\section{Introduction}\label{sec:introduction}

\subsection*{Why random matrices?}

Take one thousand independent samples of a five-hundred-dimensional Gaussian vector whose population covariance is the identity, and compute the sample covariance matrix. Every one of its entries is an honest average, and every one is a good estimate: the true diagonal entries are \(1\), the true off-diagonal entries are \(0\), and a typical entry misses by only a few hundredths. The eigenvalues are another story. Instead of concentrating near \(1\), they spread across the interval from \(0.09\) to \(2.91\), the largest nearly three times the truth and the smallest less than a tenth of it (Figure~\ref{fig:intro-regimes}, right). This is not bad luck: as dimension and sample size grow with their ratio held fixed, the spread converges to this interval. The matrix is right in every entry and wrong in every eigenvalue.

The culprit is the regime: five hundred variables measured against only one thousand observations. When the dimension \(p\) is small and the sample size \(n\) is large, the classical picture holds and the eigenvalues land where they should (Figure~\ref{fig:intro-regimes}, left). But modern data live in the other regime. A genomics experiment measures tens of thousands of gene expressions in a few hundred cells; a portfolio tracks hundreds of assets over a few years of trading days; a vision system works in feature spaces of hundreds of dimensions. In each case \(p\) is comparable to \(n\) or larger, and the most basic question one can ask of the data, which directions carry the most variance, is answered by the eigenvalues and eigenvectors of the \vocab{sample covariance matrix}
\begin{equation}
\Sigmahat_n \;=\; \frac{1}{n} \sum_{i=1}^n \vect{X}_i \vect{X}_i\transpose,
\label{eq:scm-intro}
\end{equation}
where the \(\vect{X}_i \in \R^p\) are the \(n\) observed data points, assumed centered. That computation is \vocab{principal components analysis}, and Figure~\ref{fig:intro-regimes} says its raw output cannot be taken at face value in high dimension; the eigenvectors fare no better.

\vocab{Random matrix theory} is the framework that explains and predicts what the eigenvalues and eigenvectors of large random matrices actually do \citep{YaoZhengBai2015, Vershynin2018, CouilletLiao2022}. For the questions of this report it delivers something rare: a complete answer, organized by a single threshold. A signal direction planted in the population covariance, carrying variance above the threshold \(1 + \sqrt{y}\), where \(y = p/n\), produces an isolated outlier eigenvalue and an eigenvector measurably aligned with the truth; below it, the signal is absorbed into the noise and the eigenvector retains no information. In the limit, detection switches on all at once: a genuine phase transition. The promise of this report is to build that statement from first principles, watch it happen in simulation at realistic sizes, and then collect the payoff: the very same mathematics decides when data can be sorted into groups at all. On twelve thousand real handwritten digits, it predicts the accuracy of spectral clustering before we run it, and the prediction lands within half a percentage point of what we observe.

\subsection*{What this report does, and how}

The route runs from nothing to something, building on the probability and linear algebra collected in Appendix~\ref{sec:foundations}. Section~\ref{sec:scm} studies the null case \(\Sigma = I_p\), where there is nothing to detect: in fixed dimension the sample covariance matrix behaves like the classical average it is; in the high-dimensional regime its eigenvalues spread into the bulk of Figure~\ref{fig:intro-regimes}, the \vocab{Marchenko-Pastur law} \citep{MarchenkoPastur1967}, and the largest one fluctuates at the bulk's edge by the \vocab{Tracy-Widom law} \citep{TracyWidom1996, Johnstone2001}. This is the null baseline any signal must beat. Section~\ref{sec:spiked} then plants signal, one spiked direction at a time: it states and verifies the phase transition, locates the outlier exactly, quantifies how well its eigenvector recovers the true direction, and asks what changes when two spikes compete. The same section then turns to its application, \vocab{spectral clustering} \citep{vonLuxburg2007, NgJordanWeiss2002}: data with hidden groups turn out to be a spiked model in disguise, the top eigenvectors act as noisy labels that tell the groups apart, and the theory becomes a formula for how often those labels are right. The formula is tested on synthetic data and then on the MNIST handwritten digits. Section~\ref{sec:conclusion} closes with what was found and what lies beyond.

The report is a guided reconstruction of known results, not new mathematics, and it keeps two promises: every claim is proved in the text, sketched with a pointer to a full proof, or cited at the precise statement used (the deepest limit laws, Tracy-Widom among them, are stated, simulated, and cited rather than proved); and every theoretical claim comes with a Monte Carlo experiment, reproducible from the accompanying notebooks. The figures are evidence, not decoration.

\subsection*{How to read this report}

\enlargethispage{2\baselineskip}

The prerequisites are a first course in probability and one in linear algebra; a fair self-test is whether you can state the central limit theorem and diagonalize a symmetric matrix, and all of it is developed from scratch in the self-contained course of Appendix~\ref{sec:foundations}. A reader meeting any of these topics for the first time, or wanting a refresher, should read Appendix~\ref{sec:foundations} first; a reader comfortable with them can start at Section~\ref{sec:scm} and treat the appendix as a reference: the main text cites the exact result it needs at every step. We begin where classical statistics is at its strongest: \(p\) fixed and \(n\) growing. By the last section, Figure~\ref{fig:intro-regimes} should read not as a pair of histograms but as a pair of theorems.

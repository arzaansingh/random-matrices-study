\section{Introduction}\label{sec:introduction}

\subsection*{Why random matrices?}

\GD{In order to reveal structure in a data set, the methodology of \vocab{principal components analysis} makes use of the leading eigenvalues and eigenvectors of sample covariance matrices. When} the number of observations \(n\) is large relative to the number of variables \(p\), classical \GD{statistical theory} guarantees that \GD{this idea is sound. In fact, by the classical strong law of large numbers (SLLN), }the sample covariance matrix is close to the true population covariance \(\Sigma\), and its eigenvalues and eigenvectors converge to those of \(\Sigma\). \GD{Nevertheless,} many modern data sets do not sit in that regime. \GD{In the modern Big Data era, several data sets are \textit{high-dimensional}, i.e., \(p\) is comparable to \(n\). In this setting, the guarantees provided by the SLLN do not old, and} the sample eigenvalues no longer converge to those of \(\Sigma\). \GD{What happens instead, and how the information available in the data set can be used, is the subject of this report.}

\GD{For the sake of illustration, consider the simplest case, which is when measurements are just standard Gaussian noise. Then, the population covariance is the identity, and every direction in \(\R^p\) has the same variance. Hence, no signal is available.} \GDcomment{Here, one is mixing up two different issues: the geometry of spiked eigenvalues and the MP phenomenon.} Each entry of the sample covariance matrix is then an unbiased average, close to its true value once \(n\) is moderately large. However, the eigenvalues do not concentrate at \(1\). As \(p\) and \(n\) grow together with the ratio \(y = p/n\) held fixed, they spread across the \GD{asymptotic support} \([(1-\sqrt{y})^2, (1+\sqrt{y})^2]\). \vocab{Random matrix theory} describes this behavior precisely, as a function of the single ratio \(y\) \citep{YaoZhengBai2015, Vershynin2018, CouilletLiao2022}. Its central consequence for this report is a threshold. Suppose a genuine signal is present: one population direction along which the variance is inflated to a level \(\alpha > 1\), with the rest of the spectrum left at \(1\). The sample spectrum reveals that signal only when \(\alpha\) exceeds \(1 + \sqrt{y}\). Above the threshold, an outlier eigenvalue separates from the noise and its eigenvector aligns, to a computable degree, with the true direction; below it, the signal leaves no detectable trace. The transition is sharp: as the dimension grows, the signal becomes detectable \GD{once} \(\alpha\) crosses \(1 + \sqrt{y}\), and not before.

The same kind of threshold governs a problem that looks, at first, unrelated: \GD{the machine learning task of} sorting data into groups. The sample covariance matrix is \(p \times p\) and compares the variables \GDcomment{The variables?}; the analogue for clustering is an \(n \times n\) \vocab{similarity matrix} that compares the data points instead, the same data viewed the other way around, and the theory carries over to \vocab{spectral clustering} \citep{vonLuxburg2007, NgJordanWeiss2002}. When the data fall into several groups, that grouping shows up as a few strong directions, one per group, standing out against the noise. \GDcomment{It's a good idea to write shorter sentences.} The leading eigenvectors of the similarity matrix estimate which group each point belongs to; and, exactly as for a covariance spike, the groups can be separated only when their signal is strong enough to clear the threshold. Above it, the theory gives an explicit formula for the fraction of points a method will cluster correctly. \GD{We test this on a celebrated data set, the so-called MNIST handwritten digits.} We sort twelve thousand images of the digits \(0\) and \(1\) into two groups, using none of the labels. The method places \(93.4\%\) of them in the right group; the formula predicted \(93.8\%\), a match to within half a percentage point. \GDcomment{Too many details for an introduction.}

\subsection*{What this report does, and how}

The report builds from the ground up. The probability and linear algebra it uses are developed in Appendix~\ref{sec:foundations}. Section~\ref{sec:scm} treats the null case \(\Sigma = I_p\): in fixed dimension the sample covariance matrix obeys the classical limit theorems, while in the high-dimensional regime \(p/n \to y\) its eigenvalues follow the \vocab{Marchenko-Pastur law} \citep{MarchenkoPastur1967} and the largest fluctuates at the edge of that bulk by the \vocab{Tracy-Widom law} \citep{TracyWidom1996, Johnstone2001}. This is the baseline any claim of signal must exceed. Section~\ref{sec:spiked} introduces signal one direction at a time: it establishes the detection threshold \(1 + \sqrt{y}\), locates the outlier eigenvalue and measures how well its eigenvector recovers the true direction, and asks what changes when two signals are present. Section~\ref{sec:clustering} then turns to \vocab{spectral clustering}, where the similarity matrix of grouped data carries the same spiked structure, one strong direction per group, and the leading eigenvectors estimate the group labels; the resulting accuracy formula is tested on synthetic data and on the MNIST digits. Section~\ref{sec:conclusion} summarizes and points beyond.

Two conventions hold throughout. The report is a guided reconstruction of known results, not new mathematics: each claim is proved \GDcomment{This is not really true. :) }, sketched with a reference to a complete proof, or cited at the precise statement used, and the most difficult limit laws are stated and simulated rather than proved. \GD{Moreover,} each theoretical claim is accompanied by a Monte Carlo simulation, reproducible from the notebooks in the accompanying repository; the figures are evidence, not decoration.

\subsection*{How to read this report}

The prerequisites are a first course in probability and one in linear algebra: a reader who can state the central limit theorem and diagonalize a symmetric matrix has enough, and Appendix~\ref{sec:foundations} develops the rest from the beginning. \GDcomment{This last sentence is long.} A reader new to this material should start there; one already comfortable with it can begin at Section~\ref{sec:scm}, since the main text cites the exact result it needs at each step, and can consult the notation table of Appendix~\ref{app:notation} whenever a symbol is unfamiliar. We begin where the classical theory is exact, with \(p\) fixed and \(n\) growing, and add dimension from there.

\section{Spiked Covariance Matrices and the Spike Phase Transition}\label{sec:spiked}

The Marchenko-Pastur and Tracy-Widom results of Section~\ref{subsec:scm-highdim} describe the sample spectrum under the null hypothesis \(\Sigma = I_p\). Real datasets carry structure: a small number of distinguished directions account for most of the variance (regulatory pathways in single-cell genomics, market factors in finance, edge orientations in image processing). The simplest model that captures this picture, introduced by \citet{Johnstone2001} and developed systematically by \citet[][Ch.~11]{YaoZhengBai2015}, allows a small number of population eigenvalues to exceed the noise baseline of \(1\). The questions this section asks are when such a \emph{spike} produces a detectable outlier in the sample spectrum (Section~\ref{subsec:johnstone}--\ref{subsec:bbp}), what the corresponding fluctuations look like (Section~\ref{subsec:fluctuations}), and what the sample eigenvector reveals about the population spike direction (Section~\ref{subsec:eigenvector}). The answer is a clean phase transition with an explicit threshold determined by the aspect ratio \(y\) \citep{BaikBenArousPeche2005, BaikSilverstein2006}.

\subsection{Johnstone's Spiked Model and the Spike-Location Map}\label{subsec:johnstone}

We fix the model first.

\begin{definition}[Johnstone's spiked covariance model]\label{def:spiked-model}
Fix an integer \(m \ge 0\) (the number of \vocab{spikes}) and real numbers
\[
  \alpha_1 \;\ge\; \alpha_2 \;\ge\; \cdots \;\ge\; \alpha_m \;>\; 0,\qquad \alpha_j \ne 1.
\]
The \vocab{Johnstone spiked covariance} of dimension \(p \ge m + 1\) is
\begin{equation}
  \Sigma \;=\; \diag(\alpha_1, \dots, \alpha_m, \underbrace{1, \dots, 1}_{p - m \text{ times}}) \;\in\; \R^{p \times p}.
  \label{eq:spiked-sigma}
\end{equation}
Throughout this section, \(\vect{X}_1, \dots, \vect{X}_n\) are iid \(\Normal{\vect{0}, \Sigma}\) and \(\Sigmahat_n = (1/n)\sum_i \vect{X}_i \vect{X}_i\transpose\) is the sample covariance, with \(p, n \to \infty\), \(p/n \to y \in (0, 1)\), and \(m\) fixed.
\end{definition}

This is the simplest case of the \emph{generalised spiked population model} of \citet[][\S~11.2]{YaoZhengBai2015}, in which the population and sample covariances inherit the block structure
\[
  \Sigma \;=\; \begin{pmatrix} \Lambda & 0 \\ 0 & \mat{V}_p \end{pmatrix}, \qquad
  \Sigmahat_n \;=\; \begin{pmatrix} S_{11} & S_{12} \\ S_{21} & S_{22} \end{pmatrix}.
\]
The fixed-size block \(\Lambda = \diag(\alpha_1, \dots, \alpha_m)\) carries the spikes; the \((p - m) \times (p - m)\) baseline block \(\mat{V}_p\) has empirical spectral distribution converging to a limit \(H\), and an eigenvalue of \(\Lambda\) is a \emph{spike} when it lies outside the support of \(H\) \citep[][Def.~11.2]{YaoZhengBai2015}. The sample blocks \(S_{ij}\) arise from the partition \(\vect{X}_i = \Sigma^{1/2} \vect{Y}_i\), \(\vect{Y}_i \sim \Normal{\vect{0}, I_p}\) \citep[][Eqs.~(11.6)--(11.8)]{YaoZhengBai2015}. Johnstone's model (\S~11.2.1) is the specialisation \(\mat{V}_p = I_{p - m}\), so \(H = \delta_1\) and ``spike'' reduces to \(\alpha_j \ne 1\). Spikes split into \emph{large} (\(\alpha_j > 1\)) and \emph{small} (\(\alpha_j < 1\)); we focus on the large case throughout.

The structural question is whether a large spike produces an outlier in the sample spectrum. Figure~\ref{fig:spiked-spectrum} shows the answer empirically: at \(y = 1/2\) and \(n = 1000\), a spike \(\alpha = 2.5\) lifts \(\lambda_{\max}(\Sigmahat_n)\) to \(\approx 3.42\), well above the MP edge \(\lambda_+ \approx 2.91\); a spike \(\alpha = 1.4\) leaves \(\lambda_{\max}\) at \(\approx 2.90\), indistinguishable from the edge. Both spikes exceed \(1\), yet only one is detectable. The dividing threshold depends on \(y\) in a precise way.

The threshold is encoded in a single algebraic object: the value at which a separated sample eigenvalue would have to sit so that the perturbed bulk still matches the MP shape. The derivation passes through the Stieltjes transform and is the \(H = \delta_1\) specialisation of \citet[][Eq.~(11.15)]{YaoZhengBai2015}. We take it as given.

\begin{definition}[Spike-location map]\label{def:psi}
For \(\alpha > 1\) and aspect ratio \(y \in (0, 1)\),
\begin{equation}
  \Psi(\alpha) \;=\; \alpha \;+\; \frac{y\, \alpha}{\alpha - 1}.
  \label{eq:psi}
\end{equation}
\end{definition}

The whole phase transition is encoded in the shape of \(\Psi\) alone.

\begin{proposition}[Properties of the spike-location map]\label{prop:psi}
For each fixed \(y \in (0, 1)\), the map \(\Psi: (1, \infty) \to \R\) defined by~\eqref{eq:psi} satisfies:
\begin{enumerate}[label=\textnormal{(\arabic*)},leftmargin=*]
  \item \(\displaystyle \Psi'(\alpha) \;=\; 1 \;-\; \frac{y}{(\alpha - 1)^2}\).
  \item \(\Psi\) has a unique minimum on \((1, \infty)\) at \(\alpha = 1 + \sqrt{y}\). On \((1, 1 + \sqrt{y})\) the map is strictly decreasing; on \((1 + \sqrt{y}, \infty)\) it is strictly increasing.
  \item The minimum value is exactly the Marchenko-Pastur upper edge:
  \[
    \Psi(1 + \sqrt{y}) \;=\; (1 + \sqrt{y})^2 \;=\; \lambda_+.
  \]
\end{enumerate}
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
For (1), differentiate:
\[
  \Psi'(\alpha) \;=\; 1 \;+\; y \cdot \frac{(\alpha - 1) - \alpha}{(\alpha - 1)^2} \;=\; 1 \;-\; \frac{y}{(\alpha - 1)^2}.
\]
For (2), \(\Psi'(\alpha) = 0\) gives \((\alpha - 1)^2 = y\), so \(\alpha = 1 \pm \sqrt{y}\); on \((1, \infty)\) the relevant root is \(\alpha = 1 + \sqrt{y}\). For \(1 < \alpha < 1 + \sqrt{y}\), \((\alpha - 1)^2 < y\) and \(\Psi' < 0\); for \(\alpha > 1 + \sqrt{y}\), \(\Psi' > 0\). For (3), substituting \(\alpha = 1 + \sqrt{y}\) into~\eqref{eq:psi} and using \(y/\sqrt{y} = \sqrt{y}\),
\[
  \Psi(1 + \sqrt{y}) \;=\; (1 + \sqrt{y}) \;+\; \sqrt{y}\,(1 + \sqrt{y}) \;=\; (1 + \sqrt{y})^2 \;=\; \lambda_+.
\]
\end{proof}

Following \citet[][Thm.~11.3]{YaoZhengBai2015}, we call a large spike \vocab{fundamental} if \(\Psi'(\alpha) > 0\) (equivalently \(\alpha > 1 + \sqrt{y}\)) and \emph{non-fundamental} otherwise. The two regimes correspond exactly to the two panels of Figure~\ref{fig:spiked-spectrum}: the fundamental spike \(\alpha = 2.5\) lifts \(\lambda_{\max}\) to \(\Psi(2.5) \approx 3.33\); the non-fundamental spike \(\alpha = 1.4\) lands at \(\lambda_+\). Section~\ref{subsec:bbp} makes the convergence precise.

\subsection{The Spike Phase Transition for Eigenvalues}\label{subsec:bbp}

With \(\Psi\) in hand the phase transition has a clean statement.

\begin{theorem}[Spike phase transition, large case]\label{thm:bbp}
Under the assumptions of Definition~\ref{def:spiked-model}, fix a large population spike \(\alpha_j > 1\) of multiplicity \(m_j\), and let \(J_j\) index the \(m_j\) sample eigenvalues associated with it. As \(p, n \to \infty\) with \(p/n \to y \in (0, 1)\):
\begin{enumerate}[label=\textnormal{(\arabic*)},leftmargin=*]
  \item if \(\alpha_j > 1 + \sqrt{y}\) (fundamental),
  \[
    \lambda_i(\Sigmahat_n) \;\convas\; \Psi(\alpha_j) \;=\; \alpha_j \;+\; \frac{y \alpha_j}{\alpha_j - 1}, \qquad i \in J_j;
  \]
  \item if \(1 < \alpha_j \le 1 + \sqrt{y}\) (non-fundamental),
  \[
    \lambda_i(\Sigmahat_n) \;\convas\; \lambda_+ \;=\; (1 + \sqrt{y})^2, \qquad i \in J_j.
  \]
\end{enumerate}
\end{theorem}

This is the Johnstone specialisation of \citet[][Cor.~11.4]{YaoZhengBai2015}, itself a consequence of the general phase-transition theorem \citep[][Thm.~11.3]{YaoZhengBai2015}; the original results for sample covariance matrices are due to \citet{BaikBenArousPeche2005, BaikSilverstein2006}. The proof passes through the Stieltjes transform of the unspiked bulk: an outlier sample eigenvalue is the unique solution \(\lambda\) of \(s_F(\lambda) = -1/\alpha_j\) outside the support of \(F_y\), and substituting the explicit Stieltjes transform of the Marchenko-Pastur law recovers \(\lambda = \Psi(\alpha_j)\). When \(\Psi'(\alpha_j) > 0\) (i.e.\ \(\alpha_j > 1 + \sqrt{y}\), by Proposition~\ref{prop:psi}) such a \(\lambda\) exists outside the bulk and case~(1) applies; otherwise the equation has no outside-the-bulk solution and the associated sample eigenvalue snaps to the upper edge \(\lambda_+\), giving case~(2). We use Theorem~\ref{thm:bbp} as a black box.

\begin{example}[Numerical predictions at \(y = 1/2\)]\label{ex:bbp-numbers}
With \(y = 1/2\), the threshold is \(1 + \sqrt{1/2} \approx 1.71\) and the upper edge is \(\lambda_+ \approx 2.91\). For \(\alpha = 2.5\) (fundamental),
\[
  \Psi(2.5) \;=\; 2.5 + \frac{(0.5)(2.5)}{2.5 - 1} \;=\; 2.5 + \tfrac{5}{6} \;\approx\; 3.33.
\]
For \(\alpha = 1.4 < 1.71\) (non-fundamental), Theorem~\ref{thm:bbp}(2) gives the limit \(\lambda_+ \approx 2.91\) directly, regardless of the precise value of \(\alpha\) as long as it stays below the threshold. The two panels of Figure~\ref{fig:spiked-spectrum} already showed the top sample eigenvalue near these limits at a single \((n, p)\); Figure~\ref{fig:spike-phase-transition} now traces the convergence as \(n\) grows.
\end{example}

Both curves track Theorem~\ref{thm:bbp} cleanly, and the convergence becomes visible by \(n = 800\); the residual width of the bands shrinks at the slow \(O(n^{-1/2})\) rate that Section~\ref{subsec:fluctuations} will quantify. When the population covariance carries more than one large spike the theorem applies to each \(\alpha_j\) separately, with the associated sample eigenvalues converging independently to either \(\Psi(\alpha_j)\) or \(\lambda_+\) according to the fundamental/non-fundamental dichotomy; a simulation with one strong and one weak spike, for instance, produces a top sample eigenvalue near \(\Psi(\alpha_1)\) and a second-largest eigenvalue near \(\lambda_+\). Theorem~\ref{thm:bbp} is, however, only an almost-sure statement; it does not say how \(\lambda_{\max}\) fluctuates around its limit. Section~\ref{subsec:fluctuations} addresses that.

\subsection{Fluctuations of Spiked Eigenvalues}\label{subsec:fluctuations}

Theorem~\ref{thm:bbp} pins down the almost-sure limits but says nothing about how \(\lambda_{\max}\) fluctuates around them. The fluctuation laws differ qualitatively across the two regimes: in the fundamental case they are Gaussian on the \(\sqrt{n}\) scale, in the non-fundamental case they are Tracy-Widom on the \(p^{-2/3}\) scale.

\begin{theorem}[Gaussian fluctuations for a simple fundamental spike]\label{thm:fund-fluct}
Under the assumptions of Definition~\ref{def:spiked-model}, let \(\alpha > 1 + \sqrt{y}\) be a fundamental spike of multiplicity \(m_j = 1\), and let \(\lambda_i(\Sigmahat_n)\) be the associated sample eigenvalue. Then
\begin{equation}
  \sqrt{n}\,\bigl(\lambda_i(\Sigmahat_n) - \Psi(\alpha)\bigr) \;\convd\; \Normal{0,\, \sigma_\alpha^2}, \qquad
  \sigma_\alpha^2 \;=\; 2\, \alpha^2\, \Psi'(\alpha) \;=\; \frac{2 \alpha^2\bigl[(\alpha - 1)^2 - y\bigr]}{(\alpha - 1)^2}.
  \label{eq:fund-fluct}
\end{equation}
\end{theorem}

This is the Johnstone simple-spike specialisation of \citet[][Thm.~11.11 and Eq.~(11.36)]{YaoZhengBai2015}; the explicit asymptotic variance \(\sigma_\alpha^2\) uses the fact that we are in the real Gaussian case (the fourth-cumulant correction \(\beta_y = \E\abs{y_{11}}^4 - 3\) vanishes). \citet[][Thm.~1.6]{FeralPeche2009} give a clean direct proof for diagonal spiked Gaussian models. The heuristic is the one already familiar from Section~\ref{subsec:scm-lowdim}: a separated, simple sample eigenvalue is a smooth functional of \(\Sigmahat_n\), and a delta-method argument lifts the \(\sqrt{n}\)-CLT for \(\Sigmahat_n\) entrywise to a \(\sqrt{n}\)-Gaussian limit for the eigenvalue. The Gaussian limit fails when the spike has multiplicity \(m_j \ge 2\) (the limiting joint distribution of the corresponding eigenvalues is then a finite-dimensional GOE-type object \citep[][Eq.~(11.35)]{YaoZhengBai2015}) and is conjectured to be Tracy-Widom in the non-fundamental case below.

\begin{theorem}[Edge fluctuations for a non-fundamental spike]\label{thm:nonfund-fluct}
Under the assumptions of Definition~\ref{def:spiked-model}, let \(1 < \alpha < 1 + \sqrt{y}\) be a strictly non-fundamental spike. With the Johnstone constants \(\mu_{n p}\) and \(\sigma_{n p}\) of Theorem~\ref{thm:tw},
\begin{equation}
  \frac{\lambda_{\max}(\Sigmahat_n) - \mu_{n p}}{\sigma_{n p}} \;\convd\; \mathrm{TW}_1.
\end{equation}
\end{theorem}

The result is implicit in the spectral universality at the edge proved by \citet{BaikSilverstein2006} and is the natural analogue of the null Johnstone edge fluctuation \citep{Johnstone2001}: the spike is subcritical, the corresponding sample eigenvalue is attached to the bulk's right edge, and the Tracy-Widom law that governs the null edge governs this near-critical edge as well. The critical case \(\alpha = 1 + \sqrt{y}\) is genuinely different — the limit is neither Gaussian nor TW\(_1\) but an interpolating distribution due to \citet{BaikBenArousPeche2005} — and we do not treat it here.

\begin{example}[Numerical predictions at \(y = 1/2\)]\label{ex:fluct-numbers}
At \(y = 1/2\), \(\Psi'(2.5) = 1 - 0.5/(1.5)^2 \approx 0.778\), so Theorem~\ref{thm:fund-fluct} predicts
\[
  \sigma_\alpha^2 \;=\; 2 \cdot (2.5)^2 \cdot 0.778 \;\approx\; 9.72,
\]
i.e.\ \(\lambda_{\max} - \Psi(2.5) \approx 3.33\) should look like \(\Normal{0,\, 9.72/n}\). For the non-fundamental spike \(\alpha = 1.4\), Theorem~\ref{thm:nonfund-fluct} predicts \(\mathrm{TW}_1\) shape after Johnstone standardisation; the constants \(\mu_{n p}, \sigma_{n p}\) at \(n = 1500\), \(p = 750\) are recorded in Theorem~\ref{thm:tw}.
\end{example}

The four panels of Figure~\ref{fig:spike-fluctuations} confirm the two regimes at \(n = 1500\). For the fundamental spike, the standardised samples have empirical mean \(\approx -0.04\) and standard deviation \(\approx 1.01\), and the Shapiro-Wilk statistic \(W = 0.998\) is close to its perfect-normal value of \(1\); the very small \(p = 0.026\) reflects the sensitivity of the test at \(R = 2000\) trials rather than meaningful non-normality. For the non-fundamental spike, the Kolmogorov-Smirnov test against \(\Normal{0, 1}\) returns \(D = 0.377\) with \(p < 10^{-250}\): the Johnstone-standardised statistic is decisively non-Gaussian, and its empirical mean \(\approx -1.02\) and standard deviation \(\approx 1.27\) are close to the theoretical \(\mathrm{TW}_1\) values \(\approx -1.21\) and \(\approx 1.27\). The Gaussian-versus-TW\(_1\) split therefore mirrors exactly the qualitative dichotomy of Theorem~\ref{thm:bbp}: separation from the bulk gives smooth Gaussian fluctuations on the \(\sqrt{n}\) scale, while absorption into the edge gives the TW\(_1\) signature on the \(p^{-2/3}\) scale.

Theorem~\ref{thm:fund-fluct} also has its own phase transition, distinct from the location bifurcation of Theorem~\ref{thm:bbp}: the variance \(\sigma_\alpha^2 = 2 \alpha^2 \Psi'(\alpha)\) is positive and grows for \(\alpha > 1 + \sqrt{y}\), but \emph{vanishes} at \(\alpha = 1 + \sqrt{y}\) (because \(\Psi'(1 + \sqrt{y}) = 0\), Proposition~\ref{prop:psi}). Just above the threshold the Gaussian limit therefore degenerates, and just below it the relevant scale is no longer \(\sqrt{n}\) at all. Figure~\ref{fig:spike-fluctuation-phase-transition} traces this transition empirically.

The empirical band hugs the predicted Gaussian envelope on the fundamental side, with the spread growing from zero at the threshold to roughly \(\pm 1.28\, \sigma_{2.5} \approx \pm 4\) at \(\alpha = 2.5\), and collapses to a tight band near zero on the non-fundamental side. The collapse below the threshold is not a failure of theory: on the \(\sqrt{n}\) scale the edge fluctuations are of order \(\sqrt{n} \cdot p^{-2/3} = O(n^{-1/6})\), invisible against the Gaussian scale above the threshold. Theorem~\ref{thm:fund-fluct} and Theorem~\ref{thm:nonfund-fluct} thus together carve out a two-axis phase transition: above the threshold, location lifts off from \(\lambda_+\) along \(\Psi(\alpha)\) and the fluctuation amplitude opens up along \(\sigma_\alpha\); below it, both quantities flatten at the bulk-edge regime.

Theorem~\ref{thm:bbp} and Theorem~\ref{thm:fund-fluct} together tell us \emph{where} a detectable spike lives in the sample spectrum and \emph{how} it fluctuates there, but they say nothing about whether the sample \emph{eigenvector} carries information about the population spike direction. The next subsection takes up that question.

\section{Spiked Covariance Matrices and the Spike Phase Transition}\label{sec:spiked}

The Marchenko-Pastur and Tracy-Widom results of Section~\ref{subsec:scm-highdim} describe the sample spectrum under the null hypothesis \(\Sigma = I_p\). Real datasets \GD{often carry structure: a small number of distinguished directions may account for most of the variance. This is the case, for example,} in regulatory pathways in single-cell genomics, market factors in finance, edge orientations in image processing. The simplest model that captures this picture, introduced by \citet{Johnstone2001} and developed systematically by \citet[][Ch.~11]{YaoZhengBai2015}, allows a small number of population eigenvalues to exceed the noise baseline of \(1\). The questions this section asks are when such a \emph{spike} produces a detectable outlier in the sample spectrum (Section~\ref{subsec:johnstone}--\ref{subsec:bbp}), what the corresponding fluctuations look like (Section~\ref{subsec:fluctuations}), and what the sample eigenvector reveals about the population spike direction (Section~\ref{subsec:eigenvector}). The answer is a clean phase transition with an explicit threshold determined by the aspect ratio \(y\) \citep{BaikBenArousPeche2005, BaikSilverstein2006}.

\subsection{Johnstone's Spiked Model and the Spike-Location Map}\label{subsec:johnstone}

We fix the model first. \GD{Throughout this section, \(\vect{X}_1, \dots, \vect{X}_n\) are iid \(\Normal{\vect{0}, \Sigma}\) and \(\Sigmahat_n = (1/n)\sum_i \vect{X}_i \vect{X}_i\transpose\) is the sample covariance, with \(p, n \to \infty\), \(p/n \to y \in (0, 1)\), and \(m\) fixed.}


\begin{definition}[Johnstone's spiked covariance model]\label{def:spiked-model}
Fix an integer \(m \ge 0\) (the number of \vocab{spikes}) and real numbers
\[
  \alpha_1 \;\ge\; \alpha_2 \;\ge\; \cdots \;\ge\; \alpha_m \;>\; 0,\qquad \alpha_j \ne 1.
\]
The \vocab{Johnstone spiked covariance} of dimension \(p \ge m + 1\) is given by 
\begin{equation}
  \Sigma \;=\; \diag(\alpha_1, \dots, \alpha_m, \underbrace{1, \dots, 1}_{p - m \text{ times}}) \;\in\; \R^{p \times p}.
  \label{eq:spiked-sigma}
\end{equation}
\end{definition}

\GD{The model \eqref{eq:spiked-sigma}} is the simplest case of the \emph{generalised spiked population model} of \citet[][\S~11.2]{YaoZhengBai2015}, in which the population and sample covariances inherit the block structure
\begin{equation}\label{eq:generalized_spiked}
  \Sigma \;=\; \begin{pmatrix} \Lambda & 0 \\ 0 & \mat{V}_p \end{pmatrix}, \qquad
  \Sigmahat_n \;=\; \begin{pmatrix} S_{11} & S_{12} \\ S_{21} & S_{22} \end{pmatrix}.
\end{equation}
\GD{In \eqref{eq:generalized_spiked}, the fixed-size block \(\Lambda = \diag(\alpha_1, \dots, \alpha_m)\) carries the spikes. In turn,} the \((p - m) \times (p - m)\) baseline block \(\mat{V}_p\) has empirical spectral distribution converging to a limit \(H\). \GD{We say} an eigenvalue of \(\Lambda\) is a \emph{spike} when it lies outside the support of \(H\) \citep[][Def.~11.2]{YaoZhengBai2015}. The sample blocks \(S_{ij}\) arise from the partition \(\vect{X}_i = \Sigma^{1/2} \vect{Y}_i\), \(\vect{Y}_i \sim \Normal{\vect{0}, I_p}\) \citep[][Eqs.~(11.6)--(11.8)]{YaoZhengBai2015}. 

\GD{\begin{example}
Johnstone's model (\S~11.2.1) is the specialisation \(\mat{V}_p = I_{p - m}\). In this case, \(H = \delta_1\) and the ``spike'' reduces to \(\alpha_j \ne 1\). Spikes split into \emph{large} (\(\alpha_j > 1\)) and \emph{small} (\(\alpha_j < 1\))
\end{example}}
%; we focus on the large case throughout.

The structural question is whether a large spike produces an outlier in the sample spectrum. Figure~\ref{fig:spiked-spectrum} shows the answer empirically. \GD{Namely,} at \(y = 1/2\) and \(n = 1000\), a spike \(\alpha = 2.5\) lifts \(\lambda_{\max}(\Sigmahat_n)\) to \(\approx 3.42\), \GD{which lies} well above the MP edge \(\lambda_+ \approx 2.91\). \GD{On the other hand,} a spike \(\alpha = 1.4\) leaves \(\lambda_{\max}\) at \(\approx 2.90\), indistinguishable from the edge. \GD{In summary,} both spikes exceed \(1\), yet only one is detectable. \GDcomment{``The dividing threshold depends on \(y\) in a precise way." This is only explained below. I would delete this sentence.}

The threshold is encoded in a single algebraic object: the value at which a separated sample eigenvalue would have to sit so that the perturbed bulk still matches the MP shape. The derivation passes through the Stieltjes transform and is the \(H = \delta_1\) specialisation of \citet[][Eq.~(11.15)]{YaoZhengBai2015}. We take it as given.

\begin{definition}[Spike-location map]\label{def:psi}
\GDcomment{It seems like we are focusing on Johnstone's model. That's fine, but that was not clear from the narrative.} For \(\alpha > 1\) and aspect ratio \(y \in (0, 1)\),
\begin{equation}
  \Psi(\alpha) \;=\; \alpha \;+\; \frac{y\, \alpha}{\alpha - 1}.
  \label{eq:psi}
\end{equation}
\end{definition}

The whole phase transition is encoded in the shape of \(\Psi\) alone.

\begin{proposition}[Properties of the spike-location map]\label{prop:psi}
For each fixed \(y \in (0, 1)\), the map \(\Psi: (1, \infty) \to \R\) defined by~\eqref{eq:psi} satisfies the following properties.
\begin{enumerate}[label=\textnormal{(\arabic*)},leftmargin=*]
  \item \(\displaystyle \Psi'(\alpha) \;=\; 1 \;-\; \frac{y}{(\alpha - 1)^2}\).
  \item \(\Psi\) has a unique minimum on \((1, \infty)\) at \(\alpha = 1 + \sqrt{y}\). On \((1, 1 + \sqrt{y})\) the map is strictly decreasing; on \((1 + \sqrt{y}, \infty)\) it is strictly increasing.
  \item The minimum value is exactly the Marchenko-Pastur upper edge:
  \[
    \Psi(1 + \sqrt{y}) \;=\; (1 + \sqrt{y})^2 \;=\; \lambda_+.
  \]
\end{enumerate}
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
For (1), differentiate:
\[
  \Psi'(\alpha) \;=\; 1 \;+\; y \cdot \frac{(\alpha - 1) - \alpha}{(\alpha - 1)^2} \;=\; 1 \;-\; \frac{y}{(\alpha - 1)^2}.
\]
For (2), \(\Psi'(\alpha) = 0\) gives \((\alpha - 1)^2 = y\), so \(\alpha = 1 \pm \sqrt{y}\); on \((1, \infty)\) the relevant root is \(\alpha = 1 + \sqrt{y}\). For \(1 < \alpha < 1 + \sqrt{y}\), \((\alpha - 1)^2 < y\) and \(\Psi' < 0\); for \(\alpha > 1 + \sqrt{y}\), \(\Psi' > 0\). For (3), substituting \(\alpha = 1 + \sqrt{y}\) into~\eqref{eq:psi} and using \(y/\sqrt{y} = \sqrt{y}\),
\[
  \Psi(1 + \sqrt{y}) \;=\; (1 + \sqrt{y}) \;+\; \sqrt{y}\,(1 + \sqrt{y}) \;=\; (1 + \sqrt{y})^2 \;=\; \lambda_+.
\]
\end{proof}

Following \citet[][Thm.~11.3]{YaoZhengBai2015}, we call a large spike \vocab{fundamental} if \(\Psi'(\alpha) > 0\) (equivalently \(\alpha > 1 + \sqrt{y}\)) and \emph{non-fundamental} otherwise. The two regimes correspond exactly to the two panels of Figure~\ref{fig:spiked-spectrum}: the fundamental spike \(\alpha = 2.5\) lifts \(\lambda_{\max}\) to \(\Psi(2.5) \approx 3.33\); the non-fundamental spike \(\alpha = 1.4\) lands at \(\lambda_+\). Section~\ref{subsec:bbp} makes the convergence precise.

\subsection{The Spike Phase Transition for Eigenvalues}\label{subsec:bbp}

With \(\Psi\) in hand, the phase transition has a clean statement -- we provide it next.

\begin{theorem}[Spike phase transition, large case]\label{thm:bbp}
Under the assumptions of Definition~\ref{def:spiked-model}, fix a large population spike \(\alpha_j > 1\) of multiplicity \(m_j\), and let \(J_j\) index the \(m_j\) sample eigenvalues associated with it. As \(p, n \to \infty\) with \(p/n \to y \in (0, 1)\):
\begin{enumerate}[label=\textnormal{(\arabic*)},leftmargin=*]
  \item if \(\alpha_j > 1 + \sqrt{y}\) (fundamental),
  \[
    \lambda_i(\Sigmahat_n) \;\convas\; \Psi(\alpha_j) \;=\; \alpha_j \;+\; \frac{y \alpha_j}{\alpha_j - 1}, \qquad i \in J_j;
  \]
  \item if \(1 < \alpha_j \le 1 + \sqrt{y}\) (non-fundamental),
  \[
    \lambda_i(\Sigmahat_n) \;\convas\; \lambda_+ \;=\; (1 + \sqrt{y})^2, \qquad i \in J_j.
  \]
\end{enumerate}
\end{theorem}

\noindent{\sc Sketch of the proof}: \GD{The argument for proving Theorem \ref{thm:bbp} passes through the Stieltjes transform of the unspiked bulk. An outlier sample eigenvalue is the unique solution \(\lambda\) of \(s_F(\lambda) = -1/\alpha_j\) outside the support of \(F_y\). Substituting the explicit Stieltjes transform of the Marchenko-Pastur law recovers \(\lambda = \Psi(\alpha_j)\). When \(\Psi'(\alpha_j) > 0\) (i.e.\ \(\alpha_j > 1 + \sqrt{y}\), by Proposition~\ref{prop:psi}) such a \(\lambda\) exists outside the bulk and case~(1) applies. Otherwise, the equation has no outside-the-bulk solution and the associated sample eigenvalue snaps to the upper edge \(\lambda_+\), giving case~(2). $\Box$}



\GD{\begin{remark} The original results for sample covariance matrices are due to \citet{BaikBenArousPeche2005, BaikSilverstein2006}.
Theorem \ref{thm:bbp} is the Johnstone specialisation of \citet[][Cor.~11.4]{YaoZhengBai2015}, itself a consequence of the general phase-transition theorem \citep[][Thm.~11.3]{YaoZhengBai2015}.  
\end{remark}}


%We use Theorem~\ref{thm:bbp} as a black box.

\begin{example}[Numerical predictions at \(y = 1/2\)]\label{ex:bbp-numbers}
With \(y = 1/2\), the threshold is \(1 + \sqrt{1/2} \approx 1.71\) and the upper edge is \(\lambda_+ \approx 2.91\). For \(\alpha = 2.5\) (fundamental),
\[
  \Psi(2.5) \;=\; 2.5 + \frac{(0.5)(2.5)}{2.5 - 1} \;=\; 2.5 + \tfrac{5}{6} \;\approx\; 3.33.
\]
For \(\alpha = 1.4 < 1.71\) (non-fundamental), Theorem~\ref{thm:bbp}(2) gives the limit \(\lambda_+ \approx 2.91\) directly, regardless of the precise value of \(\alpha\) as long as it stays below the threshold. The two panels of Figure~\ref{fig:spiked-spectrum} already showed the top sample eigenvalue near these limits at a single \((n, p)\); Figure~\ref{fig:spike-phase-transition} now traces the convergence as \(n\) grows.
\end{example}

When the population covariance carries more than one large spike the theorem applies to each \(\alpha_j\) separately, with the associated sample eigenvalues converging independently to either \(\Psi(\alpha_j)\) or \(\lambda_+\) according to the fundamental/non-fundamental dichotomy; a simulation with one strong and one weak spike, for instance, produces a top sample eigenvalue near \(\Psi(\alpha_1)\) and a second-largest eigenvalue near \(\lambda_+\). Theorem~\ref{thm:bbp} is, however, only an almost-sure statement; it does not say how \(\lambda_{\max}\) fluctuates around its limit. Section~\ref{subsec:fluctuations} addresses that.

\subsection{Fluctuations of Spiked Eigenvalues}\label{subsec:fluctuations}

Theorem~\ref{thm:bbp} pins down the almost-sure limits but says nothing about how the corresponding sample eigenvalue fluctuates around them. The two regimes turn out to inherit qualitatively different limit laws and qualitatively different scales: on the fundamental side, the fluctuations are Gaussian on the \(\sqrt{n}\) scale; on the non-fundamental side, they are Tracy-Widom on the much finer edge scale of Section~\ref{subsec:scm-highdim}.

\begin{theorem}[Gaussian fluctuations for a simple fundamental spike]\label{thm:fund-fluct}
Under the assumptions of Definition~\ref{def:spiked-model}, let \(\alpha > 1 + \sqrt{y}\) be a fundamental spike of multiplicity \(m_j = 1\), and let \(\lambda_i(\Sigmahat_n)\) be the associated sample eigenvalue. Then
\begin{equation}
  \sqrt{n}\,\bigl(\lambda_i(\Sigmahat_n) - \Psi(\alpha)\bigr) \;\convd\; \Normal{0,\, \sigma_\alpha^2}, \qquad
  \sigma_\alpha^2 \;=\; 2\, \alpha^2\, \Psi'(\alpha) \;=\; \frac{2 \alpha^2\bigl[(\alpha - 1)^2 - y\bigr]}{(\alpha - 1)^2}.
  \label{eq:fund-fluct}
\end{equation}
\end{theorem}

This is the Johnstone simple-spike specialisation of \citet[][Thm.~11.11 and Eq.~(11.36)]{YaoZhengBai2015}; the explicit variance \(\sigma_\alpha^2\) uses the fact that we are in the real Gaussian case, so the fourth-cumulant correction \(\beta_y = \E\abs{y_{11}}^4 - 3\) appearing in the general statement of Theorem~11.11 vanishes. \citet[][Thm.~1.6]{FeralPeche2009} give a clean direct proof for diagonal spiked Gaussian models. The heuristic mirrors Section~\ref{subsec:scm-lowdim}: a separated simple sample eigenvalue is a smooth functional of \(\Sigmahat_n\), and a delta-method argument lifts the entrywise \(\sqrt{n}\)-CLT for \(\Sigmahat_n\) to a \(\sqrt{n}\)-Gaussian limit for the eigenvalue. The Gaussian limit fails once the spike has multiplicity \(m_j \ge 2\), in which case the limiting joint distribution of the corresponding sample eigenvalues is a finite-dimensional GOE-type object \citep[][Eq.~(11.35)]{YaoZhengBai2015}; we do not treat that case.

\begin{theorem}[Edge fluctuations for a non-fundamental spike]\label{thm:nonfund-fluct}
Under the assumptions of Definition~\ref{def:spiked-model}, let \(1 < \alpha < 1 + \sqrt{y}\) be a strictly non-fundamental spike. With the Johnstone constants \(\mu_{n p}\) and \(\sigma_{n p}\) of Theorem~\ref{thm:tw},
\begin{equation}
  \frac{\lambda_{\max}(\Sigmahat_n) - \mu_{n p}}{\sigma_{n p}} \;\convd\; \mathrm{TW}_1.
\end{equation}
\end{theorem}

The result is implicit in the spectral universality at the edge proved by \citet{BaikSilverstein2006} and is the natural analogue of the null Johnstone edge fluctuation \citep{Johnstone2001}: a subcritical spike does not separate, the corresponding sample eigenvalue is pinned to the bulk's right edge, and the Tracy-Widom law that governs the null edge continues to govern this near-critical edge. The critical case \(\alpha = 1 + \sqrt{y}\) is genuinely different: the limit is neither Gaussian nor \(\mathrm{TW}_1\) but an interpolating crossover law studied by \citet{BaikBenArousPeche2005}, which we examine empirically at the end of this subsection.

\begin{example}[Numerical predictions at \(y = 1/2\)]\label{ex:fluct-numbers}
At \(y = 1/2\), \(\Psi'(2.5) = 1 - 0.5/(1.5)^2 \approx 0.778\), so Theorem~\ref{thm:fund-fluct} predicts
\[
  \sigma_\alpha^2 \;=\; 2 \cdot (2.5)^2 \cdot 0.778 \;\approx\; 9.72,
\]
i.e.\ \(\lambda_{\max} - \Psi(2.5)\) should look like \(\Normal{0,\, 9.72/n}\) at large \(n\). For the non-fundamental spike \(\alpha = 1.4\), Theorem~\ref{thm:nonfund-fluct} predicts the \(\mathrm{TW}_1\) shape after Johnstone standardisation; the centring and scaling constants \(\mu_{n p}, \sigma_{n p}\) are the same as in Theorem~\ref{thm:tw}. At \(n = 800\), \(p = 400\), \(\mu_{n p} \approx 2.91\), only slightly below the asymptotic edge \(\lambda_+ \approx 2.91\).
\end{example}

The visual contrast between the two regimes is already clear at modest \(n\). Figure~\ref{fig:top-eigenvalue-distributions} overlays the empirical distributions of \(\lambda_{\max}(\Sigmahat_n)\) at \(n = 800\), \(R = 1000\), under the two spike values of Example~\ref{ex:fluct-numbers}.

First, the two histograms are disjoint: the fundamental and non-fundamental cases place \(\lambda_{\max}(\Sigmahat_n)\) in qualitatively different regions of the spectrum. The four panels of Figure~\ref{fig:spike-fluctuations} confirm the predictions quantitatively. On the fundamental side, the empirically standardised statistic has mean \(\approx -0.04\) and standard deviation \(\approx 1.01\); the Shapiro-Wilk statistic is \(W = 0.998\) (its perfect-normal value is \(1\)) and a small \(p\)-value of \(0.026\) reflects the very large sample size \(R = 2000\) of the test rather than any meaningful departure from normality. On the non-fundamental side, the Johnstone-standardised statistic has mean \(\approx -1.02\) and standard deviation \(\approx 1.27\), close to the theoretical \(\mathrm{TW}_1\) values of \(-1.21\) and \(1.27\); a Kolmogorov-Smirnov test against \(\Normal{0, 1}\) returns \(D = 0.377\) with \(p < 10^{-250}\), confirming that the Johnstone-standardised statistic is decisively non-Gaussian and consistent instead with TW\(_1\). The two regimes therefore inherit not only different almost-sure limits, as in Theorem~\ref{thm:bbp}, but different fluctuation laws on different scales: smooth \(\sqrt{n}\)-Gaussian fluctuations from a separated outlier, versus the \(p^{-2/3}\)-Tracy-Widom signature of an edge eigenvalue.

\paragraph{The critical window.}
Theorems~\ref{thm:fund-fluct} and~\ref{thm:nonfund-fluct} cover two fixed cases, one on each side of the threshold \(\alpha_c = 1 + \sqrt{y}\). What happens right at the threshold is less clear-cut, and two facts about the map \(\Psi\) (Proposition~\ref{prop:psi}) explain why. First, the prediction curve meets the edge \emph{flatly}: \(\Psi(\alpha_c) = \lambda_+\) and \(\Psi'(\alpha_c) = 0\), so just above the threshold the outlier has barely pulled away from the edge. Second, the Gaussian spread \(\sigma_\alpha^2 = 2\alpha^2 \Psi'(\alpha)\) shrinks to zero as \(\alpha\) drops toward \(\alpha_c\). So at any finite \(n\) the change cannot be sudden; it is spread over a range of \(\alpha\) around \(\alpha_c\).

Figure~\ref{fig:critical-violin} shows the full distribution of \(\lambda_{\max}(\Sigmahat_n)\) at each spike value. Below the threshold the violins are small, sit on the edge line, and look nearly the same from one \(\alpha\) to the next: a subcritical spike does not separate, so its exact value barely matters, and the slight differences that show are magnified by the zoomed-in vertical axis, which spans only a thin range of eigenvalues. Above the threshold the violins climb along \(\Psi(\alpha)\), grow taller as the spread increases, and change from a lopsided edge shape into a symmetric, bell-shaped Gaussian. The change happens gradually, not as a jump.

So the transition is not the Tracy-Widom shape below the threshold slowly bending into the Gaussian shape above it. Instead, close to \(\alpha_c\) both sides look like a single in-between shape, and the two different behaviours appear only once the spike is well away from the threshold. That in-between shape is a single bump rather than two, and was studied by \citet{BaikBenArousPeche2005}; our simulations only illustrate it, and we do not work it out here.

The location result of Theorem~\ref{thm:bbp} and the fluctuation result of Theorem~\ref{thm:fund-fluct} together tell us \emph{where} a detectable spike lives in the sample spectrum and \emph{how} it fluctuates there; they say nothing yet about whether the corresponding sample \emph{eigenvector} carries information about the population spike direction. The next subsection takes up that question.

\subsection{Eigenvector Alignment Beyond the Threshold}\label{subsec:eigenvector}

Theorem~\ref{thm:bbp} tells us \emph{that} a fundamental spike is visible in the sample spectrum, and Theorem~\ref{thm:fund-fluct} how its eigenvalue fluctuates. Neither says whether the corresponding sample \emph{eigenvector} points anywhere near the true population spike direction. In the one-spike model that direction is \(\vect e_1 = (1, 0, \dots, 0)\transpose\), the axis carrying the inflated variance \(\alpha\). Writing \(\widehat{\vect u}_1\) for the unit eigenvector of the top sample eigenvalue, we measure how well it recovers \(\vect e_1\) by the \vocab{squared alignment}
\[
  \abs{\inner{\widehat{\vect u}_1}{\vect e_1}}^2 \;=\; \cos^2 \theta \;\in\; [0, 1],
\]
where \(\theta\) is the angle between the two directions; squaring removes the harmless sign ambiguity in \(\widehat{\vect u}_1\). A value near \(1\) means near-perfect recovery; a value near \(0\) means the sample eigenvector is essentially noise.

A simulation makes the question concrete before any theory. Figure~\ref{fig:eigenvector-histogram} histograms the squared alignment over many trials for a weak spike (\(\alpha = 1.4\), below the threshold) and a strong spike (\(\alpha = 2.5\), above it), at \(y = 1/2\) and \(n = 1200\). The two land in cleanly separated places: the weak-spike alignment piles up near \(0\), so the eigenvector is essentially noise, while the strong-spike alignment concentrates around \(0.58\), so the eigenvector recovers a definite fraction of the true direction. The rest of this subsection explains where these two clusters sit and why a threshold separates them.

The two distributions are disjoint: the weak case sits at \(0\), the strong case near its predicted limit \(\rho(2.5, 1/2) \approx 0.58\) (dashed lines). Generated by \texttt{notebooks/04\_section4\_demos.py}.}
  \label{fig:eigenvector-histogram}
\end{figure}

\citet{YaoZhengBai2015} explain this by splitting the sample eigenvector into the coordinates that carry signal and those that carry only noise.

\begin{theorem}[Signal-block length, {\citealp[Thm.~11.5]{YaoZhengBai2015}}]\label{thm:eigvec-general}
Let \(\alpha_j\) be a fundamental spike (\(\Psi'(\alpha_j) > 0\)), and let \(\widehat{\vect u}\) be a unit sample eigenvector whose eigenvalue converges to \(\Psi(\alpha_j)\). Split its coordinates as \(\widehat{\vect u} = (\widehat{\vect u}_{(1)}, \widehat{\vect u}_{(2)})\), with \(\widehat{\vect u}_{(1)}\) the \(m\) spike directions and \(\widehat{\vect u}_{(2)}\) the \(p - m\) baseline directions. Then \(\widehat{\vect u}_{(1)}\) converges almost surely to a population spike eigenvector for \(\alpha_j\) (the direction \(\vect e_1\) when the spike is simple), and the two blocks have squared lengths
\begin{equation}
  \norm{\widehat{\vect u}_{(1)}}^2 \;\convas\; d^2 \;=\; \frac{\alpha_j\, \Psi'(\alpha_j)}{\Psi(\alpha_j)}, \qquad \norm{\widehat{\vect u}_{(2)}}^2 \;\convas\; 1 - d^2.
  \label{eq:signal-block}
\end{equation}
\end{theorem}

In words: of the unit length of \(\widehat{\vect u}\), a fraction \(d^2\) lands on the true spike directions and the remaining \(1 - d^2\) leaks into noise. Specialising to Johnstone's model makes the fraction explicit.

\begin{corollary}[Johnstone model, {\citealp[Cor.~11.6]{YaoZhengBai2015}}]\label{cor:eigvec-johnstone}
For \(\Sigma = \diag(\alpha, 1, \dots, 1)\) and a fundamental spike \(\alpha > 1 + \sqrt{y}\),
\begin{equation}
  d^2 \;=\; \frac{(\alpha - 1)^2 - y}{(\alpha - 1)(\alpha - 1 + y)}.
  \label{eq:johnstone-d2}
\end{equation}
\end{corollary}

For a \emph{simple} spike (\(m = 1\)) the signal block is the single coordinate \(\vect e_1\), so \(\norm{\widehat{\vect u}_{(1)}}^2 = \abs{\inner{\widehat{\vect u}_1}{\vect e_1}}^2\) and~\eqref{eq:johnstone-d2} is a formula for the squared alignment itself. Calling it \(\rho(\alpha, y)\),
\begin{equation}
  \abs{\inner{\widehat{\vect u}_1}{\vect e_1}}^2 \;\convas\;
  \rho(\alpha, y) \;=\; \frac{1 - \dfrac{y}{(\alpha - 1)^2}}{1 + \dfrac{y}{\alpha - 1}}
  \quad (\alpha > 1 + \sqrt{y}), \qquad \text{and} \;\convas\; 0 \quad (1 < \alpha \le 1 + \sqrt{y}).
  \label{eq:rho-overlap}
\end{equation}

The two expressions~\eqref{eq:signal-block} and~\eqref{eq:rho-overlap} are the same quantity: with \(\Psi(\alpha) = \alpha\bigl(1 + y/(\alpha - 1)\bigr)\) and \(\Psi'(\alpha) = 1 - y/(\alpha - 1)^2\),
\[
  \frac{\alpha\, \Psi'(\alpha)}{\Psi(\alpha)} \;=\; \frac{1 - y/(\alpha - 1)^2}{1 + y/(\alpha - 1)} \;=\; \rho(\alpha, y).
\]
This ties the two phase transitions together exactly: the alignment carries the same factor \(\Psi'(\alpha)\) that sets the eigenvalue fluctuations in Theorem~\ref{thm:fund-fluct}, so the eigenvalue and the eigenvector become informative at the very same threshold. At \(\alpha = 1 + \sqrt{y}\) we have \(\Psi'(\alpha) = 0\) and the alignment is \(0\); as \(\alpha \to \infty\), \(\rho \to 1\) and the recovery becomes perfect.

Below the threshold \(\alpha_c \approx 1.71\) the top eigenvector is essentially noise (alignment \(\approx 0\)); above it the alignment turns on and climbs toward \(1\), tracking the prediction \(\rho(\alpha, y)\) of~\eqref{eq:rho-overlap}. Figure~\ref{fig:eigenvector-alignment} confirms~\eqref{eq:rho-overlap} at \(y = 1/2\): the simulated alignment stays at zero until the threshold and then rises along \(\rho(\alpha, y)\), reaching \(\rho(2.5, 1/2) \approx 0.58\) at \(\alpha = 2.5\) and approaching \(1\) for large spikes. Detecting a spike and recovering its direction thus share one threshold, but the eigenvector recovers the direction only partially unless the spike is well above it.

The mean is only part of the story. Figure~\ref{fig:eigenvector-violin} shows the whole distribution as the spike grows: it is squeezed against \(0\) below the threshold, broad and skewed upward just above it (the recovery is genuine but highly variable there), and tightens into a narrow band as \(\alpha\) grows and recovery becomes reliable. The points of Figure~\ref{fig:eigenvector-alignment} are the centres of these violins.

The aspect ratio \(y\) sets how far above is ``well above''. Because the threshold \(1 + \sqrt{y}\) grows with \(y\), a higher-dimensional problem (larger \(y = p/n\)) needs a stronger spike to reach the same alignment. Figure~\ref{fig:eigenvector-dimension} shows this directly.

  \centering

Reading off the target line in Figure~\ref{fig:eigenvector-dimension} answers the practical question: to hold the alignment at \(0.5\), a problem at \(y = 1/4\) needs only a modest spike, whereas at \(y = 1\) the required spike is substantially larger. In high dimensions a signal must be proportionally stronger to be not merely detectable but \emph{recoverable}.

Sections~\ref{subsec:johnstone}--\ref{subsec:eigenvector} now give a complete account of a single spike: when it is detectable (\(\alpha > 1 + \sqrt{y}\)), where its eigenvalue settles (\(\Psi(\alpha)\)), how that eigenvalue fluctuates (Gaussian, on the \(n^{-1/2}\) scale), and how well its eigenvector is recovered (\(\rho(\alpha, y)\)). The same signal-versus-noise threshold reappears, in a slightly different guise, when one moves from covariance matrices to the similarity matrices used for clustering, the subject of the final subsection.

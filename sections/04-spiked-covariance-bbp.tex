\section{Spiked Covariance Matrices and the Spike Phase Transition}\label{sec:spiked}

The Marchenko-Pastur and Tracy-Widom results of Section~\ref{subsec:scm-highdim} describe the sample spectrum under the null hypothesis \(\Sigma = I_p\). Real datasets often carry structure: a small number of distinguished directions may account for most of the variance (regulatory pathways in single-cell genomics, market factors in finance, edge orientations in image processing). The simplest model that captures this picture, introduced by \citet{Johnstone2001} and developed systematically by \citet[][Ch.~11]{YaoZhengBai2015}, allows a small number of population eigenvalues to exceed the noise baseline of \(1\). The questions this section asks are when such a \emph{spike} produces a detectable outlier in the sample spectrum (Sections~\ref{subsec:johnstone}--\ref{subsec:bbp}), what the corresponding fluctuations look like (Section~\ref{subsec:fluctuations}), what the sample eigenvector reveals about the population spike direction (Section~\ref{subsec:eigenvector}), and what changes when two spikes are present at once (Section~\ref{subsec:two-spikes}). The answer is a clean phase transition with an explicit threshold determined by the aspect ratio \(y\) \citep{BaikBenArousPeche2005, BaikSilverstein2006}. The section then closes with the payoff: spectral clustering is itself a spiked-model problem (Section~\ref{subsec:clustering}), and the theory developed here predicts its accuracy on real handwritten digits (Section~\ref{subsec:mnist}).

\subsection{Johnstone's Spiked Model and the Spike-Location Map}\label{subsec:johnstone}

We fix the model first. Throughout this section, \(\vect{X}_1, \dots, \vect{X}_n\) are iid \(\Normal{\vect{0}, \Sigma}\) and \(\Sigmahat_n = (1/n)\sum_i \vect{X}_i \vect{X}_i\transpose\) is the sample covariance, with \(p, n \to \infty\) and \(p/n \to y \in (0, 1)\); we take \(p < n\) for convenience, and the number of spikes (the integer \(m\) of the next definition) stays fixed as the dimension grows.


\begin{definition}[Johnstone's spiked covariance model]\label{def:spiked-model}
Fix an integer \(m \ge 0\) (the number of \vocab{spikes}) and real numbers
\[
  \alpha_1 \;\ge\; \alpha_2 \;\ge\; \cdots \;\ge\; \alpha_m \;>\; 0,\qquad \alpha_j \ne 1.
\]
The \vocab{Johnstone spiked covariance} of dimension \(p \ge m + 1\) is given by 
\begin{equation}
  \Sigma \;=\; \diag(\alpha_1, \dots, \alpha_m, \underbrace{1, \dots, 1}_{p - m \text{ times}}) \;\in\; \R^{p \times p}.
  \label{eq:spiked-sigma}
\end{equation}
\end{definition}

The model~\eqref{eq:spiked-sigma} is the simplest case of the \emph{generalized spiked population model} of \citet[][\S~11.2]{YaoZhengBai2015}, in which the population covariance carries the block structure
\begin{equation}\label{eq:generalized_spiked}
  \Sigma \;=\; \begin{pmatrix} \Lambda & 0 \\ 0 & \mat{V}_p \end{pmatrix}.
\end{equation}
In~\eqref{eq:generalized_spiked}, the fixed-size block \(\Lambda = \diag(\alpha_1, \dots, \alpha_m)\) carries the spikes, while the \((p - m) \times (p - m)\) baseline block \(\mat{V}_p\) has empirical spectral distribution converging to a limit \(H\). An eigenvalue of \(\Lambda\) is a \emph{spike} when it lies outside the support of \(H\) \citep[][Def.~11.2]{YaoZhengBai2015}.

\begin{example}[Johnstone's model]\label{ex:johnstone-special}
Johnstone's model \citep[\S~11.2.1]{YaoZhengBai2015} is the specialization \(\mat{V}_p = I_{p - m}\). Then \(H = \delta_1\), the point mass at \(1\) (every baseline eigenvalue equals \(1\)), and a ``spike'' reduces to \(\alpha_j \ne 1\); the spikes split into \vocab{large} \((\alpha_j > 1)\) and \emph{small} \((\alpha_j < 1)\) ones. We focus on the large case throughout; small spikes produce outliers below the bulk by a mirror-image argument that we do not pursue.\gdmark{johnstone-example}
\end{example}

The structural question is whether a large spike produces an outlier in the sample spectrum. Figure~\ref{fig:spiked-spectrum} shows the answer empirically: at \(y = 1/2\) and \(n = 1000\), a spike \(\alpha = 2.5\) lifts \(\lambda_{\max}(\Sigmahat_n)\) to \(\approx 3.42\), well above the MP edge \(\lambda_+ \approx 2.91\), while a spike \(\alpha = 1.4\) leaves \(\lambda_{\max}\) at \(\approx 2.90\), indistinguishable from the edge. Both spikes exceed \(1\), yet only one is detectable.\gdmark{delete-threshold}

From here on we work in Johnstone's model (Example~\ref{ex:johnstone-special}), so \(\mat{V}_p = I\) and \(H = \delta_1\). The threshold is then encoded in a single explicit function \(\Psi(\alpha)\), which predicts where the outlier produced by a spike of strength \(\alpha\) will sit. Its derivation passes through the \vocab{Stieltjes transform} of the limiting bulk, an integral transform of the limiting spectral distribution whose machinery we do not develop here, and is the \(H = \delta_1\) specialization of \citet[][Eq.~(11.15)]{YaoZhengBai2015}; we take it as given.\gdmark{johnstone-focus}

\begin{definition}[Spike-location map]\label{def:psi}
For \(\alpha > 1\) and aspect ratio \(y \in (0, 1)\),
\begin{equation}
  \Psi(\alpha) \;=\; \alpha \;+\; \frac{y\, \alpha}{\alpha - 1}.
  \label{eq:psi}
\end{equation}
\end{definition}

The whole phase transition is encoded in the shape of \(\Psi\) alone.

\begin{proposition}[Properties of the spike-location map]\label{prop:psi}
For each fixed \(y \in (0, 1)\), the map \(\Psi: (1, \infty) \to \R\) defined by~\eqref{eq:psi} satisfies the following properties.
\begin{enumerate}[label=\textnormal{(\arabic*)},leftmargin=*]
  \item \(\displaystyle \Psi'(\alpha) \;=\; 1 \;-\; \frac{y}{(\alpha - 1)^2}\).
  \item \(\Psi\) has a unique minimum on \((1, \infty)\) at \(\alpha = 1 + \sqrt{y}\). On \((1, 1 + \sqrt{y})\) the map is strictly decreasing; on \((1 + \sqrt{y}, \infty)\) it is strictly increasing.
  \item The minimum value is exactly the Marchenko-Pastur upper edge:
  \[
    \Psi(1 + \sqrt{y}) \;=\; (1 + \sqrt{y})^2 \;=\; \lambda_+.
  \]
\end{enumerate}
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
For (1), differentiate:
\[
  \Psi'(\alpha) \;=\; 1 \;+\; y \cdot \frac{(\alpha - 1) - \alpha}{(\alpha - 1)^2} \;=\; 1 \;-\; \frac{y}{(\alpha - 1)^2}.
\]
For (2), \(\Psi'(\alpha) = 0\) gives \((\alpha - 1)^2 = y\), so \(\alpha = 1 \pm \sqrt{y}\); on \((1, \infty)\) the relevant root is \(\alpha = 1 + \sqrt{y}\). For \(1 < \alpha < 1 + \sqrt{y}\), \((\alpha - 1)^2 < y\) and \(\Psi' < 0\); for \(\alpha > 1 + \sqrt{y}\), \(\Psi' > 0\). For (3), substituting \(\alpha = 1 + \sqrt{y}\) into~\eqref{eq:psi} and using \(y/\sqrt{y} = \sqrt{y}\),
\[
  \Psi(1 + \sqrt{y}) \;=\; (1 + \sqrt{y}) \;+\; \sqrt{y}\,(1 + \sqrt{y}) \;=\; (1 + \sqrt{y})^2 \;=\; \lambda_+.
\]
\end{proof}

Following \citet[][Thm.~11.3]{YaoZhengBai2015}, we call a large spike \vocab{fundamental} if \(\Psi'(\alpha) > 0\) (equivalently \(\alpha > 1 + \sqrt{y}\)) and \emph{non-fundamental} otherwise; fundamental spikes are also called \emph{supercritical} and non-fundamental ones \emph{subcritical}, and we use the names interchangeably. Figure~\ref{fig:spike-location-map} plots the map and marks the two branches.

Section~\ref{subsec:bbp} makes the convergence precise.

\subsection{The Spike Phase Transition for Eigenvalues}\label{subsec:bbp}

With \(\Psi\) in hand, the phase transition has a clean statement.

\begin{theorem}[Spike phase transition, large case]\label{thm:bbp}
In the setting of Section~\ref{subsec:johnstone}, fix a large population spike \(\alpha_j > 1\) of multiplicity \(m_j\), and let \(J_j\) index the \(m_j\) sample eigenvalues at the same ranks that \(\alpha_j\) occupies in the ordered population spectrum. As \(p, n \to \infty\) with \(p/n \to y \in (0, 1)\):
\begin{enumerate}[label=\textnormal{(\arabic*)},leftmargin=*]
  \item if \(\alpha_j > 1 + \sqrt{y}\) (fundamental),
  \[
    \lambda_i(\Sigmahat_n) \;\convas\; \Psi(\alpha_j) \;=\; \alpha_j \;+\; \frac{y \alpha_j}{\alpha_j - 1}, \qquad i \in J_j;
  \]
  \item if \(1 < \alpha_j \le 1 + \sqrt{y}\) (non-fundamental),
  \[
    \lambda_i(\Sigmahat_n) \;\convas\; \lambda_+ \;=\; (1 + \sqrt{y})^2, \qquad i \in J_j.
  \]
\end{enumerate}
\end{theorem}

\noindent{\sc Sketch of the proof.}\enspace The argument passes through the Stieltjes transform of the unspiked bulk, already met in the derivation of \(\Psi\). Each spike \(\alpha_j\) is paired with a candidate outlier location by an explicit equation in that transform, and solving the equation for the Marchenko-Pastur bulk gives exactly the candidate \(\lambda = \Psi(\alpha_j)\) \citep[][\S~11.2]{YaoZhengBai2015}. When \(\Psi'(\alpha_j) > 0\) (i.e.\ \(\alpha_j > 1 + \sqrt{y}\), by Proposition~\ref{prop:psi}) the candidate lies outside the bulk and is realized, giving case~(1). Otherwise no candidate exists outside the bulk and the associated sample eigenvalue snaps to the upper edge \(\lambda_+\), giving case~(2). \(\Box\)\gdmark{proof-sketch}

\begin{remark}
The original results for sample covariance matrices are due to \citet{BaikBenArousPeche2005, BaikSilverstein2006}. Theorem~\ref{thm:bbp} is the Johnstone specialization of \citet[][Cor.~11.4]{YaoZhengBai2015}, itself a consequence of the general phase-transition theorem \citep[][Thm.~11.3]{YaoZhengBai2015}.
\end{remark}

\begin{example}[Numerical predictions at \(y = 1/2\)]\label{ex:bbp-numbers}
With \(y = 1/2\), the threshold is \(1 + \sqrt{1/2} \approx 1.71\) and the upper edge is \(\lambda_+ \approx 2.91\). For \(\alpha = 2.5\) (fundamental),
\[
  \Psi(2.5) \;=\; 2.5 + \frac{(0.5)(2.5)}{2.5 - 1} \;=\; 2.5 + \tfrac{5}{6} \;\approx\; 3.33.
\]
For \(\alpha = 1.4 < 1.71\) (non-fundamental), Theorem~\ref{thm:bbp}(2) gives the limit \(\lambda_+ \approx 2.91\) directly, regardless of the precise value of \(\alpha\) as long as it stays below the threshold. The two panels of Figure~\ref{fig:spiked-spectrum} already showed the top sample eigenvalue near these limits at a single \((n, p)\); Figure~\ref{fig:spike-phase-transition} now traces the convergence as \(n\) grows.
\end{example}

Both curves track Theorem~\ref{thm:bbp} cleanly, and the convergence becomes visible by \(n = 800\); the residual width of the fundamental band shrinks at the slow \(O(n^{-1/2})\) rate that Section~\ref{subsec:fluctuations} will quantify, while the non-fundamental band lives on the much finer edge scale. When the population covariance carries more than one large spike the theorem applies to each \(\alpha_j\) separately, with the associated sample eigenvalues converging to either \(\Psi(\alpha_j)\) or \(\lambda_+\) according to the fundamental/non-fundamental dichotomy; a simulation with one strong and one weak spike, for instance, produces a top sample eigenvalue near \(\Psi(\alpha_1)\) and a second-largest eigenvalue near \(\lambda_+\). (Section~\ref{subsec:two-spikes} returns to the case of several spikes.)

\subsection{Fluctuations of Spiked Eigenvalues}\label{subsec:fluctuations}

Theorem~\ref{thm:bbp} pins down the almost-sure limits but says nothing about how the corresponding sample eigenvalue fluctuates around them. The two regimes turn out to inherit qualitatively different limit laws and qualitatively different scales: on the fundamental side, the fluctuations are Gaussian on the \(\sqrt{n}\) scale; on the non-fundamental side, they are Tracy-Widom on the much finer edge scale of Section~\ref{subsec:scm-highdim}.

\begin{theorem}[Gaussian fluctuations for a simple fundamental spike]\label{thm:fund-fluct}
In the setting of Section~\ref{subsec:johnstone}, let \(\alpha > 1 + \sqrt{y}\) be a fundamental spike of multiplicity \(1\), and let \(\lambda_i(\Sigmahat_n)\) be the associated sample eigenvalue. Then
\begin{equation}
  \sqrt{n}\,\bigl(\lambda_i(\Sigmahat_n) - \Psi(\alpha)\bigr) \;\convd\; \Normal{0,\, \sigma_\alpha^2}, \qquad
  \sigma_\alpha^2 \;=\; 2\, \alpha^2\, \Psi'(\alpha) \;=\; \frac{2 \alpha^2\bigl[(\alpha - 1)^2 - y\bigr]}{(\alpha - 1)^2}.
  \label{eq:fund-fluct}
\end{equation}
\end{theorem}

This is the Johnstone simple-spike specialization of \citet[][Thm.~11.11 and Eq.~(11.36)]{YaoZhengBai2015}; the explicit variance \(\sigma_\alpha^2\) uses the fact that we are in the real Gaussian case: the general statement of that theorem carries a correction term involving the fourth moment of the standardized data entries, and for Gaussian data that correction vanishes. \citet[][Thm.~1.6]{FeralPeche2009} give a clean direct proof for diagonal spiked Gaussian models. The heuristic mirrors Section~\ref{subsec:scm-lowdim}: a separated simple sample eigenvalue is a smooth functional of \(\Sigmahat_n\), and a delta-method argument lifts the entrywise \(\sqrt{n}\)-CLT for \(\Sigmahat_n\) to a \(\sqrt{n}\)-Gaussian limit for the eigenvalue. The Gaussian limit fails once the spike has multiplicity \(m_j \ge 2\), in which case the limiting joint distribution of the corresponding sample eigenvalues is a finite-dimensional GOE-type object \citep[][Eq.~(11.35)]{YaoZhengBai2015}; we do not treat that case.

\begin{theorem}[Edge fluctuations for a non-fundamental spike]\label{thm:nonfund-fluct}
In the setting of Section~\ref{subsec:johnstone}, suppose every spike is non-fundamental, and let \(1 < \alpha < 1 + \sqrt{y}\) be one of them. With the Johnstone constants \(\mu_{n p}\) and \(\sigma_{n p}\) of Theorem~\ref{thm:tw},
\begin{equation}
  \frac{\lambda_{\max}(\Sigmahat_n) - \mu_{n p}}{\sigma_{n p}} \;\convd\; \mathrm{TW}_1.
\end{equation}
\end{theorem}

The result is part of the real-case spiked analysis of \citet[][Thm.~1.6]{FeralPeche2009} and is the natural analogue of the null Johnstone edge fluctuation \citep{Johnstone2001}: a subcritical spike does not separate, the corresponding sample eigenvalue is pinned to the bulk's right edge, and the Tracy-Widom law that governs the null edge continues to govern this near-critical edge. The critical case \(\alpha = 1 + \sqrt{y}\) is genuinely different: the limit is neither Gaussian nor \(\mathrm{TW}_1\) but an interpolating crossover law studied by \citet{BaikBenArousPeche2005}, which we examine empirically at the end of this subsection.

\begin{example}[Numerical predictions at \(y = 1/2\)]\label{ex:fluct-numbers}
At \(y = 1/2\), \(\Psi'(2.5) = 1 - 0.5/(1.5)^2 \approx 0.778\), so Theorem~\ref{thm:fund-fluct} predicts
\[
  \sigma_\alpha^2 \;=\; 2 \cdot (2.5)^2 \cdot 0.778 \;\approx\; 9.72,
\]
i.e.\ \(\lambda_{\max} - \Psi(2.5)\) should look like \(\Normal{0,\, 9.72/n}\) at large \(n\). For the non-fundamental spike \(\alpha = 1.4\), Theorem~\ref{thm:nonfund-fluct} predicts the \(\mathrm{TW}_1\) shape after Johnstone standardization; the centering and scaling constants \(\mu_{n p}, \sigma_{n p}\) are the same as in Theorem~\ref{thm:tw}. At \(n = 800\), \(p = 400\), \(\mu_{n p} \approx 2.912\), only slightly below the asymptotic edge \(\lambda_+ \approx 2.914\).
\end{example}

The visual contrast between the two regimes is already clear at modest \(n\). Figure~\ref{fig:top-eigenvalue-distributions} overlays the empirical distributions of \(\lambda_{\max}(\Sigmahat_n)\) at \(n = 800\), \(R = 1000\), under the two spike values of Example~\ref{ex:fluct-numbers}.

Three features of Figure~\ref{fig:top-eigenvalue-distributions} match the theory cleanly. First, the two histograms are disjoint: the fundamental and non-fundamental cases place \(\lambda_{\max}(\Sigmahat_n)\) in qualitatively different regions of the spectrum. Second, the fundamental-spike histogram is roughly symmetric and visibly wider, consistent with the Gaussian variance \(\sigma_{2.5}^2 / n \approx 9.72/800 \approx 0.012\) of Example~\ref{ex:fluct-numbers}. Third, the non-fundamental histogram is asymmetric, much tighter, and sits slightly to the \emph{left} of the asymptotic edge \(\lambda_+\); this is not a contradiction with Theorem~\ref{thm:bbp} (which guarantees only almost-sure convergence as \(n \to \infty\)), but exactly the finite-\(n\) signature of the Tracy-Widom limit, which is centered at the finite-sample edge \(\mu_{n p}\) and has a negative mean of \(\approx -1.21\) after Johnstone standardization.

To verify the shapes themselves we move to the standardized statistics suggested by Theorems~\ref{thm:fund-fluct} and~\ref{thm:nonfund-fluct}. Figure~\ref{fig:spike-fluctuations} shows them at \(n = 1500\), where the limiting laws emerge cleanly.

The four panels of Figure~\ref{fig:spike-fluctuations} confirm the predictions quantitatively. On the fundamental side, the empirically standardized statistic has mean \(\approx -0.04\) and standard deviation \(\approx 1.01\); the Shapiro-Wilk statistic is \(W = 0.998\) (its perfect-normal value is \(1\)) and a small \(p\)-value of \(0.026\) reflects the very large sample size \(R = 2000\) of the test rather than any meaningful departure from normality. On the non-fundamental side, the Johnstone-standardized statistic has standard deviation \(\approx 1.27\), matching the theoretical \(\mathrm{TW}_1\) value \(1.27\), and mean \(\approx -1.02\) against the theoretical \(-1.21\); the residual upward shift is a genuine finite-\(n\) effect (the spike \(\alpha = 1.4\) sits not far below the threshold and lifts the edge slightly), visible in the figure as the histogram sitting just right of the \(\mathrm{TW}_1\) peak. A Kolmogorov-Smirnov test against \(\Normal{0, 1}\) returns \(D = 0.377\) with \(p < 10^{-250}\), so the statistic is decisively not standard normal, while the histogram and the bent Q-Q plot match the \(\mathrm{TW}_1\) shape. The two regimes therefore inherit not only different almost-sure limits, as in Theorem~\ref{thm:bbp}, but different fluctuation laws on different scales: smooth \(\sqrt{n}\)-Gaussian fluctuations from a separated outlier, versus the \(n^{-2/3}\)-Tracy-Widom signature of an edge eigenvalue.

\paragraph{The critical window.}
Theorems~\ref{thm:fund-fluct} and~\ref{thm:nonfund-fluct} cover two fixed cases, one on each side of the threshold \(\alpha_c = 1 + \sqrt{y}\). Right at the threshold sits the crossover law of \citet{BaikBenArousPeche2005} flagged after Theorem~\ref{thm:nonfund-fluct}; here we examine the crossover empirically, and two facts about the map \(\Psi\) (Proposition~\ref{prop:psi}) explain what we will see. First, the prediction curve meets the edge \emph{flatly}: \(\Psi(\alpha_c) = \lambda_+\) and \(\Psi'(\alpha_c) = 0\), so just above the threshold the outlier has barely pulled away from the edge. Second, the Gaussian spread \(\sigma_\alpha^2 = 2\alpha^2 \Psi'(\alpha)\) shrinks to zero as \(\alpha\) drops toward \(\alpha_c\). So at any finite \(n\) the change cannot be sudden; it is spread over a range of \(\alpha\) around \(\alpha_c\).

Figure~\ref{fig:critical-violin} shows the full distribution of \(\lambda_{\max}(\Sigmahat_n)\) at each spike value. Below the threshold the violins are small, sit at or just under the edge line, and look nearly the same from one \(\alpha\) to the next: a subcritical spike does not separate, so its exact value barely matters, and the slight differences that show are magnified by the zoomed-in vertical axis, which spans only a thin range of eigenvalues. Above the threshold the violins climb along \(\Psi(\alpha)\), grow taller as the spread increases, and change from a lopsided edge shape into a symmetric, bell-shaped Gaussian. The change happens gradually, not as a jump.

So near \(\alpha_c\) the below- and above-threshold distributions are essentially the same, and they become clearly different only away from it.

It is important to read this merging as a finite-sample effect, not as a statement about the limits. For any \emph{fixed} spike off the threshold the two regimes have genuinely different limits: a subcritical spike (\(\alpha < \alpha_c\)) gives the Tracy-Widom edge law, a supercritical one (\(\alpha > \alpha_c\)) the Gaussian law of Theorem~\ref{thm:fund-fluct}. As \(n\) and \(p\) grow with \(\delta = \abs{\alpha - \alpha_c}\) held fixed, the just-below and just-above distributions pull apart and settle onto those two distinct limits. The only subtlety is resolution: the closer the spike sits to \(\alpha_c\), the larger \(n\) and \(p\) must be before the separation becomes visible. At the fixed sample size of Figure~\ref{fig:critical-merge}, a small \(\delta\) is simply below that resolution, so the two sides look identical even though their limits differ.\gdmark{critical-finite-n}

The next subsection turns from the eigenvalue to the eigenvector.

\subsection{Eigenvector Alignment Beyond the Threshold}\label{subsec:eigenvector}

To recap, Theorem~\ref{thm:bbp} establishes conditions under which a spike is visible in the sample spectrum, whereas Theorem~\ref{thm:fund-fluct} describes how its eigenvalue fluctuates. Nevertheless, neither result\gdmark{r3-recap} says whether the corresponding sample \emph{eigenvector} points anywhere near the true population spike direction. In the one-spike model that direction is \(\vect e_1 = (1, 0, \dots, 0)\transpose\), the axis carrying the inflated variance \(\alpha\). Writing \(\widehat{\vect u}_1\) for the unit eigenvector of the top sample eigenvalue, we measure how well it recovers \(\vect e_1\) by the \vocab{squared alignment}
\[
  \abs{\inner{\widehat{\vect u}_1}{\vect e_1}}^2 \;=\; \cos^2 \theta \;\in\; [0, 1],
\]
where \(\theta\) is the angle between the two directions. Note that\gdmark{r3-notethat} squaring removes the harmless sign ambiguity in \(\widehat{\vect u}_1\). A value near \(1\) means near-perfect recovery; a value near \(0\) means the sample eigenvector is essentially noise.

A simulation makes the question concrete before any theory. Figure~\ref{fig:eigenvector-overview} shows two views of the alignment for a weak spike (\(\alpha = 1.4\), below the threshold) and a strong spike (\(\alpha = 2.5\), above it) at \(y = 1/2\). The right panel is the endpoint: at \(n = 1200\) the two land in cleanly separated places, the weak alignment piling up near \(0\) (the eigenvector is essentially noise) and the strong alignment concentrating around \(0.58\) (the eigenvector recovers a definite fraction of the true direction). The left panel shows how they get there: as \(n\) grows the strong alignment settles onto a positive value, while the weak one drifts toward \(0\). The drift is itself a theorem, not just an empirical pattern: it is the subcritical branch of~\eqref{eq:rho-overlap} below, due to \citet{Paul2007}.\gdmark{r3-nonfund-thm} The rest of this subsection explains where these two limits sit and why a threshold separates them.

Generated by \texttt{notebooks/04\_section4\_demos.py}.}
  \label{fig:eigenvector-overview}

\citet{YaoZhengBai2015} explain this by splitting the sample eigenvector into the coordinates that carry signal and those that carry only noise.

\begin{theorem}[Signal-block length, {\citealp[Thm.~11.5]{YaoZhengBai2015}}]\label{thm:eigvec-general}
Let \(\alpha_j\) be a fundamental spike (\(\Psi'(\alpha_j) > 0\)), and let \(\widehat{\vect u}\) be a unit sample eigenvector whose eigenvalue converges to \(\Psi(\alpha_j)\). Split its coordinates as \(\widehat{\vect u} = (\widehat{\vect u}_{(1)}, \widehat{\vect u}_{(2)})\), with \(\widehat{\vect u}_{(1)}\) the \(m\) spike directions and \(\widehat{\vect u}_{(2)}\) the \(p - m\) baseline directions. Then the normalized signal block \(\widehat{\vect u}_{(1)}/\norm{\widehat{\vect u}_{(1)}}\) converges almost surely to a population spike eigenvector for \(\alpha_j\), a definite direction only when the spike is simple (in Johnstone's model with a simple spike, the corresponding coordinate axis), and the two blocks have squared lengths
\begin{equation}
  \norm{\widehat{\vect u}_{(1)}}^2 \;\convas\; d^2 \;=\; \frac{\alpha_j\, \Psi'(\alpha_j)}{\Psi(\alpha_j)}, \qquad \norm{\widehat{\vect u}_{(2)}}^2 \;\convas\; 1 - d^2.
  \label{eq:signal-block}
\end{equation}
\end{theorem}

In words: a fraction \(d^2\) of the unit length of \(\widehat{\vect u}\) lands on the true spike directions, whereas\gdmark{r3-fraction} the remaining \(1 - d^2\) leaks into noise. Specializing to Johnstone's model makes that fraction explicit.

\begin{corollary}[Johnstone model, {\citealp[Cor.~11.6]{YaoZhengBai2015}}]\label{cor:eigvec-johnstone}
For \(\Sigma = \diag(\alpha, 1, \dots, 1)\) and a fundamental spike \(\alpha > 1 + \sqrt{y}\),
\begin{equation}
  d^2 \;=\; \frac{(\alpha - 1)^2 - y}{(\alpha - 1)(\alpha - 1 + y)}.
  \label{eq:johnstone-d2}
\end{equation}
\end{corollary}

For a \emph{simple} spike (\(m = 1\)), the signal block is the single coordinate \(\vect e_1\). In this case,\gdmark{r3-simple-spike} \(\norm{\widehat{\vect u}_{(1)}}^2 = \abs{\inner{\widehat{\vect u}_1}{\vect e_1}}^2\) and~\eqref{eq:johnstone-d2} is a formula for the squared alignment itself. Calling this common limit\gdmark{r3-whatisit} \(\rho(\alpha, y)\), and collecting both branches in a single display:\gdmark{r3-cases}
\begin{equation}
  \abs{\inner{\widehat{\vect u}_1}{\vect e_1}}^2 \;\convas\; \rho(\alpha, y) \;=\;
  \begin{cases}
    \dfrac{1 - y/(\alpha - 1)^2}{1 + y/(\alpha - 1)} & \text{if } \alpha > 1 + \sqrt{y}, \\[1.2em]
    0 & \text{if } 1 < \alpha \le 1 + \sqrt{y}.
  \end{cases}
  \label{eq:rho-overlap}
\end{equation}
The supercritical branch is the corollary above, only rewritten: with \(\Psi(\alpha) = \alpha\bigl(1 + y/(\alpha - 1)\bigr)\) and \(\Psi'(\alpha) = 1 - y/(\alpha - 1)^2\), a one-line computation turns the theorem's \(d^2 = \alpha \Psi'(\alpha) / \Psi(\alpha)\) into exactly the displayed ratio, so~\eqref{eq:signal-block} and~\eqref{eq:rho-overlap} are the same quantity written two ways and no third display is needed.\gdmark{r3-two-displays} The subcritical branch, that the alignment of a non-fundamental spike vanishes in the limit, is due to \citet{Paul2007}.

The identity \(\rho = \alpha \Psi'(\alpha) / \Psi(\alpha)\) ties the spiked model's two phase transitions together exactly: the \emph{eigenvalue} transition of Theorem~\ref{thm:bbp}, where the outlier leaves the bulk as \(\alpha\) crosses \(1 + \sqrt{y}\), and the \emph{eigenvector} transition of~\eqref{eq:rho-overlap}, where the alignment becomes positive at the same crossing.\gdmark{r3-transitions} The alignment carries the same factor \(\Psi'(\alpha)\) that sets the eigenvalue fluctuations in Theorem~\ref{thm:fund-fluct}, so the eigenvalue and the eigenvector become informative at the very same threshold. At \(\alpha = 1 + \sqrt{y}\) we have \(\Psi'(\alpha) = 0\) and the alignment is \(0\); as \(\alpha \to \infty\), \(\rho \to 1\) and the recovery becomes perfect.

In high dimensions, a signal, by which we mean the spike \(\alpha\) itself, the inflated population variance along the spiked direction, must be proportionally stronger to be not merely detectable but \emph{recoverable}.\gdmark{r3-signal}

One last observation closes the loop, and it opens a door to a different corner of the subject. Below the threshold the top sample eigenvector is \vocab{delocalized}: instead of concentrating on the true direction \(\vect e_1\), it spreads its weight roughly evenly over all \(p\) coordinates, with no single one standing out. This is a theorem, not only a simulation: the sharp delocalization bounds of \citet{BloemendalKnowlesYauYin2016} make the \(1/p\) picture below precise (with refinements very close to the threshold).\gdmark{r3-deloc-ref} The clean mental picture is a uniformly random unit vector in \(\R^p\): its squared coordinates each sit at about \(1/p\), so its squared overlap with any one fixed axis is only about \(1/p\), which shrinks to \(0\) as the dimension grows. A subcritical eigenvector behaves the same way. Figure~\ref{fig:eigenvector-samplesize} shows it directly: on log-log axes the weak-spike alignment decays parallel to the line \(1/p\) (the random-direction rate), while the strong-spike alignment flattens out at the constant \(\rho(\alpha, y)\).

We do not develop delocalization any further; it is a substantial subject in its own right, concerned with exactly this question of whether the eigenvectors of a large random matrix spread out across all coordinates or concentrate on a few, and it recurs widely in random matrix theory and its applications \citep{BloemendalKnowlesYauYin2016}. We single it out for the bridge it builds: the one threshold \(\alpha = 1 + \sqrt{y}\) governs both halves of the spiked model at once, so detecting a spike and recovering its direction are two faces of the same transition.\gdmark{delocalization}

Sections~\ref{subsec:johnstone}--\ref{subsec:eigenvector} now give a complete account of a single spike: when it is detectable (\(\alpha > 1 + \sqrt{y}\)), where its eigenvalue settles (\(\Psi(\alpha)\)), how that eigenvalue fluctuates (Gaussian, on the \(n^{-1/2}\) scale), and how well its eigenvector is recovered (\(\rho(\alpha, y)\)). One assumption has been in force at every key step: that the spike is \emph{simple}. The next subsection asks what changes when there are two spikes.

\subsection{Two Spikes: When Can We Tell the Directions Apart?}\label{subsec:two-spikes}

So far, each spike has stood alone. Suppose now there are two large spikes, namely, suppose we can write
\[
\Sigma = \diag(\alpha_1, \alpha_2, 1, \dots, 1)
\]
with\gdmark{r3-two-spike-model} \(\alpha_1 \ge \alpha_2 > 1 + \sqrt{y}\). The eigenvalue side needs nothing new: Theorem~\ref{thm:bbp} applies to each spike on its own, so each lifts its own outlier, at \(\Psi(\alpha_1)\) and \(\Psi(\alpha_2)\). The eigenvector side is where something new appears, and to describe it we need one piece of vocabulary.

The two population directions \(\vect e_1\) and \(\vect e_2\) together span a two-dimensional plane inside \(\R^p\), namely the set of all combinations \(c_1 \vect e_1 + c_2 \vect e_2\). Such a plane is an example of a \vocab{subspace} (a subspace can have any dimension; this one has dimension two), and we call it the spike subspace. There are now two different things we might hope to recover from the data: the spike subspace itself (the plane the signal lives in), or the two individual directions \(\vect e_1, \vect e_2\) inside it. These are different goals, and whether the second is reachable turns out to depend on whether the two spikes are \emph{distinct} or \emph{equal}.

\paragraph{Distinct spikes.}
Take \(\alpha_1 > \alpha_2\). Then the two outliers are themselves separated, \(\Psi(\alpha_1) > \Psi(\alpha_2)\), so the top two sample eigenvalues sit at clearly different heights. The right panel of Figure~\ref{fig:two-spikes-distinct} shows this directly: over many trials the largest eigenvalue \(\lambda_1\) clusters at \(\Psi(\alpha_1) \approx 3.75\) and the second \(\lambda_2\) at \(\Psi(\alpha_2) \approx 3.12\), both well clear of the bulk edge \(\lambda_+ \approx 2.91\). Two spikes produce two separate outliers, just as Theorem~\ref{thm:bbp} predicts for each one.

That gap between the eigenvalues is exactly what lets the data tell the two directions apart. The left panel measures the four squared overlaps between the top two sample eigenvectors \(\widehat{\vect u}_1, \widehat{\vect u}_2\) and the two true directions \(\vect e_1, \vect e_2\). The two \emph{matched} overlaps land on the single-spike values, \(\abs{\inner{\widehat{\vect u}_1}{\vect e_1}}^2 \approx \rho(\alpha_1, y) \approx 0.70\) and \(\abs{\inner{\widehat{\vect u}_2}{\vect e_2}}^2 \approx \rho(\alpha_2, y) \approx 0.46\), while the two \emph{crossed} overlaps, \(\abs{\inner{\widehat{\vect u}_1}{\vect e_2}}^2\) and \(\abs{\inner{\widehat{\vect u}_2}{\vect e_1}}^2\), sit at \(0\). Read in plain terms: the top eigenvector recovers the first direction, the second eigenvector recovers the second, and neither leaks into the other. Two distinct spikes behave like two \emph{decoupled} single spikes: each matched overlap settles at its own single-spike constant and the crossed overlaps settle at zero \citep{BenaychGeorgesNadakuditi2011, Paul2007}. Decoupled is the right word, rather than independent: the two sample eigenvectors are exactly orthogonal by construction, so the pair is strongly constrained; what separates is their \emph{limits}, each matching the corresponding one-spike problem.\gdmark{r3-decoupled}

\paragraph{Equal spikes.}
Now take \(\alpha_1 = \alpha_2 = \alpha\). The two spike eigenvalues coincide, so the top two sample eigenvalues converge to the same limit \(\Psi(\alpha)\), no gap opens between them, and the argument above loses its footing. The reason is a basic fact of linear algebra we already met in Section~\ref{subsec:scm-lowdim}: when a matrix has a repeated eigenvalue, the eigenvectors for it are not unique, and any orthonormal pair spanning the plane is an equally valid choice (Example~\ref{ex:eigenvec-fail} made this vivid for a \(2 \times 2\) matrix whose eigenvectors spun around as it approached a multiple of the identity). So the sample has no reason to prefer \(\vect e_1\) and \(\vect e_2\) over any rotated pair in the plane; it returns an essentially \emph{random} orthonormal pair inside the spike subspace. The plane is identifiable, the directions in it are not.

Figure~\ref{fig:two-spikes-equal} shows both halves of this. The left panel compares two quantities over many trials. The first is the \vocab{subspace alignment}: how much of the recovered pair \(\widehat{\vect u}_1, \widehat{\vect u}_2\) lies in the true plane, scaled to equal \(1\) for a perfect match and \(0\) for none (concretely, half the sum of the four squared overlaps, whose trial averages appear in the right panel). It is sharply concentrated near \(\rho(\alpha, y) \approx 0.58\): the plane is identified to exactly the same partial degree \(\rho < 1\) as a single spike's direction would be, no better and no worse. The second quantity is one individual overlap, \(\abs{\inner{\widehat{\vect u}_1}{\vect e_1}}^2\). It is smeared all the way from \(0\) to \(\rho\), because \(\widehat{\vect u}_1\) points in a random direction inside the plane, landing a random amount along \(\vect e_1\) from one trial to the next.

The right panel makes the same point in one image. It is the \(2 \times 2\) table of \emph{average} squared overlaps \(\abs{\inner{\widehat{\vect u}_i}{\vect e_j}}^2\), with the two sample eigenvectors down the rows and the two true directions across the columns. With equal spikes every entry is about the same size, near \(\rho/2 \approx 0.29\): the table is flat, with no diagonal standing out, which is the fingerprint of two directions that have been scrambled together. Had the spikes been distinct, this same table would have been \emph{diagonal} (large on the matched entries, \(0\) off them), matching the left panel of Figure~\ref{fig:two-spikes-distinct}.

The lesson extends the spectral-separation theme of Section~\ref{sec:scm} into the spiked world. Theorem~\ref{thm:eigvec-general} already hinted at it, in its proviso that the limiting spike eigenvector is a definite direction only when the spike is \emph{simple}. Counting \emph{how many} strong directions are present needs only that each \(\alpha_j\) clear the threshold; telling the directions \emph{apart} needs the spikes themselves to be separated. The next subsection turns this machinery loose on a problem that at first looks nothing like a spiked covariance: clustering.

\subsection{From Spiked Covariance to Spectral Clustering}\label{subsec:clustering}

Everything so far has concerned one kind of hidden structure: a few directions in \(\R^p\) along which the data vary more than the noise baseline. Clustering asks about a different kind. Suppose the \(n\) observations come from \(K\) groups, with the points of group \(k\) scattered around an unknown center \(\vect{\mu}_k \in \R^p\):
\begin{equation}
  \vect{x}_i \;=\; \vect{\mu}_{k(i)} \;+\; \vect{w}_i, \qquad \vect{w}_i \sim \Normal{\vect{0}, I_p} \text{ iid},
  \label{eq:clustering-model}
\end{equation}
where \(k(i)\) is the group of observation \(i\). The task of \vocab{clustering} is to recover the assignment \(i \mapsto k(i)\) from the data alone, without ever being shown a label. This subsection explains, following \citet{LebeauChatelainCouillet2024}, why that task is solved by exactly the spiked-model machinery of this section.

\paragraph{The model in matrix form.}
Stack the observations as columns of \(\mat{X} \in \R^{p \times n}\), the group means as columns of \(\mat{M} = (\vect{\mu}_1 \mid \cdots \mid \vect{\mu}_K) \in \R^{p \times K}\), and record the assignments in the \vocab{assignment matrix} \(\mat{J} \in \{0, 1\}^{n \times K}\), with \(J_{i k} = 1\) if observation \(i\) belongs to group \(k\) and \(0\) otherwise. Each row of \(\mat{J}\) has exactly one \(1\); the matrix is the bookkeeping that the clustering problem wants to find. Model~\eqref{eq:clustering-model} then reads
\begin{equation}
  \mat{X} \;=\; \underbrace{\mat{M} \mat{J}\transpose}_{\text{signal } \mat{P}} \;+\; \underbrace{\mat{W}}_{\text{noise}},
  \label{eq:clustering-matrix}
\end{equation}
with \(\mat{W}\) a \(p \times n\) matrix of iid \(\Normal{0, 1}\) entries. The signal \(\mat{P} = \mat{M}\mat{J}\transpose\) has rank at most \(K\): a low-rank signal buried in full-rank noise, the same shape of problem as a spiked covariance, with the structure now sitting in the \emph{means} rather than in the variances.

It is convenient to normalize the factorization. Let \(n_k\) be the size of group \(k\) and \(\mat{D} = \diag(n_1, \dots, n_K)\). Writing
\begin{equation}
  \mat{P} \;=\; \mat{L} \mat{V}\transpose, \qquad \mat{L} = \mat{M} \mat{D}^{1/2}, \qquad \mat{V} = \mat{J} \mat{D}^{-1/2},
  \label{eq:LV-factorisation}
\end{equation}
the columns \(\vect{v}_1, \dots, \vect{v}_K\) of \(\mat{V}\) are orthonormal: \(\vect{v}_k\) has entries \(1/\sqrt{n_k}\) on the members of group \(k\) and \(0\) elsewhere. We call them the \vocab{normalized indicator vectors}. They are the cleanest possible encoding of the answer: knowing the \(\vect{v}_k\) (even approximately, even in scrambled order) is knowing the clusters.

\paragraph{The similarity matrix.}
Spectral clustering works not with \(\mat{X}\) itself but with an \(n \times n\) \vocab{similarity matrix} whose \((i, j)\) entry measures how alike observations \(i\) and \(j\) are. We use the simplest choice, the \vocab{Gram matrix}
\[
  \mat{G} \;=\; \frac{1}{p}\, \mat{X}\transpose \mat{X}, \qquad G_{i j} \;=\; \frac{1}{p} \inner{\vect{x}_i}{\vect{x}_j}.
\]
Note the reversal: \(\Sigmahat_n = (1/n) \mat{X}\mat{X}\transpose\) is \(p \times p\) and compares \emph{features}, while \(\mat{G}\) is \(n \times n\) and compares \emph{observations}. An eigenvector of \(\mat{G}\) therefore has one entry per data point, which is exactly the right shape for an object that is supposed to label the data points. Practical pipelines often apply a nonlinear kernel function to the similarities and normalize each row by its total weight, its \emph{degree} \citep{vonLuxburg2007}. The identity kernel used here is the tame member of that family, and the analysis of general kernels rests on a delicate matrix Taylor expansion \citep{CouilletBenaych2016} that we do not need.

Two observations connect \(\mat{G}\) to everything we have done. First, \emph{the noise part of \(\mat{G}\) is the Marchenko-Pastur picture of Section~\ref{subsec:scm-highdim} in disguise}. For any matrix \(\mat{W}\), the products \(\mat{W}\transpose\mat{W}\) and \(\mat{W}\mat{W}\transpose\) have the same nonzero eigenvalues: if \(\mat{W}\mat{W}\transpose \vect{u} = \lambda \vect{u}\) with \(\lambda \ne 0\), then multiplying by \(\mat{W}\transpose\) shows \(\mat{W}\transpose\vect{u}\) is an eigenvector of \(\mat{W}\transpose\mat{W}\) with the same \(\lambda\) (and it is not the zero vector, since \(\mat{W}\mat{W}\transpose\vect{u} = \lambda\vect{u} \ne \vect{0}\)); the converse direction is symmetric. So \((1/p)\mat{W}\transpose\mat{W}\) and \((1/n)\mat{W}\mat{W}\transpose\) share their nonzero eigenvalues up to the factor \(n/p = 1/y\), and the bulk of \(\mat{G}\) is the MP bulk rescaled by \(1/y\), with edges
\[
  \frac{\lambda_\pm}{y} \;=\; \frac{(1 \pm \sqrt{y})^2}{y} \;=\; \Bigl(\frac{1}{\sqrt{y}} \pm 1\Bigr)^{2},
\]
plus, when \(p < n\), a block of \(n - p\) exact zero eigenvalues (the matrix has rank at most \(p\)). Second, \emph{the signal part is a low-rank perturbation whose eigenvectors encode the clusters}: the noiseless similarity matrix is \((1/p)\mat{P}\transpose\mat{P} = \mat{V}\bigl[(1/p)\mat{L}\transpose\mat{L}\bigr]\mat{V}\transpose\), so the eigenvectors attached to its nonzero eigenvalues are linear combinations of the normalized indicators \(\vect{v}_k\). Conversely, when the group means are linearly independent, each \(\vect{v}_k\) is a linear combination of those eigenvectors, so the two families span the same \(K\)-dimensional subspace. When the group means are orthogonal the combinations are trivial and the population eigenvectors \emph{are} the indicators. Spectral clustering is, in one sentence, the bet that the top eigenvectors of \(\mat{G}\) stay close to this population structure once the noise is added; whether the bet pays is precisely a spiked-model question.

\paragraph{The two theorems.}
The strength of group \(k\)'s signal is measured by the eigenvalues \(\ell_1 \ge \cdots \ge \ell_K \ge 0\) of the \(K \times K\) matrix \((1/n)\mat{L}\transpose\mat{L}\); for orthogonal means, \(\ell_k = \norm{\vect{\mu}_k}^2 \cdot n_k / n\), so \(\ell_k\) is simply a signal-to-noise ratio. (When the means are linearly dependent, as in the \(\pm\vect{\mu}\) design of study~(a) below, some \(\ell_k\) vanish and the signal carries correspondingly fewer spikes.) The first theorem is the clustering analogue of the spike phase transition of Theorem~\ref{thm:bbp}, with the threshold now at \(\sqrt{y}\).

\begin{theorem}[Spikes of the Gram matrix, {\citealp[Thm.~1]{LebeauChatelainCouillet2024}}]\label{thm:gram-spikes}
In model~\eqref{eq:clustering-matrix}, assume the group sizes stay comparable, the positive \(\ell_k\) are distinct, and \(p, n \to \infty\) with \(p/n \to y \in (0, \infty)\). Let \((\lambda_k, \widehat{\vect{v}}_k)\) be the \(k\)-th largest eigenvalue-eigenvector pair of \(\mat{G}\), and let \(\vect{v}_k\) now denote the eigenvector of the noiseless similarity matrix \((1/p)\mat{P}\transpose\mat{P}\) attached to its \(k\)-th largest eigenvalue (for orthogonal means this is exactly the \(k\)-th normalized indicator). Then, for each \(k\) with \(\ell_k > 0\),
\[
  \lambda_k \;\convas\;
  \begin{cases}
    \xi_k \;=\; \dfrac{(\ell_k + y)(\ell_k + 1)}{y\, \ell_k} & \text{if } \ell_k > \sqrt{y}, \\[1.2em]
    \Bigl(1 + \dfrac{1}{\sqrt{y}}\Bigr)^{2} & \text{otherwise},
  \end{cases}
  \qquad
  \abs{\inner{\vect{v}_k}{\widehat{\vect{v}}_k}}^2 \;\convas\;
  \begin{cases}
    \zeta_k \;=\; 1 - \dfrac{\ell_k + y}{\ell_k (\ell_k + 1)} & \text{if } \ell_k > \sqrt{y}, \\[1.2em]
    0 & \text{otherwise}.
  \end{cases}
\]
\end{theorem}

The statement is proved in \citet[][Thm.~2]{CouilletChatelainLeBihan2021}, and every feature is familiar. A signal direction whose signal-to-noise ratio clears \(\sqrt{y}\) produces an isolated eigenvalue at \(\xi_k\) outside the bulk and an eigenvector that aligns with its population counterpart to the definite degree \(\zeta_k < 1\); one below the threshold is absorbed into the bulk and its eigenvector carries no information, exactly the fundamental versus non-fundamental dichotomy of Sections~\ref{subsec:bbp} and~\ref{subsec:eigenvector}. Even the fine structure repeats: the map \(\ell \mapsto \xi\) has derivative \(1/y - 1/\ell^2\), which vanishes exactly at \(\ell = \sqrt{y}\), so the outlier leaves the bulk edge flatly, the same tangency that made the critical window of Section~\ref{subsec:fluctuations} delicate.

The second theorem is the one our Section~\ref{subsec:eigenvector} could not offer for the covariance model. There we measured eigenvectors only through limiting \emph{constants}; the raw alignment converges to a number, so its limiting distribution is degenerate, and to see an honest limit law one must first center and then rescale. The following result does exactly that, one entry at a time.

\begin{theorem}[Entrywise fluctuations, {\citealp[Thm.~2]{LebeauChatelainCouillet2024}}]\label{thm:eigvec-clt}
In the setting of Theorem~\ref{thm:gram-spikes}, fix \(k\) with \(\ell_k > \sqrt{y}\), fix the sign of \(\widehat{\vect{v}}_k\) by \(\inner{\vect{v}_k}{\widehat{\vect{v}}_k} \ge 0\), and let \(\mathcal{I} \subseteq \{1, \dots, n\}\) be any finite set of entries. Then
\[
  \frac{\sqrt{n}\, \bigl[\widehat{\vect{v}}_k - \sqrt{\zeta_k}\, \vect{v}_k\bigr]_{\mathcal{I}}}{\sqrt{1 - \zeta_k}}
  \;\convd\; \Normal{\vect{0},\, I_{\abs{\mathcal{I}}}}.
\]
\end{theorem}

Read entry by entry, Theorem~\ref{thm:eigvec-clt} says
\begin{equation}
  [\widehat{\vect{v}}_k]_i \;\approx\; \Normal{\sqrt{\zeta_k}\, [\vect{v}_k]_i,\; \tfrac{1 - \zeta_k}{n}},
  \label{eq:entrywise}
\end{equation}
with distinct entries asymptotically independent: each entry of the sample eigenvector is the corresponding entry of the true indicator, shrunk by the factor \(\sqrt{\zeta_k}\) and blurred by Gaussian noise whose variance \((1 - \zeta_k)/n\) is exactly the alignment that is missing. The signal-plus-noise structure of the data matrix~\eqref{eq:clustering-matrix} is transferred intact to the eigenvector. The proof is short and elegant, and we sketch its two steps. First, the eigenvector decomposes as \(\widehat{\vect{v}}_k = \sqrt{\zeta_k}\, \vect{v}_k + \sqrt{1 - \zeta_k}\, \widehat{\vect{v}}_k^{\perp}\) up to a vanishing error, where \(\widehat{\vect{v}}_k^{\perp}\) is a unit vector orthogonal to all the population eigenvectors \(\vect{v}_1, \dots, \vect{v}_K\). Second, because the noise \(\mat{W}\) looks the same in every orthonormal basis (the rotational invariance of Corollary~\ref{cor:gaussian-rotation}, met again with the GOE in Section~\ref{sec:scm}), \(\widehat{\vect{v}}_k^{\perp}\) is uniformly distributed on the unit sphere of that orthogonal complement. And any fixed handful of coordinates of a uniform high-dimensional sphere vector is asymptotically Gaussian: a uniform sphere vector is a Gaussian vector divided by its length, and in high dimension the length concentrates \citep{Vershynin2018}. Nothing about the argument needs the noise to be Gaussian, only rotationally invariant, which is why the prediction survives contact with real data below.

\paragraph{From fluctuations to accuracy.}
Equation~\eqref{eq:entrywise} turns clustering performance into a Gaussian tail computation. Take the balanced two-group case with means \(\pm\vect{\mu}\), where the single informative direction is \(\vect{v}\) with entries \(\pm 1/\sqrt{n}\) and the natural rule assigns observation \(i\) to a group according to the sign of \([\widehat{\vect{v}}]_i\). By~\eqref{eq:entrywise}, an entry has mean \(\pm\sqrt{\zeta/n}\) and standard deviation \(\sqrt{(1-\zeta)/n}\), so it lands on the wrong side of zero with probability \(\Phi\bigl(-\sqrt{\zeta/(1-\zeta)}\bigr)\), where \(\Phi\) is the standard normal CDF. The expected fraction of correctly clustered points is therefore
\begin{equation}
  \text{accuracy} \;\approx\; \Phi\Bigl(\sqrt{\tfrac{\zeta}{1 - \zeta}}\Bigr),
  \label{eq:sign-accuracy}
\end{equation}
ranging from \(1/2\) (a coin flip) at \(\zeta = 0\) to perfect as \(\zeta \to 1\); a version for any number of groups is given in \citet{LebeauChatelainCouillet2024}. This was the missing piece: Theorem~\ref{thm:gram-spikes} alone says the eigenvector points partway at the truth, but only the fluctuation law says how many individual points end up on the wrong side.

\paragraph{Computational study (a): one fundamental spike.}
We test the chain end to end in the smallest instance: two balanced groups with means \(\pm \vect{\mu}\), which gives a rank-one signal with the single ratio \(\ell = \norm{\vect{\mu}}^2\), at \(y = 1/2\), \(n = 800\), \(p = 400\), \(\ell = 4\). Theorem~\ref{thm:gram-spikes} predicts an outlier at \(\xi = 11.25\) against a bulk ending at \((1 + \sqrt{2})^2 \approx 5.83\), and an alignment \(\zeta = 0.775\). Figure~\ref{fig:clustering-one-spike} confirms all three panels of the story: the spectrum is a rescaled MP bulk plus one spike at the predicted spot; the eigenvector entries form two bands at \(\pm\sqrt{\zeta/n}\), tracking the shrunken indicator with the predicted one-standard-deviation envelope; and the residuals match the predicted Gaussian \(\Normal{0, (1-\zeta)/n}\). The predicted accuracy~\eqref{eq:sign-accuracy} is \(\Phi(1.86) \approx 0.97\), visible in the middle panel as the small overlap of the two bands across zero.

\paragraph{Computational study (b): two fundamental spikes.}
Next, two balanced groups with \emph{orthogonal} means of different strengths, \(\ell_1 = 5\) and \(\ell_2 = 2\), both above \(\sqrt{1/2} \approx 0.71\). Orthogonal means make the population eigenvectors exactly the two normalized indicators, so each sample eigenvector should light up on its own group and stay flat on the other, with alignments \(\zeta_1 \approx 0.82\) and \(\zeta_2 \approx 0.58\). Figure~\ref{fig:clustering-two-spikes} shows precisely this. Because \(\ell_1 \ne \ell_2\) the two spikes are distinct in the sense of Section~\ref{subsec:two-spikes}, so the eigenvectors are individually identifiable; equal ratios would scramble them inside their two-dimensional span, exactly as in Figure~\ref{fig:two-spikes-equal}. The right panel plots each observation by its pair of eigenvector entries \(\bigl([\widehat{\vect{v}}_1]_i, [\widehat{\vect{v}}_2]_i\bigr)\). This scatter is the \vocab{spectral embedding} of the data, and Theorem~\ref{thm:eigvec-clt} describes each of its coordinates: one Gaussian cloud per group, centered at the predicted location, with per-coordinate variances \((1 - \zeta_k)/n\) (slightly wider along the weaker spike's axis). Clustering has been reduced to telling two well-separated clouds apart, which is the easy step that a routine algorithm such as \(k\)-means performs by grouping nearby points \citep{vonLuxburg2007}.

\paragraph{The clustering phase transition.}
Finally, the threshold itself. Figure~\ref{fig:clustering-accuracy} sweeps the signal-to-noise ratio \(\ell\) through \(\sqrt{y}\) in the two-group problem and records the fraction of points classified correctly by the sign rule. Below the threshold the eigenvector is noise (Section~\ref{subsec:eigenvector}'s delocalization, now seen on the Gram matrix) and the accuracy sits at the coin-flip level \(1/2\); above it, the accuracy follows the predicted curve \(\Phi(\sqrt{\zeta/(1-\zeta)})\) toward \(1\). This is the phase transition of Section~\ref{subsec:bbp} in applied form: the same algebra that decided when an eigenvalue escapes the bulk decides when a dataset can be clustered at all.

\subsection{Spectral Clustering in Practice: Handwritten Digits}\label{subsec:mnist}

The clustering theory of Section~\ref{subsec:clustering} was proved, and simulated, under a model: Gaussian noise, exact low-rank means. Real data obey no such model. The reason to expect the predictions to survive anyway was built into the proof of Theorem~\ref{thm:eigvec-clt}: the argument used only the fact that the noise has no preferred directions, not that it is Gaussian. This final subsection tests that on the MNIST dataset of handwritten digits \citep{LeCun1998}: seventy thousand grayscale images of the digits \(0\) through \(9\), each \(28 \times 28\) pixels. An image is a vector \(\vect{x}_i \in \R^{784}\) of pixel intensities scaled to \([0, 1]\), so \(p = 784\), and the ``cluster means'' are whatever the average shapes of the ten digits happen to be. Nothing else is assumed.

\paragraph{Two digits.}
We start with the cleanest pair, \(0\) versus \(1\), taking \(6{,}000\) images of each (\(n = 12{,}000\)). The pipeline is the one Figure~\ref{fig:clustering-one-spike} validated, as implemented in the published code of \citet{LebeauChatelainCouillet2024}: form the Gram matrix, take its top eigenvector \(\widehat{\vect{v}}\), and read off the two groups from the signs of its entries (the eigenvector is first centered and renormalized: all images share a large average shape, so the raw top eigenvector partly tracks that common mean rather than the digit contrast, and centering removes exactly that component). The true labels are used only to \emph{evaluate}: with \(\vect{v}\) the normalized label contrast (entries \(\pm 1/\sqrt{n}\)), the alignment is estimated by \(\widehat{\zeta} = \abs{\inner{\vect{v}}{\widehat{\vect{v}}}}^2\), and Theorem~\ref{thm:eigvec-clt} then predicts everything else with no free parameters. Figure~\ref{fig:mnist-zero-one} shows the result. The eigenvector entries form the two predicted bands; the residuals match the predicted Gaussian \(\Normal{0, (1 - \widehat{\zeta})/n}\); and with \(\widehat{\zeta} \approx 0.703\), the predicted accuracy \(\Phi(\sqrt{\widehat{\zeta}/(1-\widehat{\zeta})}) \approx 0.938\) against an observed \(0.934\). A theorem proved for an idealized spiked model predicts the error rate on real handwriting to within half a percentage point.

\paragraph{All forty-five pairs.}
Repeating this for every pair of digits gives Figure~\ref{fig:mnist-pairs}, which compares the observed accuracy (upper triangle) with the prediction~\eqref{eq:sign-accuracy} (lower triangle) for all \(45\) two-digit problems. The agreement is uniform: the mean absolute gap between prediction and observation is \(0.004\) and the worst is \(0.015\). The matrix is, in effect, a difficulty map of MNIST as seen by the identity kernel. Pairs involving the visually distinctive \(1\) are easiest, with \(0\)/\(1\) at \(0.93\) the best of all; the pairs \(2\)/\(3\), \(2\)/\(6\), and \(4\)/\(7\) sit at \(0.50\) to \(0.51\), genuine coin flips: their mean images are too alike, relative to the within-digit variability, for the signal-to-noise ratio \(\ell\) to clear the threshold \(\sqrt{y}\). The theory does not merely fit these numbers, it explains them: each cell is a Gaussian tail \(\Phi(\sqrt{\widehat{\zeta}/(1-\widehat{\zeta})})\), and a hard pair is simply one whose \(\widehat{\zeta}\) is near zero.

\paragraph{All ten digits.}
Finally, the full problem: cluster all ten digits at once (\(5{,}400\) images per digit, \(n = 54{,}000\)). Following the recipe of Figure~\ref{fig:clustering-two-spikes}, each image is embedded by the entries of the top nine eigenvectors of the Gram matrix (computed after removing the mean image), and the standard \(k\)-means algorithm groups the embedded points into ten clusters. Why nine eigenvectors for ten classes? The ten indicator vectors are not ten independent pieces of information: every observation belongs to exactly one class, so knowing nine memberships determines the tenth. In matrix terms, the indicators sum to the constant vector, which carries no class contrast and is exactly the direction that removing the mean deletes; what remains is the \(K - 1 = 9\) dimensions of genuine contrast, just as each two-digit problem above needed only \(K - 1 = 1\) centered eigenvector. The cluster labels are matched to digits, for scoring only, by the assignment that maximizes agreement. Figure~\ref{fig:mnist-ten-class} reports the outcome: \(53\%\) of the \(54{,}000\) images land in the right cluster, against \(10\%\) for random guessing.

The confusion matrix and the difficulty map of Figure~\ref{fig:mnist-pairs} tell one consistent story. The digits that clustering recovers well (\(6\) at \(74\%\), \(7\) at \(71\%\), \(3\) at \(69\%\)) are those whose rows in the pairwise map are mostly easy, and the failures are exactly the predicted ones: digit \(9\), whose pairwise accuracy against \(4\) is \(0.54\) and against \(7\) is \(0.54\) (alignments \(\widehat{\zeta} \approx 0.01\)), is essentially never given its own cluster, because in the embedding its cloud sits on top of those of \(4\) and \(7\). These failures are not algorithmic accidents; they are information limits \emph{of this similarity matrix}. The identity kernel sees only the average shapes of the digits, and where average shapes nearly coincide, Theorem~\ref{thm:gram-spikes} says the relevant spike is below threshold and no method based on the top eigenvectors of \(\mat{G}\) can do better. Nonlinear kernels change the similarity, and hence the signal-to-noise ratios, which is how practical pipelines push past this limit \citep{CouilletBenaych2016}.

\paragraph{The PCA connection.}
Readers who know some machine learning will recognize the embedding step: the top eigenvectors of the Gram matrix \((1/p)\mat{X}\transpose\mat{X}\) are, up to scale, the top \emph{principal component scores} of the data, so the procedure of this subsection is ``run \(k\)-means on the first few principal components,'' one of the oldest recipes in applied data analysis. In that light, this section and Section~\ref{sec:scm} together form the theory of when that recipe works: Section~\ref{sec:scm} described what the components look like when there is nothing to find, Sections~\ref{subsec:johnstone}--\ref{subsec:two-spikes} located the threshold a signal must clear before a component carries information and quantified how much of it is recovered, and Theorems~\ref{thm:gram-spikes} and~\ref{thm:eigvec-clt} priced the residual noise down to the accuracy of the final clustering.

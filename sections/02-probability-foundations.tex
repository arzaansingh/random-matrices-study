\section{Probability Foundations}\label{sec:foundations}

The high-dimensional phenomena studied in later sections all rest on a small set of foundational ideas from classical probability: distributions of random variables, ways to summarize and compare them, modes of convergence, the law of large numbers, and the central limit theorem. This section develops these tools in the depth needed to make later proofs go through cleanly. The exposition starts with the random variable, the basic object of probability theory, and builds toward the multivariate normal distribution that will drive every Gaussian sample-covariance computation in Section~\ref{sec:scm}.

%====================================================================
\subsection{Random Variables and Distributions}\label{subsec:rvs}

Probability begins with a \vocab{probability space} \((\Omega, \mathcal{F}, \Prob)\), where \(\Omega\) is the set of possible outcomes, \(\mathcal{F}\) is a collection of events, and \(\Prob\) assigns each event a number in \([0, 1]\). The full development imposes some technical regularity on \(\mathcal{F}\) and \(\Prob\) (the language of \(\sigma\)-algebras and measures, developed in measure-theoretic probability); we will not need to engage with it directly. We work with this triple in the background; the foreground objects are real-valued functions of the outcome.

\begin{definition}[Random variable]\label{def:rv}
A \emph{random variable} is a function \(X: \Omega \to \R\). Strictly, \(X\) is required to satisfy the technical condition \(\{\omega : X(\omega) \le x\} \in \mathcal{F}\) for every \(x \in \R\), so that \(\Prob(X \le x)\) is well defined; this is called \emph{measurability}, holds for every random variable we will encounter, and we will not verify it explicitly. The distribution of \(X\) is captured by the \vocab{cumulative distribution function} (CDF)
\[
  F_X(x) \;=\; \Prob(X \le x), \qquad x \in \R.
\]
\end{definition}

A CDF is determined by three properties.

\begin{proposition}[Characterization of CDFs]\label{prop:cdf-characterization}
The CDF of any random variable satisfies:
\begin{enumerate}[label=\textnormal{(F\arabic*)},leftmargin=*]
  \item \(\displaystyle\lim_{x \to -\infty} F_X(x) = 0\) and \(\displaystyle\lim_{x \to +\infty} F_X(x) = 1\);
  \item \(F_X\) is right-continuous: \(\lim_{x \to a^+} F_X(x) = F_X(a)\);
  \item \(F_X\) is non-decreasing: \(a \le b \Rightarrow F_X(a) \le F_X(b)\).
\end{enumerate}
Conversely, any \(F: \R \to [0,1]\) satisfying (F1)--(F3) is the CDF of some random variable \citep[][\S 1.5]{Casella2002}.
\end{proposition}

Random variables come in two main flavors. \emph{Discrete} random variables take values in a countable set \(\{x_1, x_2, \ldots\}\), and their distribution is described by the \vocab{probability mass function} (PMF) \(p_X(x_i) = \Prob(X = x_i)\). \emph{Absolutely continuous} random variables admit a nonnegative \vocab{probability density function} (PDF) \(f_X\) with
\[
  F_X(x) \;=\; \int_{-\infty}^x f_X(t)\, dt.
\]

Two random variables \(X, Y\) are \emph{independent} if \(\Prob(X \in A,\, Y \in B) = \Prob(X \in A)\, \Prob(Y \in B)\) for all intervals \(A, B \subseteq \R\). (More generally one asks the identity to hold on all events; the version on intervals suffices for real-valued random variables.)

\begin{example}[Distributions used throughout]\label{ex:common-distributions}
The following appear repeatedly in later sections:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.5em,itemsep=0.25em]
  \item \emph{Bernoulli}\((p)\): \(X \in \{0, 1\}\) with \(\Prob(X = 1) = p\).
  \item \emph{Poisson}\((\lambda)\): \(\Prob(X = k) = e^{-\lambda} \lambda^k / k!\) for \(k = 0, 1, 2, \ldots\).
  \item \emph{Normal} \(\Normal{\mu, \sigma^2}\): \(f_X(x) = (\sqrt{2\pi}\,\sigma)^{-1} \exp\!\bigl(-(x - \mu)^2/(2\sigma^2)\bigr)\).
  \item \emph{Exponential}\((\lambda)\): \(f_X(x) = \lambda e^{-\lambda x}\) for \(x \ge 0\).
  \item \emph{Cauchy}: \(f_X(x) = 1 / \bigl(\pi (1 + x^2)\bigr)\).
\end{itemize}
Their shapes are shown in Figure~\ref{fig:distribution-shapes}.
\end{example}

\begin{figure}[h]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{figures/distribution_shapes.png}
  \caption{Shapes of the five distributions used throughout the report. Bernoulli and Poisson are discrete, displayed as probability mass functions; Normal, Exponential, and Cauchy are absolutely continuous, displayed as densities. The Cauchy distribution will be a recurring foil: its heavy tails make some standard tools break, motivating sharper tools that do not.

\subsection{Moments and Moment Generating Functions}\label{subsec:mgf}

Moments quantify the shape of a distribution.

\begin{definition}[Moments and central moments]\label{def:moments}
For an integer \(j \ge 1\) such that \(\E\abs{X}^j < \infty\), the \(j\)\textsuperscript{th} \emph{moment} of \(X\) is \(\E[X^j]\) and the \(j\)\textsuperscript{th} \emph{central moment} is \(\E[(X - \mu)^j]\), where \(\mu = \E[X]\). The second central moment is the \emph{variance}, \(\Var(X) = \E[(X - \mu)^2]\). The absolute-moment hypothesis ensures that \(X^j\) is integrable, so the bare expectation \(\E[X^j]\) is well defined.
\end{definition}

A generating function for the moments compresses all of this information into a single function.

\begin{definition}[Moment generating function]\label{def:mgf}
The \vocab{moment generating function} (MGF) of \(X\) is
\[
  M_X(t) \;=\; \E[e^{tX}], \qquad t \in \R,
\]
provided the expectation is finite on an open neighborhood \((-h, h)\) of zero.
\end{definition}

The name is justified by the following.

\begin{theorem}[MGFs generate moments]\label{thm:mgf-generates-moments}
If \(M_X(t)\) is finite for \(t \in (-h, h)\), then \(M_X\) is infinitely differentiable at \(0\) and
\[
  M_X^{(n)}(0) \;=\; \E[X^n], \qquad n = 1, 2, \ldots
\]
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
We treat the continuous case in detail; the discrete case is identical with sums in place of integrals. The hypothesis that \(M_X\) is finite on an open interval around zero is the standard regularity condition that justifies differentiating under the integral sign in the calculation that follows; see \citet[][Thm.~2.3.7]{Casella2002}. Therefore,
\[
  \frac{d}{dt} M_X(t)
  \;=\; \frac{d}{dt} \int_{-\infty}^\infty e^{tx} f_X(x)\, dx
  \;=\; \int_{-\infty}^\infty \frac{d}{dt}\!\bigl(e^{tx}\bigr) f_X(x)\, dx.
\]
The inner derivative is \(\frac{d}{dt} e^{tx} = x\, e^{tx}\), so
\[
  \frac{d}{dt} M_X(t)
  \;=\; \int_{-\infty}^\infty x\, e^{tx} f_X(x)\, dx
  \;=\; \E\!\bigl[X\, e^{tX}\bigr].
\]
Setting \(t = 0\) inside the expectation gives \(e^{0 \cdot X} = 1\), and hence
\[
  M_X'(0) \;=\; \E\!\bigl[X\, e^{0 \cdot X}\bigr] \;=\; \E[X].
\]
Iterating the same step \(n\) times produces
\[
  M_X^{(n)}(t) \;=\; \E\!\bigl[X^n\, e^{tX}\bigr],
\]
and evaluating at \(t = 0\) yields \(M_X^{(n)}(0) = \E[X^n]\), as claimed.

In the discrete case, the same calculation applies to the series \(M_X(t) = \sum_x e^{tx} p_X(x)\); convergence of \(M_X\) on \((-h, h)\) justifies term-by-term differentiation, and the rest of the argument is unchanged.
\end{proof}

A second property is the source of most computational power: the MGF of a sum of independent random variables is the product of their MGFs. Indeed, if \(X\) and \(Y\) are independent,
\[
  M_{X+Y}(t) \;=\; \E[e^{t(X+Y)}] \;=\; \E[e^{tX}]\, \E[e^{tY}] \;=\; M_X(t)\, M_Y(t).
\]
We compute MGFs of the canonical distributions to make this concrete.

\begin{example}[MGFs of standard distributions]\label{ex:mgfs}
We compute MGFs from the definition for three of the distributions in Example~\ref{ex:common-distributions}.

\smallskip
\noindent\emph{Bernoulli}\((p)\). The variable \(X\) takes only the values \(0\) and \(1\), so the expectation is a two-term sum:
\[
  M_X(t) \;=\; \E[e^{tX}]
  \;=\; e^{t \cdot 0}\, \Prob(X = 0) \;+\; e^{t \cdot 1}\, \Prob(X = 1)
  \;=\; (1 - p) \;+\; p\, e^t.
\]

\smallskip
\noindent\emph{Poisson}\((\lambda)\). Substitute the PMF \(\Prob(X = k) = e^{-\lambda} \lambda^k / k!\) into the expectation:
\[
  M_X(t) \;=\; \sum_{k = 0}^\infty e^{tk}\, e^{-\lambda}\, \frac{\lambda^k}{k!}
  \;=\; e^{-\lambda}\, \sum_{k = 0}^\infty \frac{(\lambda\, e^{t})^k}{k!}.
\]
The inner sum is the Taylor expansion of \(\exp\) evaluated at \(\lambda e^t\), namely \(\exp(\lambda e^t)\). Combining,
\[
  M_X(t) \;=\; e^{-\lambda}\, e^{\lambda e^t}
  \;=\; \exp\!\bigl(\lambda (e^t - 1)\bigr).
\]

\smallskip
\noindent\emph{Normal} \(\Normal{\mu, \sigma^2}\). Starting from the density,
\[
  M_X(t) \;=\; \frac{1}{\sqrt{2 \pi}\, \sigma} \int_{-\infty}^\infty \exp\!\Bigl(t x \;-\; \frac{(x - \mu)^2}{2 \sigma^2}\Bigr) dx.
\]
The trick is to complete the square in the exponent so the integrand becomes a Gaussian kernel. Expanding the quadratic and collecting terms,
\[
  t x \;-\; \frac{(x - \mu)^2}{2 \sigma^2}
  \;=\; -\frac{1}{2 \sigma^2}\Bigl(x - (\mu + \sigma^2 t)\Bigr)^2 \;+\; \Bigl(\mu t \;+\; \tfrac{1}{2}\sigma^2 t^2\Bigr).
\]
The first piece is the kernel of a normal density with mean \(\mu + \sigma^2 t\) and variance \(\sigma^2\); divided by \(\sqrt{2 \pi}\, \sigma\) it integrates to \(1\). The second piece does not depend on \(x\) and comes outside the integral. Therefore,
\[
  M_X(t) \;=\; \exp\!\Bigl(\mu t \;+\; \tfrac{1}{2} \sigma^2 t^2\Bigr), \qquad t \in \R.
\]
\end{example}

These three examples illustrate the technique. The multiplicative property turns difficult convolutions into easy multiplications: if \(X_1, \ldots, X_n\) are iid Bernoulli\((p)\), their sum has MGF \((1 - p + p e^t)^n\), which is the MGF of Binomial\((n, p)\); independent Poissons of rates \(\lambda_1, \lambda_2\) sum to a Poisson of rate \(\lambda_1 + \lambda_2\); independent normals sum to a normal with summed mean and summed variance.

Beyond computing moments, the MGF determines the distribution uniquely.

\begin{theorem}[MGF uniqueness]\label{thm:mgf-uniqueness}
Let \(X\) and \(Y\) be real-valued random variables.
\begin{enumerate}[label=\textnormal{(\arabic*)},leftmargin=*]
  \item If \(X\) and \(Y\) have the same distribution and \(M_X(t)\) is finite at \(t\), then \(M_X(t) = M_Y(t)\).
  \item Conversely, if \(M_X(t) = M_Y(t)\) for all \(t\) in some open neighborhood \((-h, h)\) of zero, then \(X\) and \(Y\) have the same distribution.
\end{enumerate}
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}[Proof of (1)]
If \(X\) and \(Y\) have the same distribution, then for any function \(g\) for which both expectations are well defined,
\[
  \E[g(X)] \;=\; \E[g(Y)].
\]
(Both sides are computed by the same integral or sum against the common distribution function, so they agree.) Choosing \(g(x) = e^{tx}\), which by hypothesis has a finite expectation under \(X\) at \(t\), yields
\[
  M_X(t) \;=\; \E[e^{tX}] \;=\; \E[e^{tY}] \;=\; M_Y(t),
\]
which is the claim.
\end{proof}

The converse direction (2) is the harder one: it says that the entire distribution of \(X\) is encoded in the MGF on any open interval around zero. The proof reduces to the uniqueness of the two-sided Laplace transform, since \(M_X(t)\) is exactly that transform of the distribution evaluated at \(s = -t\); see \citet[][Thm.~2.3.11]{Casella2002} for the details.

There is a catch. The MGF requires \(e^{tX}\) to have a finite expectation for \(t\) near zero, which can fail if \(X\) has heavy tails.

\begin{example}[Cauchy: no MGF]\label{ex:cauchy-no-mgf}
For the standard Cauchy density \(f(x) = 1 / (\pi (1 + x^2))\), the MGF integral
\[
  \int_{-\infty}^\infty \frac{e^{tx}}{\pi (1 + x^2)}\, dx
\]
diverges for every \(t \ne 0\), since the integrand grows like \(e^{tx}/x^2\) at the right tail (for \(t > 0\)) or at the left tail (for \(t < 0\)). The MGF therefore does not exist on any open neighborhood of zero. This is not a pathology; the Cauchy is a perfectly well-defined distribution, central in probability and physics.
\end{example}

The remedy is the characteristic function.

\subsection{Characteristic Functions}\label{subsec:cf}

Replacing the real argument \(t\) of the MGF by the imaginary argument \(it\) yields an object that always exists.

\begin{definition}[Characteristic function]\label{def:cf}
The \vocab{characteristic function} (CF) of a random variable \(X\) is
\[
  \varphi_X(t) \;=\; \E[e^{itX}] \;=\; \E[\cos(tX)] + i\, \E[\sin(tX)], \qquad t \in \R.
\]
\end{definition}

Because \(\abs{e^{itx}} = 1\) for every real \(x\), the integrand is bounded, and so
\[
  \abs{\varphi_X(t)} \;\le\; \E[\abs{e^{itX}}] \;=\; 1, \qquad \varphi_X(0) = 1.
\]
The CF is therefore well defined for every distribution. When \(X\) has a density, \(\varphi_X\) is exactly the Fourier transform of the density:
\[
  \varphi_X(t) \;=\; \int_{-\infty}^\infty e^{itx}\, f_X(x)\, dx.
\]
When the MGF exists, formal substitution gives \(\varphi_X(t) = M_X(it)\).

\begin{example}[Standard normal CF]\label{ex:cf-normal}
For \(X \sim \Normal{0, 1}\), the MGF computed in Example~\ref{ex:mgfs} is
\[
  M_X(s) \;=\; e^{s^2/2},
\]
and this expression is well defined for every complex \(s\). Substituting \(s = it\) using the identity \(\varphi_X(t) = M_X(it)\) yields
\begin{equation}
  \varphi_X(t) \;=\; M_X(it) \;=\; e^{(it)^2 / 2} \;=\; e^{-t^2/2}, \qquad t \in \R.
  \label{eq:cf-normal}
\end{equation}
A direct computation from the definition of \(\varphi_X\), completing the square in the exponent of the integrand, gives the same answer.
\end{example}

\begin{example}[Standard Cauchy CF]\label{ex:cf-cauchy}
Let \(X\) be standard Cauchy with density \(f(x) = 1 / (\pi (1 + x^2))\). By definition,
\[
  \varphi_X(t) \;=\; \int_{-\infty}^\infty e^{itx}\, \frac{1}{\pi (1 + x^2)}\, dx
  \;=\; \frac{1}{\pi}\, \int_{-\infty}^\infty \frac{e^{itx}}{1 + x^2}\, dx.
\]
The remaining integral is a standard Fourier-transform identity (\citet[][\S XV.4]{Feller1971}; or any Fourier-transform table):
\[
  \int_{-\infty}^\infty \frac{e^{itx}}{1 + x^2}\, dx \;=\; \pi\, e^{-\abs{t}}, \qquad t \in \R.
\]
Dividing by \(\pi\),
\begin{equation}
  \varphi_X(t) \;=\; e^{-\abs{t}}, \qquad t \in \R.
  \label{eq:cf-cauchy}
\end{equation}
The Cauchy CF is well defined and continuous, even though the Cauchy MGF is identically infinite for every \(t \ne 0\).
\end{example}

Figure~\ref{fig:cf-comparison} contrasts the two cases. Although the Cauchy and Normal densities look superficially similar near the origin, their tails are very different, and that difference is exactly what the characteristic function transports into a well-behaved object.

\begin{figure}[h]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{figures/cf_comparison.png}
  \caption{Left: the standard Normal and standard Cauchy densities on the same axes. The Cauchy has a slightly lower peak and noticeably heavier tails; the area in the tails beyond \(\abs{x} = 3\) is several times larger for the Cauchy than for the Normal. Right: their characteristic functions, \(e^{-t^2/2}\) for the Normal and \(e^{-\abs{t}}\) for the Cauchy. Both are well-defined real functions on all of \(\R\) and decay to zero, even though the Cauchy's MGF is identically infinite for every \(t \ne 0\). This is the practical sense in which the CF rescues the heavy-tailed case.}
  \label{fig:cf-comparison}
\end{figure}

Two algebraic properties make CFs computationally convenient. By independence, \(\varphi_{X+Y}(t) = \varphi_X(t)\, \varphi_Y(t)\); by direct substitution, \(\varphi_{aX + b}(t) = e^{itb}\, \varphi_X(at)\). Like the MGF, the CF determines the distribution uniquely, and an inversion formula recovers the CDF from \(\varphi_X\) \citep[][\S 26]{Billingsley1995}.

The deepest property of characteristic functions is the bridge they provide between pointwise convergence of \(\varphi_{X_n}\) and convergence in distribution of \(X_n\).

\begin{theorem}[L\'evy continuity theorem]\label{thm:levy-continuity}
Let \(X_n\) and \(X\) be random variables with characteristic functions \(\varphi_{X_n}\) and \(\varphi_X\).
\begin{enumerate}[label=\textnormal{(\arabic*)},leftmargin=*]
  \item If \(X_n \convd X\), then \(\varphi_{X_n}(t) \to \varphi_X(t)\) for every \(t \in \R\).
  \item Conversely, if \(\varphi_{X_n}(t) \to \varphi(t)\) for every \(t \in \R\) and \(\varphi\) is continuous at \(0\), then \(\varphi\) is the CF of some random variable \(X\), and \(X_n \convd X\).
\end{enumerate}
\end{theorem}

A full proof is in \citet[][Thm.~26.3]{Billingsley1995}. The theorem is the key to the central limit theorem proof in Section~\ref{subsec:limit-theorems}: it reduces a question about distributions to a calculus exercise on a single complex-valued function.

\subsection{Modes of Convergence}\label{subsec:convergence}

A sequence of random variables can converge to a limit in several inequivalent ways. The three modes used in this report, from strongest to weakest, are the following.

\begin{definition}[Three modes of convergence]\label{def:modes}
Let \(X_n, X\) be random variables on a common probability space.
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.5em,itemsep=0.25em]
  \item \(X_n \convas X\) (\emph{almost surely}): \(\Prob\!\bigl(\lim_n X_n = X\bigr) = 1\).
  \item \(X_n \convp X\) (\emph{in probability}): for every \(\varepsilon > 0\), \(\Prob(\abs{X_n - X} > \varepsilon) \to 0\).
  \item \(X_n \convd X\) (\emph{in distribution}): \(F_{X_n}(x) \to F_X(x)\) at every continuity point of \(F_X\).
\end{itemize}
\end{definition}

\begin{proposition}[Implication chain]\label{prop:convergence-implications}
\(X_n \convas X \;\Longrightarrow\; X_n \convp X \;\Longrightarrow\; X_n \convd X\). The converses are false in general.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}[Sketch]
For a.s.\ \(\Rightarrow\) in probability: pointwise convergence holds with probability one, so for every \(\varepsilon > 0\) the probability that \(\abs{X_n - X} > \varepsilon\) tends to zero. For in probability \(\Rightarrow\) in distribution: the conclusion is on CDFs at continuity points and follows by a standard \(\varepsilon\)-argument; see \citet[][Thm.~5.5.12]{Casella2002}.
\end{proof}

A canonical counterexample showing that probability does not imply almost-sure convergence:

\begin{example}[\(\convp\) but not \(\convas\)]\label{ex:p-not-as}
Define a sequence \(X_n \in \{0, 1\}\) by
\[
  \Prob(X_n = 1) \;=\; \frac{1}{n}, \qquad \Prob(X_n = 0) \;=\; 1 - \frac{1}{n},
\]
and assume the \(X_n\) are independent.

\emph{Convergence in probability to \(0\).} Fix any \(\varepsilon \in (0, 1)\). Since \(X_n\) only takes the values \(0\) and \(1\), the event \(\{\abs{X_n - 0} > \varepsilon\}\) is exactly \(\{X_n = 1\}\). Therefore,
\[
  \Prob(\abs{X_n - 0} > \varepsilon) \;=\; \Prob(X_n = 1) \;=\; \frac{1}{n} \;\longrightarrow\; 0,
\]
which is the definition of \(X_n \convp 0\).

\emph{Failure of almost-sure convergence.} Let \(A_n = \{X_n = 1\}\). The events \(A_n\) are independent and their probabilities satisfy
\[
  \sum_{n = 1}^\infty \Prob(A_n) \;=\; \sum_{n = 1}^\infty \frac{1}{n} \;=\; \infty.
\]
The second Borel-Cantelli lemma (Appendix~\ref{app:auxiliary}) says that for independent events whose probabilities sum to infinity, infinitely many occur with probability one. Concretely,
\[
  \Prob\bigl(X_n = 1 \text{ for infinitely many } n\bigr) \;=\; 1.
\]
So almost every realization of the sequence keeps producing the value \(1\) at arbitrarily late indices, which prevents it from settling at \(0\). Hence \(X_n \not\convas 0\).
\end{example}

A second standard fact will be useful in later sections: continuous functions preserve convergence.

\begin{proposition}[Continuous mapping theorem]\label{prop:cmt}
If \(X_n \convas X\) (respectively \(\convp\), \(\convd\)) and \(g: \R \to \R\) is continuous, then \(g(X_n) \convas g(X)\) (respectively \(\convp\), \(\convd\)).
\end{proposition}

Slutsky's theorem (also useful later) is recorded in Appendix~\ref{app:auxiliary}.

\subsection{Limit Theorems: LLN and CLT}\label{subsec:limit-theorems}

The sample covariance matrix studied in Section~\ref{sec:scm} is built from averages. In the mean-zero case, for example,
\[
  \Sigmahat_n \;=\; \frac{1}{n} \sum_{i = 1}^n \vect{X}_i \vect{X}_i\transpose,
\]
and each entry of this matrix is a one-dimensional sample average,
\[
  (\Sigmahat_n)_{jk} \;=\; \frac{1}{n} \sum_{i = 1}^n X_{ij}\, X_{ik}.
\]
Understanding the sample covariance matrix therefore begins with understanding averages of iid random variables. Two universality results do most of the work. The \emph{law of large numbers} (LLN) explains why averages converge to deterministic limits, so that \(\Sigmahat_n\) is a consistent estimator of \(\Sigma\). The \emph{central limit theorem} (CLT) explains the size and shape of their random fluctuations, so that we can characterize the Gaussian limit of \(\sqrt{n}(\Sigmahat_n - \Sigma)\) entrywise. The rest of this subsection develops both results and ends with an explicit bridge to the sample covariance setting.

Before proving the weak law we need a way to turn information about the size of a random variable into a bound on the probability of a large deviation. Markov's inequality is the most basic tool of this kind; Chebyshev's inequality is the version specialized to deviations from the mean.

\begin{theorem}[Markov's inequality]\label{thm:markov}
If \(Y \ge 0\) and \(a > 0\), then \(\Prob(Y \ge a) \le \E[Y]/a\).
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
On the event \(\{Y \ge a\}\), the variable \(Y\) itself satisfies \(Y \ge a\). Multiplying both sides by the indicator \(\indic_{\{Y \ge a\}}\),
\[
  Y\, \indic_{\{Y \ge a\}} \;\ge\; a\, \indic_{\{Y \ge a\}}.
\]
Outside the event both sides equal zero, so the inequality holds everywhere on \(\Omega\). Take expectations and use that \(\indic_{\{Y \ge a\}}\) integrates to \(\Prob(Y \ge a)\):
\[
  \E\!\bigl[Y\, \indic_{\{Y \ge a\}}\bigr] \;\ge\; a\, \Prob(Y \ge a).
\]
Finally, \(Y\, \indic_{\{Y \ge a\}} \le Y\) (because \(Y \ge 0\) and the indicator is at most \(1\)), so
\[
  \E[Y] \;\ge\; \E\!\bigl[Y\, \indic_{\{Y \ge a\}}\bigr] \;\ge\; a\, \Prob(Y \ge a).
\]
Dividing by \(a > 0\) gives the claim.
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[Chebyshev's inequality]\label{thm:chebyshev}
If \(\E[X] = \mu\) and \(\Var(X) = \sigma^2 < \infty\), then for any \(\varepsilon > 0\),
\[
  \Prob(\abs{X - \mu} \ge \varepsilon) \;\le\; \frac{\sigma^2}{\varepsilon^2}.
\]
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Squaring is strictly increasing on \([0, \infty)\), so the event \(\{\abs{X - \mu} \ge \varepsilon\}\) coincides with \(\{(X - \mu)^2 \ge \varepsilon^2\}\):
\[
  \Prob(\abs{X - \mu} \ge \varepsilon) \;=\; \Prob\bigl((X - \mu)^2 \ge \varepsilon^2\bigr).
\]
The variable \(Y = (X - \mu)^2\) is nonnegative with \(\E[Y] = \Var(X) = \sigma^2\). Applying Markov's inequality (Theorem~\ref{thm:markov}) to \(Y\) with threshold \(a = \varepsilon^2\),
\[
  \Prob\bigl((X - \mu)^2 \ge \varepsilon^2\bigr) \;\le\; \frac{\E[(X - \mu)^2]}{\varepsilon^2} \;=\; \frac{\sigma^2}{\varepsilon^2}.
\]
Combining the two displays gives the claim.
\end{proof}

These suffice to prove the weak law.

\begin{theorem}[Chebyshev's weak law of large numbers]\label{thm:wlln}
Let \(X_1, X_2, \ldots\) be iid with \(\E[X_1] = \mu\) and \(\Var(X_1) = \sigma^2 < \infty\). Let \(\bar X_n = n^{-1} \sum_{i=1}^n X_i\). Then \(\bar X_n \convp \mu\).
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Compute the mean and variance of \(\bar X_n\). By linearity of expectation,
\[
  \E[\bar X_n] \;=\; \frac{1}{n} \sum_{i=1}^n \E[X_i] \;=\; \mu.
\]
By independence of the \(X_i\),
\[
  \Var(\bar X_n) \;=\; \frac{1}{n^2} \sum_{i=1}^n \Var(X_i) \;=\; \frac{\sigma^2}{n}.
\]
Apply Chebyshev's inequality (Theorem~\ref{thm:chebyshev}) to \(\bar X_n\) with threshold \(\varepsilon\):
\[
  \Prob(\abs{\bar X_n - \mu} \ge \varepsilon) \;\le\; \frac{\Var(\bar X_n)}{\varepsilon^2} \;=\; \frac{\sigma^2}{n \varepsilon^2} \;\longrightarrow\; 0 \qquad (n \to \infty).
\]
This is the definition of \(\bar X_n \convp \mu\).
\end{proof}

The proof shows \emph{why} averaging reduces variance. Each individual observation has variance \(\sigma^2\), but the average has variance \(\sigma^2 / n\); as \(n\) grows the average becomes more tightly concentrated around \(\mu\). This is the prototype of the principle that makes the sample covariance entries \((\Sigmahat_n)_{jk}\) stabilize around the population entries \(\Sigma_{jk}\) in Section~\ref{sec:scm}.

The weak law and the strong law agree on the punchline (\(\bar X_n \to \mu\)) but differ on the mode of convergence. The weak law guarantees that the probability of being far from the mean tends to zero; the strong law says something more pathwise, namely that with probability one the realized sequence \((\bar X_1(\omega), \bar X_2(\omega), \ldots)\) itself settles down to \(\mu\). The strong law also requires less than finite variance.

\begin{theorem}[Kolmogorov's strong law of large numbers]\label{thm:slln}
If \(X_1, X_2, \ldots\) are iid with \(\E[\abs{X_1}] < \infty\) and \(\E[X_1] = \mu\), then \(\bar X_n \convas \mu\).
\end{theorem}

A proof is given in \citet[][Thm.~22.1]{Billingsley1995}. Figure~\ref{fig:lln} visualizes this stabilization for two distributions with very different shapes but the same mean and variance.

The central limit theorem refines the law of large numbers by saying \emph{at what rate} the convergence happens, and \emph{what the fluctuations look like}. The scale \(\sqrt{n}\) is not arbitrary: since \(\Var(\bar X_n) = \sigma^2 / n\) by the WLLN computation, the typical size of the deviation \(\bar X_n - \mu\) is \(n^{-1/2}\). Multiplying by \(\sqrt{n}\) cancels this decay and keeps the random fluctuation visible. This same dimensional logic produces the \(n^{-1/2}\) scale for the entries of \(\Sigmahat_n - \Sigma\) in Section~\ref{sec:scm}, and (after a different calculation) the \(p^{-2/3}\) scale for the top eigenvalue at the spectral edge in Section~\ref{subsec:scm-highdim}.

\begin{theorem}[Central limit theorem]\label{thm:clt}
Let \(X_1, X_2, \ldots\) be iid with \(\E[X_1] = \mu\) and \(0 < \Var(X_1) = \sigma^2 < \infty\). Then
\[
  Z_n \;:=\; \frac{\sqrt{n}\,(\bar X_n - \mu)}{\sigma} \;\convd\; \Normal{0, 1}.
\]
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
The proof has four steps: standardize, expand the characteristic function near zero, factor across the iid sum, and pass to the limit through L\'evy's continuity theorem.

\smallskip
\noindent\emph{Step 1 (standardize).} Set \(Y_i = (X_i - \mu)/\sigma\). Then \(\E[Y_i] = 0\), \(\Var(Y_i) = 1\), and
\[
  Z_n \;=\; \frac{1}{\sqrt{n}} \sum_{i=1}^n Y_i.
\]

\smallskip
\noindent\emph{Step 2 (expand the CF near zero).} Let \(\varphi(t) = \E[e^{it Y_1}]\). The Taylor expansion \(e^{iu} = 1 + iu - u^2/2 + o(u^2)\) as \(u \to 0\), applied with \(u = t Y_1\), gives
\[
  e^{i t Y_1} \;=\; 1 \;+\; i t Y_1 \;-\; \tfrac{1}{2} t^2 Y_1^2 \;+\; o(t^2 Y_1^2).
\]
The finite-variance assumption \(\Var(X_1) = \sigma^2 < \infty\) is what makes this second-order expansion legitimate after taking expectations: it ensures that the remainder integrates correctly under \(\E\). Using \(\E[Y_1] = 0\) and \(\E[Y_1^2] = 1\),
\[
  \varphi(t) \;=\; 1 \;-\; \tfrac{1}{2} t^2 \;+\; o(t^2) \qquad (t \to 0).
\]
This expansion is the analytic engine of the CLT: near zero, the characteristic function of \emph{every} standardized finite-variance random variable agrees with the standard normal CF up to second order. The CLT then says that the second-order match propagates through the iid sum to give a matching limit, which is the deep reason the Gaussian distribution appears universally.

\smallskip
\noindent\emph{Step 3 (factor by independence).} The \(Y_i\) are independent, so the characteristic function of \(Z_n\) factors into a product of \(n\) copies of \(\varphi(t/\sqrt{n})\):
\[
  \varphi_{Z_n}(t) \;=\; \E\!\Biggl[\exp\!\Bigl(\frac{i t}{\sqrt{n}} \sum_{i=1}^n Y_i\Bigr)\Biggr]
  \;=\; \prod_{i=1}^n \E\!\bigl[e^{i (t/\sqrt{n}) Y_i}\bigr]
  \;=\; \Bigl(\varphi\!\bigl(t/\sqrt{n}\bigr)\Bigr)^n.
\]
Substituting the expansion from Step 2 with \(t/\sqrt{n}\) in place of \(t\),
\[
  \varphi_{Z_n}(t) \;=\; \Bigl(1 \;-\; \tfrac{t^2}{2 n} \;+\; o(1/n)\Bigr)^n.
\]

\smallskip
\noindent\emph{Step 4 (take the limit and conclude).} The standard calculus identity \((1 + a_n/n)^n \to e^a\) when \(a_n \to a\), applied with \(a_n \to -t^2/2\), gives
\[
  \varphi_{Z_n}(t) \;\longrightarrow\; e^{-t^2/2} \qquad (n \to \infty).
\]
By Example~\ref{ex:cf-normal}, \(e^{-t^2/2}\) is the characteristic function of \(\Normal{0, 1}\). The L\'evy continuity theorem (Theorem~\ref{thm:levy-continuity}) then yields \(Z_n \convd \Normal{0, 1}\).
\end{proof}

Figure~\ref{fig:clt} illustrates the CLT for exponential samples: even though the underlying distribution is sharply skewed, the histogram of the standardized sample mean \(Z_n\) approaches the standard normal density as \(n\) grows.

To make the bridge to Section~\ref{sec:scm} concrete, observe that a single entry of the sample covariance matrix is itself an ordinary sample average to which the SLLN of Theorem~\ref{thm:slln} applies directly.

\begin{example}[A sample covariance entry is a sample average]\label{ex:cov-entry-average}
Let \(\vect{X}_1, \ldots, \vect{X}_n \in \R^p\) be iid mean-zero random vectors. The \((j, k)\) entry of the mean-zero sample covariance matrix is
\[
  (\Sigmahat_n)_{jk} \;=\; \frac{1}{n} \sum_{i = 1}^n X_{ij}\, X_{ik}.
\]
This is the sample average of the scalar random variables \(W_i = X_{ij}\, X_{ik}\). The \(W_i\) are iid and, provided \(\E\abs{W_1} < \infty\), have mean \(\E[X_{1j} X_{1k}] = \Sigma_{jk}\). Theorem~\ref{thm:slln} therefore gives
\[
  (\Sigmahat_n)_{jk} \;\convas\; \Sigma_{jk},
\]
which is the entrywise mechanism behind the sample covariance convergence proved in Section~\ref{subsec:scm-lowdim}. The CLT will likewise give the entrywise fluctuation \(\sqrt{n}\bigl((\Sigmahat_n)_{jk} - \Sigma_{jk}\bigr) \convd \Normal{0, \Var(W_1)}\) under finite fourth moments.
\end{example}

\subsection{Random Vectors and the Multivariate Normal Distribution}\label{subsec:rvecs}

The objects of this report are sample covariance matrices, so the basic observation is no longer a single number \(X_i\) but a \emph{vector}
\[
  \vect{X}_i \;=\; (X_{i1}, X_{i2}, \ldots, X_{ip})\transpose,
\]
where \(X_{ij}\) is, for instance, the \(j\)\textsuperscript{th} feature of the \(i\)\textsuperscript{th} observation. A sample covariance matrix summarizes how these features vary together across observations. This subsection collects the vector analogues of Sections~\ref{subsec:rvs}--\ref{subsec:limit-theorems}: random vectors and their joint distributions, the covariance matrix and its first structural property, the multivariate normal distribution, and the multivariate central limit theorem. The linear-algebra companion topics (the spectral theorem, the eigenvalue characterization of positive semidefiniteness, matrix norms, and the convergence of eigenvalues under matrix convergence) are collected separately in Section~\ref{subsec:linalg}.

\paragraph{Random vectors and joint distributions.}

\begin{definition}[Random vector and joint CDF]\label{def:random-vector}
A \emph{random vector} is a function \(\vect{X} = (X_1, \ldots, X_p)\transpose: \Omega \to \R^p\) whose components are random variables on a common probability space. The \emph{joint CDF} of \(\vect{X}\) is
\[
  F_{\vect{X}}(\vect{x}) \;=\; \Prob(X_1 \le x_1,\, \ldots,\, X_p \le x_p), \qquad \vect{x} \in \R^p.
\]
\end{definition}

The joint CDF is a multivariate threshold probability: it asks for the probability that \emph{every} coordinate of \(\vect{X}\) falls below the corresponding coordinate of \(\vect{x}\). Unlike the individual marginal CDFs \(F_{X_i}\), the joint CDF carries dependence information: it records not only how each coordinate behaves on its own, but how the coordinates behave together. The joint CDF obeys the multivariate analogues of the properties (F1)--(F3) of Proposition~\ref{prop:cdf-characterization}.

\begin{proposition}[Properties of the joint CDF]\label{prop:joint-cdf}
The joint CDF \(F_{\vect{X}}: \R^p \to [0, 1]\) satisfies:
\begin{enumerate}[label=\textnormal{(F\arabic*)},leftmargin=*]
  \item As \(x_i \to -\infty\) for any single coordinate \(i\), \(F_{\vect{X}}(\vect{x}) \to 0\); as all coordinates tend to \(+\infty\), \(F_{\vect{X}}(\vect{x}) \to 1\).
  \item \(F_{\vect{X}}\) is right-continuous in each coordinate.
  \item \(F_{\vect{X}}\) is non-decreasing in each coordinate.
  \item Marginal CDFs are recovered by limits: \(F_{X_1}(x_1) = \lim_{x_2, \ldots, x_p \to +\infty} F_{\vect{X}}(x_1, \ldots, x_p)\), and similarly for other coordinates.
\end{enumerate}
Moreover, \(X_1, \ldots, X_p\) are independent if and only if \(F_{\vect{X}}(\vect{x}) = \prod_{i=1}^p F_{X_i}(x_i)\) for every \(\vect{x} \in \R^p\).
\end{proposition}

The discrete and absolutely continuous cases admit joint PMFs and joint densities, defined exactly as in the scalar case.

\paragraph{Three examples of joint distributions.}
The next three examples illustrate the spectrum from full independence to maximal dependence to a smooth Gaussian dependence. They also show the difference between the \emph{support} of a random vector (where it lives) and its \emph{distribution} (how its mass is spread on its support).

\begin{example}[Independent exponentials]\label{ex:indep-expon}
Let \(X_1, X_2\) be iid with \(X_i \sim \mathrm{Exponential}(\lambda)\). Each scalar CDF is \(F_X(x) = 1 - e^{-\lambda x}\) for \(x \ge 0\) (and \(0\) for \(x < 0\)). By independence,
\[
  F_{X_1, X_2}(x_1, x_2) \;=\; F_X(x_1)\, F_X(x_2) \;=\; (1 - e^{-\lambda x_1})(1 - e^{-\lambda x_2}), \qquad x_1, x_2 \ge 0,
\]
and \(F_{X_1, X_2}(x_1, x_2) = 0\) if either coordinate is negative. The mass lives on the positive quadrant \(\{x_1 \ge 0,\, x_2 \ge 0\}\), and the joint density factors as \(f_{X_1, X_2} = f_{X_1} f_{X_2}\).
\end{example}

\begin{example}[Maximally dependent: \(X_1 = X_2\) a.s.]\label{ex:max-dependent}
Let \(X_1 \sim \mathrm{Exponential}(\lambda)\) and set \(X_2 = X_1\) almost surely. Then \(\Prob(X_1 = X_2) = 1\), so the random point \((X_1, X_2)\) lies on the diagonal \(\{x_1 = x_2 \ge 0\}\) with probability one. The joint CDF is
\begin{align*}
  F_{X_1, X_2}(x_1, x_2)
  &\;=\; \Prob(X_1 \le x_1,\, X_2 \le x_2) \\
  &\;=\; \Prob(X_1 \le \min\{x_1, x_2\})
  \;=\; F_X(\min\{x_1, x_2\}).
\end{align*}
The two components have the same one-dimensional marginals as in Example~\ref{ex:indep-expon}, but the support of the pair collapses to a one-dimensional diagonal ray. This vector does \emph{not} admit a joint density on \(\R^2\), because all its mass is concentrated on a set of Lebesgue area zero. We will see shortly that it is the prototypical case of a covariance matrix that is positive semidefinite but \emph{not} positive definite.
\end{example}

\begin{example}[Bivariate normal density]\label{ex:bvn-density}
Let \(\vect{X} = (X_1, X_2)\transpose \sim \Normal{\vect{0}, \Sigma}\) with
\[
  \Sigma \;=\; \begin{pmatrix} \sigma_1^2 & \rho \sigma_1 \sigma_2 \\ \rho \sigma_1 \sigma_2 & \sigma_2^2 \end{pmatrix}, \qquad \sigma_1, \sigma_2 > 0,\quad -1 < \rho < 1.
\]
A short computation gives \(\det \Sigma = \sigma_1^2 \sigma_2^2 (1 - \rho^2)\) and
\[
  \Sigma\inv \;=\; \frac{1}{\sigma_1^2 \sigma_2^2 (1 - \rho^2)}\, \begin{pmatrix} \sigma_2^2 & -\rho \sigma_1 \sigma_2 \\ -\rho \sigma_1 \sigma_2 & \sigma_1^2 \end{pmatrix}.
\]
Substituting into the multivariate normal density (Definition~\ref{def:mvn} below) and simplifying yields the familiar scalar formula
\[
  f_{X_1, X_2}(x_1, x_2) \;=\; \frac{1}{2 \pi \sigma_1 \sigma_2 \sqrt{1 - \rho^2}}\, \exp\!\left(\!-\frac{1}{2 (1 - \rho^2)}\!\left[\frac{x_1^2}{\sigma_1^2} \;-\; \frac{2 \rho x_1 x_2}{\sigma_1 \sigma_2} \;+\; \frac{x_2^2}{\sigma_2^2}\right]\right).
\]
When \(\rho = 0\) the cross term vanishes and the density factors as \(f_{X_1} f_{X_2}\), so \(X_1 \perp X_2\). When \(\rho \ne 0\) the cross term cannot be removed and the components are dependent.
\end{example}

\paragraph{Mean vector and covariance matrix.}

\begin{definition}[Mean vector and covariance matrix]\label{def:mean-cov}
The \emph{mean vector} of \(\vect{X}\) is \(\vect{\mu} = \E[\vect{X}] = (\E[X_1], \ldots, \E[X_p])\transpose\). The \emph{covariance matrix} is
\[
  \Sigma \;=\; \Cov(\vect{X}) \;=\; \E\!\bigl[(\vect{X} - \vect{\mu})(\vect{X} - \vect{\mu})\transpose\bigr],
\]
a \(p \times p\) matrix with entries
\[
  \Sigma_{ij} \;=\; \Cov(X_i, X_j) \;=\; \E[X_i X_j] \;-\; \E[X_i]\, \E[X_j].
\]
\end{definition}

Written out entrywise, the covariance matrix is
\[
  \Sigma \;=\; \begin{pmatrix}
    \Var(X_1)       & \Cov(X_1, X_2) & \cdots & \Cov(X_1, X_p) \\
    \Cov(X_2, X_1)  & \Var(X_2)      & \cdots & \Cov(X_2, X_p) \\
    \vdots          & \vdots         & \ddots & \vdots         \\
    \Cov(X_p, X_1)  & \Cov(X_p, X_2) & \cdots & \Var(X_p)
  \end{pmatrix}.
\]
The diagonal entries record the variability of the individual coordinates, and the off-diagonal entries record the pairwise linear association between coordinates. The matrix is automatically symmetric because \(\Cov(X_i, X_j) = \Cov(X_j, X_i)\).

\begin{example}[A \(2 \times 2\) covariance matrix]\label{ex:cov-matrix-2d}
Suppose \(\vect{X} = (X_1, X_2)\transpose\) has covariance matrix
\[
  \Sigma \;=\; \begin{pmatrix} 4 & 1.5 \\ 1.5 & 1 \end{pmatrix}.
\]
The diagonal reads off \(\Var(X_1) = 4\) and \(\Var(X_2) = 1\); the off-diagonal reads off \(\Cov(X_1, X_2) = 1.5\), so large values of \(X_1\) tend to occur together with large values of \(X_2\). For the direction \(\vect{v} = (1, -1)\transpose\), a direct computation gives \(\vect{v}\transpose \Sigma \vect{v} = 4 - 2(1.5) + 1 = 2\), which equals \(\Var(X_1 - X_2)\). The quadratic form on \(\Sigma\) at \(\vect{v}\) is the variance of the corresponding linear combination of coordinates, an identity we now state and prove in general.
\end{example}

Covariance matrices are not arbitrary symmetric matrices. They have a built-in positivity property because every linear combination of the coordinates has a nonnegative variance, and the quadratic form on \(\Sigma\) literally computes that variance. This is the central property of \(\Sigma\) that will be exploited again and again throughout the report.

\begin{proposition}[\(\Sigma\) is symmetric and PSD]\label{prop:cov-psd}
For any random vector with finite second moments:
\begin{enumerate}[label=\textnormal{(\arabic*)},leftmargin=*]
  \item \(\Sigma\) is symmetric, \(\Sigma_{ij} = \Sigma_{ji}\).
  \item \(\Sigma\) is positive semidefinite: \(\vect{v}\transpose \Sigma \vect{v} \ge 0\) for every \(\vect{v} \in \R^p\). The identity \(\vect{v}\transpose \Sigma \vect{v} = \Var(\vect{v}\transpose \vect{X})\) shows that every quadratic form on \(\Sigma\) is a variance.
  \item \(\Sigma\) is positive definite if and only if no nontrivial linear combination \(\vect{v}\transpose \vect{X}\) (\(\vect{v} \ne \vect{0}\)) is almost surely constant.
\end{enumerate}
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Symmetry: \(\Sigma_{ij} = \Cov(X_i, X_j) = \Cov(X_j, X_i) = \Sigma_{ji}\).

For (2), fix any \(\vect{v} \in \R^p\) and define the scalar \(Y = \vect{v}\transpose \vect{X}\). Then
\[
  Y \;-\; \E[Y] \;=\; \vect{v}\transpose \vect{X} \;-\; \vect{v}\transpose \vect{\mu} \;=\; \vect{v}\transpose (\vect{X} - \vect{\mu}),
\]
and computing the variance gives
\[
  \Var(Y) \;=\; \E\!\bigl[(Y - \E[Y])^2\bigr] \;=\; \E\!\bigl[(\vect{v}\transpose (\vect{X} - \vect{\mu}))^2\bigr].
\]
Since \(\vect{v}\transpose (\vect{X} - \vect{\mu})\) is a scalar, its square equals \((\vect{v}\transpose (\vect{X} - \vect{\mu}))(\vect{v}\transpose (\vect{X} - \vect{\mu}))\transpose = \vect{v}\transpose (\vect{X} - \vect{\mu})(\vect{X} - \vect{\mu})\transpose \vect{v}\). Pulling the constants \(\vect{v}\transpose\) and \(\vect{v}\) outside the expectation,
\[
  \Var(Y) \;=\; \vect{v}\transpose\, \E\!\bigl[(\vect{X} - \vect{\mu})(\vect{X} - \vect{\mu})\transpose\bigr]\, \vect{v} \;=\; \vect{v}\transpose \Sigma \vect{v}.
\]
Since \(\Var(Y) \ge 0\) for every random variable \(Y\), we conclude \(\vect{v}\transpose \Sigma \vect{v} \ge 0\) for every \(\vect{v}\), which is PSD.

For (3), \(\Sigma\) fails to be positive definite precisely when \(\vect{v}\transpose \Sigma \vect{v} = 0\) for some \(\vect{v} \ne \vect{0}\). By the identity above, this is \(\Var(\vect{v}\transpose \vect{X}) = 0\), which is the statement that \(\vect{v}\transpose \vect{X}\) is almost surely a constant.
\end{proof}

Example~\ref{ex:max-dependent} is the prototypical failure of positive definiteness: with \(X_2 = X_1\) a.s., the vector \(\vect{v} = (1, -1)\transpose\) gives \(\vect{v}\transpose \vect{X} = X_1 - X_2 = 0\) a.s., so \(\vect{v}\transpose \Sigma \vect{v} = 0\). The covariance matrix is PSD but not PD.

The identity
\[
  \vect{v}\transpose \Sigma \vect{v} \;=\; \Var(\vect{v}\transpose \vect{X})
\]
is one of the most important facts in this report. The left side is linear algebra; the right side is probability. The bridge between them is the reason every spectral statement about \(\Sigma\) translates to a statement about variances of linear combinations of \(\vect{X}\), and vice versa. In Section~\ref{subsec:linalg} the same identity, combined with the spectral theorem, will give that every eigenvalue of \(\Sigma\) is a variance along the corresponding eigenvector direction.

\paragraph{The multivariate normal distribution.} The multivariate normal distribution is the vector-valued analogue of the ordinary normal distribution. It is the workhorse distribution for the rest of this report: Gaussian sample covariance matrices lead to the Wishart distribution in Section~\ref{subsec:scm-lowdim}, and their spectral behavior in high dimensions is the Marchenko-Pastur / Tracy-Widom story of Section~\ref{subsec:scm-highdim}. We define it cleanly by its characteristic function.

\begin{definition}[Multivariate normal]\label{def:mvn}
A random vector \(\vect{X} \in \R^p\) is \emph{multivariate normal} with mean \(\vect{\mu} \in \R^p\) and covariance matrix \(\Sigma \in \R^{p \times p}\), written \(\vect{X} \sim \Normal{\vect{\mu}, \Sigma}\), if its characteristic function is
\begin{equation}
  \varphi_{\vect{X}}(\vect{t}) \;=\; \exp\!\Bigl(i\, \vect{t}\transpose \vect{\mu} \;-\; \tfrac{1}{2}\, \vect{t}\transpose \Sigma \vect{t}\Bigr), \qquad \vect{t} \in \R^p.
  \label{eq:mvn-cf}
\end{equation}
When \(\Sigma\) is positive definite, \(\vect{X}\) admits the density
\[
  f_{\vect{X}}(\vect{x}) \;=\; \frac{1}{(2 \pi)^{p/2}\, \abs{\Sigma}^{1/2}}\, \exp\!\Bigl(-\tfrac{1}{2}\, (\vect{x} - \vect{\mu})\transpose \Sigma\inv (\vect{x} - \vect{\mu})\Bigr).
\]
\end{definition}

A note on notation: in the scalar case (Example~\ref{ex:common-distributions}) the second argument of \(\Normal{\mu, \sigma^2}\) is a variance, while in the multivariate case the second argument of \(\Normal{\vect{\mu}, \Sigma}\) is a covariance \emph{matrix}. The two-argument macro \(\Normal{\,\cdot\,,\,\cdot\,}\) is the same; only the type of the second slot changes.

Equation~\eqref{eq:mvn-cf} is taken as the definition because it remains valid when \(\Sigma\) is singular (a case that arises exactly as in Example~\ref{ex:max-dependent}), whereas the density form requires \(\Sigma\) to be invertible. The CF definition also has a clean intuitive content: the exponent \(i \vect{t}\transpose \vect{\mu} - \tfrac{1}{2} \vect{t}\transpose \Sigma \vect{t}\) is the CF exponent of a univariate \(\Normal{\vect{t}\transpose \vect{\mu},\, \vect{t}\transpose \Sigma \vect{t}}\), since the scalar projection \(\vect{t}\transpose \vect{X}\) has mean \(\vect{t}\transpose \vect{\mu}\) and variance \(\vect{t}\transpose \Sigma \vect{t}\) (by the bridge identity above). Equation~\eqref{eq:mvn-cf} therefore says
\begin{equation}
  \vect{t}\transpose \vect{X} \;\sim\; \Normal{\vect{t}\transpose \vect{\mu},\; \vect{t}\transpose \Sigma \vect{t}} \qquad \text{for every } \vect{t} \in \R^p.
  \label{eq:mvn-projection}
\end{equation}
That is, \emph{every} one-dimensional projection of a multivariate normal is univariate normal. This is the most useful working characterization of \(\Normal{\vect{\mu}, \Sigma}\).

The multivariate normal is closed under affine transformations.

\begin{proposition}[Affine closure of \(\Normal{\vect{\mu}, \Sigma}\)]\label{prop:mvn-affine}
If \(\vect{X} \sim \Normal{\vect{\mu}, \Sigma}\), \(\mat{A} \in \R^{q \times p}\), and \(\vect{b} \in \R^q\), then
\[
  \mat{A} \vect{X} + \vect{b} \;\sim\; \Normal{\mat{A} \vect{\mu} + \vect{b},\; \mat{A} \Sigma \mat{A}\transpose}.
\]
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
We compute the characteristic function of \(\mat{A} \vect{X} + \vect{b}\). For \(\vect{s} \in \R^q\),
\begin{align*}
  \varphi_{\mat{A} \vect{X} + \vect{b}}(\vect{s})
  &\;=\; \E\!\bigl[e^{i \vect{s}\transpose (\mat{A} \vect{X} + \vect{b})}\bigr]
  \;=\; e^{i \vect{s}\transpose \vect{b}}\, \E\!\bigl[e^{i (\mat{A}\transpose \vect{s})\transpose \vect{X}}\bigr] \\
  &\;=\; e^{i \vect{s}\transpose \vect{b}}\, \varphi_{\vect{X}}(\mat{A}\transpose \vect{s}) \\
  &\;=\; \exp\!\Bigl(i\, \vect{s}\transpose(\mat{A} \vect{\mu} + \vect{b}) \;-\; \tfrac{1}{2}\, \vect{s}\transpose \mat{A} \Sigma \mat{A}\transpose \vect{s}\Bigr).
\end{align*}
This is the CF of \(\Normal{\mat{A} \vect{\mu} + \vect{b},\, \mat{A} \Sigma \mat{A}\transpose}\), so the distribution of \(\mat{A} \vect{X} + \vect{b}\) is identified by the uniqueness of characteristic functions.
\end{proof}

A second property is special to the Gaussian family. For a general distribution, zero covariance only means no linear association: \(\Cov(X_1, X_2) = 0\) does not imply that \(X_1\) and \(X_2\) are independent. For a multivariate normal vector with diagonal covariance, however, the coordinates \emph{are} independent, because the density factors into a product of marginal densities (and the characteristic function factors for the same reason in the singular case). \paragraph{The multivariate central limit theorem.} The univariate CLT (Theorem~\ref{thm:clt}) extends from scalar averages to vector averages.

\begin{theorem}[Multivariate central limit theorem]\label{thm:multivariate-clt}
Let \(\vect{X}_1, \vect{X}_2, \ldots\) be iid random vectors in \(\R^p\) with mean \(\vect{\mu}\) and covariance matrix \(\Sigma\) (with finite second moments). Let \(\bar{\vect{X}}_n = n^{-1} \sum_{i=1}^n \vect{X}_i\). Then
\[
  \sqrt{n}\,(\bar{\vect{X}}_n - \vect{\mu}) \;\convd\; \Normal{\vect{0},\, \Sigma}.
\]
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}[Proof sketch]
The argument is the \emph{Cram\'er-Wold device}: a sequence of random vectors converges in distribution if and only if every fixed linear projection of the sequence converges in distribution to the corresponding projection of the limit. Fix \(\vect{t} \in \R^p\) and project:
\[
  \vect{t}\transpose\, \sqrt{n}\,(\bar{\vect{X}}_n - \vect{\mu}) \;=\; \sqrt{n}\left(\frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n \vect{t}\transpose \vect{X}_i \;-\; \vect{t}\transpose \vect{\mu}\right).
\]
The scalar random variables \(\vect{t}\transpose \vect{X}_i\) are iid with mean \(\vect{t}\transpose \vect{\mu}\) and variance \(\vect{t}\transpose \Sigma \vect{t}\) (by the bridge identity \(\vect{t}\transpose \Sigma \vect{t} = \Var(\vect{t}\transpose \vect{X}_1)\)). The univariate CLT (Theorem~\ref{thm:clt}) gives
\[
  \vect{t}\transpose\, \sqrt{n}\,(\bar{\vect{X}}_n - \vect{\mu}) \;\convd\; \Normal{0,\; \vect{t}\transpose \Sigma \vect{t}}.
\]
This is exactly the projection of \(\Normal{\vect{0}, \Sigma}\) onto \(\vect{t}\), by~\eqref{eq:mvn-projection}. Since this holds for every \(\vect{t}\), the Cram\'er-Wold device delivers the vector convergence.
\end{proof}

\paragraph{Bridge to Section~\ref{sec:scm}.} We can now state, in one paragraph, the role that this entire section will play in the rest of the report. The sample covariance matrix in the mean-zero case is
\[
  \Sigmahat_n \;=\; \frac{1}{n} \sum_{i = 1}^n \vect{X}_i \vect{X}_i\transpose,
\]
so each of its entries is a sample average. The strong law of large numbers (Theorem~\ref{thm:slln}), applied entrywise, will give
\[
  \Sigmahat_n \;\convas\; \Sigma \qquad \text{entrywise as } n \to \infty,
\]
which is the consistency statement of Section~\ref{subsec:scm-lowdim}. The multivariate CLT (Theorem~\ref{thm:multivariate-clt}), applied to the entries jointly, will give the Gaussian fluctuation
\[
  \sqrt{n}\,(\Sigmahat_n - \Sigma) \;\convd\; \mat{G}, \qquad \mat{G} \text{ a real symmetric Gaussian matrix},
\]
on the \(\sqrt{n}\) scale in fixed dimension. The rest of this report studies what these matrix-level limit theorems imply for the eigenvalues and eigenvectors of \(\Sigmahat_n\), and how the story changes qualitatively when the dimension \(p\) grows with the sample size \(n\).

\subsection{Linear Algebra Preliminaries}\label{subsec:linalg}

The remainder of this section collects the linear-algebra results that the analysis of sample covariance matrices in Section~\ref{sec:scm} will rely on but that are not themselves probabilistic: the spectral theorem, the eigenvalue characterization of positive semidefiniteness, matrix norms, and the continuity of eigenvalues as functions of matrix entries. The probability content of Sections~\ref{subsec:rvs}--\ref{subsec:rvecs} gave us \emph{what} sample covariance matrices are; the content here gives us the \emph{tools} for analyzing them.

\paragraph{The spectral theorem.}
A real \(n \times n\) matrix \(A\) is \emph{symmetric} if \(A = A\transpose\). Throughout this paragraph, \(A\) denotes a real symmetric matrix. Symmetry has two crucial consequences: all eigenvalues of \(A\) are real, and there exists an orthonormal basis of \(\R^n\) consisting of eigenvectors of \(A\).

\begin{theorem}[Spectral theorem for real symmetric matrices]\label{thm:spectral}
If \(A \in \R^{n \times n}\) is symmetric, there exist an orthogonal matrix \(Q\) (meaning \(Q\transpose Q = I\)) and a diagonal matrix \(\Lambda = \diag(\lambda_1, \ldots, \lambda_n)\) such that
\begin{equation}
  A \;=\; Q\, \Lambda\, Q\transpose.
  \label{eq:spectral}
\end{equation}
The diagonal entries of \(\Lambda\) are the eigenvalues of \(A\), and the columns \(\vect{q}_1, \ldots, \vect{q}_n\) of \(Q\) form an orthonormal basis of eigenvectors: \(A \vect{q}_i = \lambda_i \vect{q}_i\) and \(\vect{q}_i\transpose \vect{q}_j = \delta_{ij}\).
\end{theorem}

A complete proof requires careful linear algebra; see \citet[][\S 6.4]{Strang2016}. We will use the conclusion freely. The decomposition \(A = Q \Lambda Q\transpose\) has a clean geometric meaning: the action of \(A\) on a vector \(\vect{x} \in \R^n\) is (i) rotate \(\vect{x}\) into the eigenvector basis via \(Q\transpose\), (ii) scale the \(i\)\textsuperscript{th} coordinate by \(\lambda_i\) via \(\Lambda\), (iii) rotate back via \(Q\). Symmetric matrices stretch space along perpendicular directions, with stretch factors given by the eigenvalues.

\paragraph{Quadratic forms diagonalize.}

\begin{corollary}[Diagonalization of quadratic forms]\label{cor:quad-form}
For any \(\vect{x} \in \R^n\), setting \(\vect{y} = Q\transpose \vect{x}\) gives
\[
  \vect{x}\transpose A\, \vect{x} \;=\; \sum_{i=1}^n \lambda_i\, y_i^2.
\]
\end{corollary}

\begin{proof}
Using \(A = Q \Lambda Q\transpose\),
\[
  \vect{x}\transpose A\, \vect{x}
  \;=\; \vect{x}\transpose Q \Lambda Q\transpose \vect{x}
  \;=\; (Q\transpose \vect{x})\transpose\, \Lambda\, (Q\transpose \vect{x})
  \;=\; \vect{y}\transpose \Lambda\, \vect{y}.
\]
Since \(\Lambda = \diag(\lambda_1, \ldots, \lambda_n)\), the last expression is \(\sum_{i=1}^n \lambda_i y_i^2\).
\end{proof}

Because \(Q\) is orthogonal, the map \(\vect{x} \mapsto \vect{y} = Q\transpose \vect{x}\) is a bijection of \(\R^n\) that sends \(\vect{0}\) to \(\vect{0}\), so \(\vect{x} \ne \vect{0}\) if and only if \(\vect{y} \ne \vect{0}\). The sign of \(\vect{x}\transpose A \vect{x}\) is therefore controlled entirely by the signs of the eigenvalues.

\paragraph{Eigenvalues characterize positive definiteness.}

\begin{theorem}[Eigenvalue characterization of PD/PSD]\label{thm:pd-psd-eigen}
Let \(A\) be a real symmetric matrix with eigenvalues \(\lambda_1, \ldots, \lambda_n\).
\begin{enumerate}[label=\textnormal{(\arabic*)},leftmargin=*]
  \item \(A\) is positive semidefinite if and only if \(\lambda_i \ge 0\) for all \(i\).
  \item \(A\) is positive definite if and only if \(\lambda_i > 0\) for all \(i\).
\end{enumerate}
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
We prove (1); (2) is identical with strict inequalities throughout.

\emph{PSD \(\Rightarrow\) \(\lambda_i \ge 0\).} Suppose \(A\) is PSD, and let \(\vect{q}_k\) be a unit eigenvector with eigenvalue \(\lambda_k\). Computing the quadratic form at \(\vect{q}_k\),
\[
  \vect{q}_k\transpose A\, \vect{q}_k \;=\; \vect{q}_k\transpose (\lambda_k \vect{q}_k) \;=\; \lambda_k\, \vect{q}_k\transpose \vect{q}_k \;=\; \lambda_k.
\]
By PSD the left side is \(\ge 0\), so \(\lambda_k \ge 0\).

\emph{\(\lambda_i \ge 0\) \(\Rightarrow\) PSD.} Suppose every eigenvalue of \(A\) is nonnegative. For any \(\vect{x} \in \R^n\), Corollary~\ref{cor:quad-form} gives
\[
  \vect{x}\transpose A\, \vect{x} \;=\; \sum_{i=1}^n \lambda_i\, y_i^2 \;\ge\; 0,
\]
where \(\vect{y} = Q\transpose \vect{x}\) and each \(\lambda_i y_i^2 \ge 0\) by hypothesis. Hence \(A\) is PSD.
\end{proof}

Combining Theorem~\ref{thm:pd-psd-eigen} with Proposition~\ref{prop:cov-psd}, every eigenvalue of a covariance matrix is nonnegative, and the eigenvalues are strictly positive if and only if no nontrivial linear combination of the coordinates is almost surely constant. The remaining sample-covariance analysis in Section~\ref{sec:scm} will use this fact silently every time it manipulates an eigenvalue.

\paragraph{Worked examples in the \(2 \times 2\) case.}
For a symmetric matrix \(A = \begin{pmatrix} a & b \\ b & d \end{pmatrix}\), the characteristic polynomial \(\det(A - \lambda I) = (a - \lambda)(d - \lambda) - b^2\) factors with eigenvalues
\begin{equation}
  \lambda_{\pm} \;=\; \frac{a + d}{2} \;\pm\; \sqrt{\Bigl(\frac{a - d}{2}\Bigr)^2 + b^2}.
  \label{eq:2x2-eigenvalues}
\end{equation}
A convenient \(2 \times 2\) test (a small case of Sylvester's criterion):
\[
  A \text{ is PD } \;\iff\; a > 0 \;\text{ and }\; \det A = ad - b^2 > 0.
\]

\begin{example}[A positive-definite and an indefinite \(2 \times 2\)]\label{ex:2x2-pd-indef}

\noindent\emph{Positive-definite case.} Let \(A_1 = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & -\tfrac12 \\ -\tfrac12 & 1 \end{pmatrix}\). Equation~\eqref{eq:2x2-eigenvalues} gives \(\lambda_{\pm} = 1 \pm \tfrac{1}{2}\), so
\[
  \lambda_+ \;=\; \tfrac{3}{2}, \qquad \lambda_- \;=\; \tfrac{1}{2},
\]
both strictly positive. Sylvester's criterion agrees: \(a = 1 > 0\) and \(\det A_1 = 1 - \tfrac{1}{4} = \tfrac{3}{4} > 0\). One can also exhibit the positivity directly by completing the square in the quadratic form:
\[
  \vect{x}\transpose A_1 \vect{x} \;=\; x_1^2 - x_1 x_2 + x_2^2 \;=\; \Bigl(x_1 - \tfrac{x_2}{2}\Bigr)^2 + \tfrac{3}{4}\, x_2^2,
\]
which is a sum of squares, manifestly \(> 0\) for \(\vect{x} \ne \vect{0}\).

\smallskip
\noindent\emph{Indefinite case.} Let \(A_2 = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & -2 \\ -2 & 1 \end{pmatrix}\). Equation~\eqref{eq:2x2-eigenvalues} gives \(\lambda_{\pm} = 1 \pm 2\), so \(\lambda_+ = 3\) and \(\lambda_- = -1\). The opposite signs make \(A_2\) indefinite. Two directional certificates confirm this: \(\vect{v} = (1, 1)\transpose\) gives \(\vect{v}\transpose A_2 \vect{v} = -2 < 0\), and \(\vect{v} = (1, -1)\transpose\) gives \(\vect{v}\transpose A_2 \vect{v} = 6 > 0\).
\end{example}

\paragraph{Vector and matrix norms.}
A \emph{norm} on a real vector space \(V\) is a function \(\norm{\,\cdot\,}: V \to [0, \infty)\) satisfying, for all \(\vect{v}, \vect{w} \in V\) and scalars \(\alpha\):
\begin{enumerate}[label=\textnormal{(N\arabic*)},leftmargin=*]
  \item \(\norm{\vect{v}} = 0\) if and only if \(\vect{v} = \vect{0}\) (definiteness);
  \item \(\norm{\alpha \vect{v}} = \abs{\alpha}\, \norm{\vect{v}}\) (homogeneity);
  \item \(\norm{\vect{v} + \vect{w}} \le \norm{\vect{v}} + \norm{\vect{w}}\) (triangle inequality).
\end{enumerate}
On \(\R^p\) the standard examples are
\[
  \norm{\vect{v}}_2 \;=\; \Bigl(\sum_{i=1}^p v_i^2\Bigr)^{1/2}, \qquad \norm{\vect{v}}_1 \;=\; \sum_{i=1}^p \abs{v_i}, \qquad \norm{\vect{v}}_\infty \;=\; \max_i \abs{v_i};
\]
each satisfies (N1)--(N3) by direct verification (the triangle inequality for \(\norm{\,\cdot\,}_2\) is the Cauchy-Schwarz inequality applied to \(\norm{\vect{v} + \vect{w}}_2^2\)).

On \(\R^{p \times p}\) we use two matrix norms.

\begin{definition}[Frobenius and operator norms]\label{def:frob-op}
For \(A \in \R^{p \times p}\),
\[
  \norm{A}_F \;=\; \sqrt{\sum_{i=1}^p \sum_{j=1}^p a_{ij}^2}, \qquad \norm{A}_{\mathrm{op}} \;=\; \sup_{\substack{\vect{x} \in \R^p \\ \norm{\vect{x}}_2 = 1}} \norm{A \vect{x}}_2.
\]
\end{definition}

The Frobenius norm is the Euclidean norm of \(A\) viewed as a vector in \(\R^{p^2}\); it satisfies (N1)--(N3) for this reason, and
\[
  A_n \to A \text{ entrywise} \quad \Longleftrightarrow \quad \norm{A_n - A}_F \to 0.
\]
The operator norm measures the largest factor by which \(A\) stretches a unit vector; for symmetric \(A\) it equals the largest absolute eigenvalue, \(\norm{A}_{\mathrm{op}} = \max_i \abs{\lambda_i}\).

\begin{theorem}[Equivalence of norms in finite dimensions]\label{thm:norm-equiv}
For any two norms \(\norm{\,\cdot\,}_a, \norm{\,\cdot\,}_b\) on a finite-dimensional real vector space, there exist constants \(c, C > 0\) such that
\[
  c\, \norm{\vect{v}}_a \;\le\; \norm{\vect{v}}_b \;\le\; C\, \norm{\vect{v}}_a \qquad \text{for every } \vect{v}.
\]
\end{theorem}

The proof is a compactness argument: the function \(\vect{v} \mapsto \norm{\vect{v}}_b / \norm{\vect{v}}_a\) is continuous and positive on the unit sphere \(\{\norm{\vect{v}}_a = 1\}\), which is compact in finite dimensions; its minimum \(c\) and maximum \(C\) are strictly positive, and the inequality extends to all \(\vect{v}\) by homogeneity. The practical consequence for our purposes is that for matrices in finite dimensions, every sense of convergence to zero (entrywise, Frobenius, operator) implies all of the others.

\paragraph{Determinant and the characteristic polynomial.}
The Leibniz formula expresses the determinant as a polynomial in the matrix entries:
\begin{equation}
  \det A \;=\; \sum_{\pi \in S_p} \sgn(\pi)\, \prod_{i=1}^p a_{i, \pi(i)},
  \label{eq:leibniz}
\end{equation}
where \(S_p\) is the group of permutations of \(\{1, \ldots, p\}\). Since polynomials in the entries are continuous functions of the entries, so is the determinant:
\[
  A_n \to A \text{ entrywise} \quad \Longrightarrow \quad \det A_n \to \det A.
\]

The \emph{characteristic polynomial} of \(A\) is
\[
  p_A(\lambda) \;=\; \det(A - \lambda I), \qquad \lambda \in \R,
\]
whose roots are exactly the eigenvalues of \(A\), counted with algebraic multiplicity. The coefficients of \(p_A\) are themselves polynomials in the entries of \(A\) (apply Leibniz to \(A - \lambda I\)), so they too are continuous functions of \(A\).

\paragraph{Eigenvalue convergence.}
We can now state the key linear-algebra ingredient powering Section~\ref{sec:scm}: when a sequence of matrices converges, their eigenvalues converge. The deterministic statement is a standard fact about polynomial roots; we then push it through the modes of convergence developed in Section~\ref{subsec:convergence}, following \citet[][Prop.~3.1]{Genzer2025}.

\begin{proposition}[Eigenvalue convergence via characteristic polynomials]\label{prop:eigen-convergence}
Let \(\{\Sigma_n\}_{n \ge 1}\) and \(\Sigma_0\) be \(p \times p\) symmetric matrices, and write \(\lambda_i(\Sigma)\) for the \(i\)\textsuperscript{th} eigenvalue of \(\Sigma\) (with some fixed ordering convention).
\begin{enumerate}[label=\textnormal{(\arabic*)},leftmargin=*]
  \item \emph{Deterministic.} If \(\Sigma_n \to \Sigma_0\) entrywise, then \(\lambda_i(\Sigma_n) \to \lambda_i(\Sigma_0)\) for every \(i\).
  \item \emph{Almost sure.} If \(\Sigma_n \convas \Sigma_0\) in any matrix norm, then \(\lambda_i(\Sigma_n) \convas \lambda_i(\Sigma_0)\).
  \item \emph{In probability.} If \(\Sigma_n \convp \Sigma_0\) in any matrix norm, then \(\lambda_i(\Sigma_n) \convp \lambda_i(\Sigma_0)\).
\end{enumerate}
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
\emph{Step 1 (deterministic).} Suppose \(\Sigma_n \to \Sigma_0\) entrywise. By the Leibniz formula~\eqref{eq:leibniz}, the coefficients of the characteristic polynomial \(p_{\Sigma_n}(\lambda) = \det(\Sigma_n - \lambda I)\) are polynomial functions of the entries of \(\Sigma_n\), so they converge to the coefficients of \(p_{\Sigma_0}\). A standard result from analysis states that the roots of a monic polynomial depend continuously on its coefficients (after a consistent ordering); applied to the characteristic polynomials this gives \(\lambda_i(\Sigma_n) \to \lambda_i(\Sigma_0)\) for every \(i\).

\emph{Step 2 (almost sure).} Suppose \(\Sigma_n \convas \Sigma_0\) in some matrix norm \(\norm{\,\cdot\,}\). By Theorem~\ref{thm:norm-equiv}, this is equivalent to convergence in Frobenius norm, which in turn is entrywise convergence (with probability one). Concretely, there is an event \(\Omega_0\) with \(\Prob(\Omega_0) = 1\) such that for every \(\omega \in \Omega_0\), \(\Sigma_n(\omega) \to \Sigma_0(\omega)\) entrywise. Step 1 applies to this deterministic sequence and yields \(\lambda_i(\Sigma_n(\omega)) \to \lambda_i(\Sigma_0(\omega))\) for every \(\omega \in \Omega_0\), which is the statement \(\lambda_i(\Sigma_n) \convas \lambda_i(\Sigma_0)\).

\emph{Step 3 (in probability).} Suppose \(\Sigma_n \convp \Sigma_0\) in some matrix norm. The map \(\Sigma \mapsto (\lambda_1(\Sigma), \ldots, \lambda_p(\Sigma))\) is continuous (entries to coefficients to roots), so for every \(\eta > 0\) there exists \(\varepsilon > 0\) such that
\[
  \norm{\Sigma - \Sigma_0} \;<\; \varepsilon \quad \Longrightarrow \quad \max_i \abs{\lambda_i(\Sigma) - \lambda_i(\Sigma_0)} \;<\; \eta.
\]
Translating to events,
\[
  \Bigl\{\max_i \abs{\lambda_i(\Sigma_n) - \lambda_i(\Sigma_0)} \ge \eta\Bigr\} \;\subseteq\; \bigl\{\norm{\Sigma_n - \Sigma_0} \ge \varepsilon\bigr\},
\]
and taking probabilities,
\[
  \Prob\!\Bigl(\max_i \abs{\lambda_i(\Sigma_n) - \lambda_i(\Sigma_0)} \ge \eta\Bigr) \;\le\; \Prob(\norm{\Sigma_n - \Sigma_0} \ge \varepsilon) \;\longrightarrow\; 0.
\]
This is \(\lambda_i(\Sigma_n) \convp \lambda_i(\Sigma_0)\) for every \(i\).
\end{proof}

The deterministic chain \emph{entries \(\to\) characteristic-polynomial coefficients \(\to\) roots} is the entire logical content. The almost-sure and in-probability versions follow by pushing continuity through the respective modes of convergence. In Section~\ref{sec:scm} this proposition will deliver, with no further work, that the eigenvalues of the sample covariance matrix \(\Sigmahat_n\) converge to those of the population covariance \(\Sigma\) whenever \(\Sigmahat_n\) converges to \(\Sigma\) in any of the standard senses.

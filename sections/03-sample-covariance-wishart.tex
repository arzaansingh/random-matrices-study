\section{Sample Covariance Matrices}\label{sec:scm}

Section~\ref{subsec:rvecs} introduced the covariance matrix \(\Sigma\) of a random vector as a theoretical object with entries \(\Sigma_{ij} = \Cov(X_i, X_j)\). In practice this matrix is unknown; what one has in hand is a sample \(\vect{X}_1, \ldots, \vect{X}_n\) and an estimator built from it. The natural estimator is the \emph{sample covariance matrix} \(\Sigmahat_n\), the central object of this report.

The remainder of this section asks two questions about \(\Sigmahat_n\). First, in what sense does \(\Sigmahat_n \to \Sigma\) when \(p\) is fixed and \(n \to \infty\), and how do its eigenvalues and eigenvectors compare to those of \(\Sigma\)? Second, what happens when \(p\) and \(n\) grow together with \(p/n \to y > 0\)? The answers, given in Sections~\ref{subsec:scm-lowdim} and~\ref{subsec:scm-highdim}, are qualitatively different, and that contrast is the central scientific point of this report.

%====================================================================
\subsection{The Sample Covariance Matrix}\label{subsec:scm-foundations}

For random vectors \(\vect{X}_1, \ldots, \vect{X}_n\) with mean \(\vect{\mu}\) and population covariance \(\Sigma\), the most concrete way to define an estimator of \(\Sigma\) is entrywise. The \((j, k)\) entry of \(\Sigma\) is \(\Sigma_{jk} = \E[X_{1j} X_{1k}] - \mu_j \mu_k\); we estimate it by the corresponding sample average. Specializing to the mean-zero case \(\vect{\mu} = \vect{0}\) (used throughout the rest of this report), the natural estimator is
\[
  (\Sigmahat_n)_{jk} \;=\; \frac{1}{n} \sum_{i = 1}^n X_{ij} X_{ik}, \qquad j, k = 1, \ldots, p.
\]
The diagonal entries \((\Sigmahat_n)_{jj} = n^{-1} \sum_i X_{ij}^2\) are sample variances, and the off-diagonal entries are sample covariances; both are sample averages of products of coordinate values across the \(n\) observations.

\begin{definition}[Sample covariance matrix]\label{def:scm}
Let \(\vect{X}_1, \ldots, \vect{X}_n\) be iid copies of a random vector \(\vect{X} \in \R^p\). In the \emph{mean-zero} case (\(\vect{\mu} = \vect{0}\) known), the \vocab{sample covariance matrix} is the \(p \times p\) matrix
\begin{equation}
  \Sigmahat_n \;=\; \frac{1}{n} \sum_{i = 1}^n \vect{X}_i \vect{X}_i\transpose,
  \label{eq:scm-outer}
\end{equation}
with entries \((\Sigmahat_n)_{jk} = n^{-1} \sum_i X_{ij} X_{ik}\). In the \emph{mean-unknown} case, the (unbiased) sample covariance matrix is
\[
  \Sigmahat_n \;=\; \frac{1}{n - 1} \sum_{i = 1}^n (\vect{X}_i - \bar{\vect{X}}_n)(\vect{X}_i - \bar{\vect{X}}_n)\transpose, \qquad \bar{\vect{X}}_n \;=\; \frac{1}{n} \sum_{i = 1}^n \vect{X}_i.
\]
\end{definition}

We work in the mean-zero case throughout the report; the Gaussian models that drive Sections~\ref{sec:scm} and~\ref{sec:spiked} all have \(\vect{\mu} = \vect{0}\) by convention, and the matrix algebra is cleaner. The asymptotic results below carry over to the mean-unknown version with no substantive change.

\paragraph{Matrix form.} Stacking the \(n\) observations as the columns of the \emph{data matrix}
\[
  \mat{X} \;=\; (\vect{X}_1 \mid \cdots \mid \vect{X}_n) \;\in\; \R^{p \times n}
\]
gives a compact rewriting of equation~\eqref{eq:scm-outer}: the sum of outer products is exactly \(\mat{X} \mat{X}\transpose\), so
\begin{equation}
  \Sigmahat_n \;=\; \frac{1}{n}\, \mat{X} \mat{X}\transpose.
  \label{eq:scm-matrix-form}
\end{equation}
A direct check confirms that \eqref{eq:scm-matrix-form} agrees entrywise with \eqref{eq:scm-outer}: the \((j, k)\) entry of \(\mat{X} \mat{X}\transpose\) is \(\sum_i (\mat{X})_{ji} (\mat{X}\transpose)_{ik} = \sum_i X_{ij} X_{ik}\). The matrix form is the version we will manipulate throughout; up to a factor of \(n\), it is the Gram matrix of the data.

\paragraph{Geometric meaning.} The eigenvectors of \(\Sigma\) are the directions of greatest, second-greatest, and so on, variance of \(\vect{X}\); the eigenvalues record the variance along each direction. The eigenvectors of \(\Sigmahat_n\) are the sample analogues. Figure~\ref{fig:scm-axes} shows the picture in two dimensions: for a sample drawn from \(\Normal{\vect{0}, \Sigma}\), the sample principal axes track the population principal axes closely, and the alignment is exact in the limit \(n \to \infty\) (we make this precise in Theorem~\ref{thm:scm-consistency} below).

\paragraph{A small worked example.} The following two-dimensional example, drawn from a hypothetical pair of exam scores, illustrates how Definition~\ref{def:scm} and equation~\eqref{eq:scm-matrix-form} translate into a concrete computation. (We use the mean-unknown version here because the means are not known to be zero.)

\begin{example}[Sample covariance from three two-dimensional observations]\label{ex:scm-numerical}
Three observations on two variables are
\[
  \vect{x}_1 = (70, 75)\transpose, \quad \vect{x}_2 = (80, 85)\transpose, \quad \vect{x}_3 = (90, 80)\transpose.
\]
Step one: compute the sample means. They are \(\bar{x}_1 = 80\) and \(\bar{x}_2 = 80\), so the centered observations are
\[
  \vect{x}_1 - \bar{\vect{x}} = (-10, -5)\transpose, \quad \vect{x}_2 - \bar{\vect{x}} = (0, 5)\transpose, \quad \vect{x}_3 - \bar{\vect{x}} = (10, 0)\transpose.
\]
Step two: sum the outer products. The three outer products are
\[
  \begin{pmatrix} 100 & 50 \\ 50 & 25 \end{pmatrix}, \quad \begin{pmatrix} 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 25 \end{pmatrix}, \quad \begin{pmatrix} 100 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 \end{pmatrix},
\]
summing to
\[
  \sum_{i = 1}^3 (\vect{x}_i - \bar{\vect{x}})(\vect{x}_i - \bar{\vect{x}})\transpose \;=\; \begin{pmatrix} 200 & 50 \\ 50 & 50 \end{pmatrix}.
\]
Step three: divide by \(n - 1 = 2\) to obtain
\[
  \Sigmahat_3 \;=\; \begin{pmatrix} 100 & 25 \\ 25 & 25 \end{pmatrix}.
\]
The diagonal entries (100 and 25) are sample variances; the off-diagonal entry (25) is the sample covariance, indicating positive linear association between the two variables.
\end{example}

\paragraph{Basic structural properties.} The sample covariance matrix inherits the symmetry and positive semidefiniteness of the population covariance.

\begin{proposition}[\(\Sigmahat_n\) is symmetric and PSD]\label{prop:scm-psd}
The sample covariance matrix \(\Sigmahat_n\) is symmetric. It is positive semidefinite, so by Theorem~\ref{thm:pd-psd-eigen} all of its eigenvalues are nonnegative.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
For the mean-zero version~\eqref{eq:scm-matrix-form}, symmetry is immediate: \((\mat{X} \mat{X}\transpose)\transpose = (\mat{X}\transpose)\transpose \mat{X}\transpose = \mat{X} \mat{X}\transpose\). For positive semidefiniteness, fix any \(\vect{v} \in \R^p\) and write
\[
  \vect{v}\transpose \Sigmahat_n \vect{v}
  \;=\; \frac{1}{n}\, \vect{v}\transpose \mat{X} \mat{X}\transpose \vect{v}
  \;=\; \frac{1}{n}\, (\mat{X}\transpose \vect{v})\transpose (\mat{X}\transpose \vect{v})
  \;=\; \frac{1}{n}\, \norm{\mat{X}\transpose \vect{v}}_2^2
  \;\ge\; 0.
\]
The eigenvalue conclusion follows from Theorem~\ref{thm:pd-psd-eigen}. The mean-unknown version uses the centered data matrix in place of \(\mat{X}\); the same identity gives \(\vect{v}\transpose \Sigmahat_n \vect{v} = (n - 1)^{-1} \norm{\mat{X}_c\transpose \vect{v}}_2^2 \ge 0\).
\end{proof}

The remainder of this section divides along the two regimes set up at the start: low-dimensional in Section~\ref{subsec:scm-lowdim} (where \(p\) is fixed and \(n\) grows), and high-dimensional in Section~\ref{subsec:scm-highdim} (where \(p\) grows with \(n\)).

\subsection{Low Dimensions: Consistency, Wishart, and Fluctuations}\label{subsec:scm-lowdim}

In the low-dimensional regime the dimension \(p\) is fixed and the sample size \(n \to \infty\). This is the classical setting of multivariate statistics, in which the limit theorems of Section~\ref{sec:foundations} apply directly to the entries of \(\Sigmahat_n\). We unpack four nested questions: (i) does \(\Sigmahat_n\) converge to \(\Sigma\), (ii) what is the exact distribution of \(\Sigmahat_n\) for Gaussian data, (iii) what is the limiting distribution of the fluctuation \(\sqrt{n}(\Sigmahat_n - \Sigma)\), and (iv) what does that say about individual eigenvalues and eigenvectors of \(\Sigmahat_n\)?

\paragraph{Consistency.} The first question has the cleanest answer. Figure~\ref{fig:scm-consistency} demonstrates it numerically: the Frobenius error \(\norm{\Sigmahat_n - \Sigma}_F\) decays like \(n^{-1/2}\) for any fixed \(p\), and goes to zero with high probability as \(n\) grows. The exact statement and proof are immediate from the strong law of large numbers in Section~\ref{subsec:limit-theorems}.

\begin{figure}[h]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.85\textwidth]{figures/scm_consistency.png}
  \caption{Sample covariance consistency in the low-dimensional regime. For \(\Sigma = I_p\) and iid Gaussian samples, the Frobenius error \(\norm{\Sigmahat_n - \Sigma}_F\) averaged over 40 Monte Carlo trials is plotted against \(n\) (log-log axes) for three values of \(p\). All three curves are parallel to the reference line of slope \(n^{-1/2}\) (dashed), confirming that the error vanishes at the expected rate. Theorem~\ref{thm:scm-consistency} below proves the entrywise version of this convergence.}
  \label{fig:scm-consistency}
\end{figure}

\begin{theorem}[Entrywise consistency of \(\Sigmahat_n\)]\label{thm:scm-consistency}
Let \(\vect{X}_1, \vect{X}_2, \ldots\) be iid copies of \(\vect{X} \in \R^p\) with mean \(\vect{0}\) and finite second moments \(\E[X_j X_k] < \infty\). Then
\[
  \Sigmahat_n \;\convas\; \Sigma \qquad \text{entrywise, as } n \to \infty.
\]
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
The \((j, k)\) entry of \(\Sigmahat_n\) is
\[
  (\Sigmahat_n)_{jk} \;=\; \frac{1}{n} \sum_{i = 1}^n X_{ij}\, X_{ik}.
\]
The random variables \(\{X_{ij} X_{ik}\}_{i \ge 1}\) are iid with mean
\[
  \E[X_{1j} X_{1k}] \;=\; \Sigma_{jk}
\]
(using \(\vect{\mu} = \vect{0}\)) and finite first moment by hypothesis. Kolmogorov's strong law of large numbers (Theorem~\ref{thm:slln}) applied to this sequence yields
\[
  (\Sigmahat_n)_{jk} \;\convas\; \Sigma_{jk}.
\]
Doing this for each pair \((j, k)\) gives entrywise almost-sure convergence \(\Sigmahat_n \convas \Sigma\).
\end{proof}

Combined with the eigenvalue-convergence machinery developed in Section~\ref{subsec:linalg}, entrywise convergence forces convergence of the eigenvalues themselves.

\begin{corollary}[Sample eigenvalues converge to population eigenvalues]\label{cor:scm-eigen-convergence}
Under the hypotheses of Theorem~\ref{thm:scm-consistency}, \(\lambda_i(\Sigmahat_n) \convas \lambda_i(\Sigma)\) for every \(i = 1, \ldots, p\).
\end{corollary}

\begin{proof}
Apply Proposition~\ref{prop:eigen-convergence}(2) (the almost-sure version of eigenvalue convergence) to the random matrix sequence \(\Sigmahat_n \convas \Sigma\).
\end{proof}

Theorem~\ref{thm:scm-consistency} and Corollary~\ref{cor:scm-eigen-convergence} together give the qualitative low-dimensional picture: \(\Sigmahat_n\) is a consistent estimator of \(\Sigma\), and the sample eigenvalues track the population eigenvalues. The remaining questions concern the size and shape of the fluctuations.

\paragraph{The Wishart distribution.} When the observations are Gaussian, \(\Sigmahat_n\) (more precisely, \(n \Sigmahat_n\)) has an exact distribution with a name. This makes it a tractable starting point for finer questions.

\begin{definition}[Wishart distribution]\label{def:wishart}
Let \(\vect{X}_1, \ldots, \vect{X}_n\) be iid \(\Normal{\vect{0}, \Sigma}\) random vectors in \(\R^p\), and set
\[
  \mat{S} \;=\; \sum_{i = 1}^n \vect{X}_i \vect{X}_i\transpose \;=\; n\, \Sigmahat_n.
\]
The distribution of \(\mat{S}\) is the \vocab{Wishart distribution} with \(n\) degrees of freedom and scale matrix \(\Sigma\), written \(\mat{S} \sim \Wishart{p}{n, \Sigma}\). When \(n \ge p\) and \(\Sigma\) is positive definite, \(\mat{S}\) admits the density (on the cone of positive-definite matrices)
\begin{equation}
  f_{\mat{S}}(\mat{S}) \;=\; \frac{\abs{\mat{S}}^{(n - p - 1)/2}\, \exp\!\bigl(-\tfrac{1}{2}\, \tr(\Sigma\inv \mat{S})\bigr)}{2^{n p / 2}\, \abs{\Sigma}^{n/2}\, \Gamma_p(n / 2)},
  \label{eq:wishart-density}
\end{equation}
where \(\Gamma_p\) is the multivariate gamma function; see \citet[][\S 3.2]{YaoZhengBai2015} for a derivation.
\end{definition}

The Wishart distribution is the multivariate analogue of the chi-square distribution, and recovers it in dimension one.

\begin{example}[Wishart in dimension one]\label{ex:wishart-p1}
Take \(p = 1\) and \(\Sigma = 1\). Then each \(\vect{X}_i\) is a scalar \(X_i \sim \Normal{0, 1}\), so \(\mat{S} = \sum_{i = 1}^n X_i^2\). By the definition of the chi-square distribution, \(\mat{S} \sim \chi_n^2\). Substituting \(p = 1\), \(\Sigma = 1\), and \(\tr(\Sigma\inv \mat{S}) = \mat{S}\) into~\eqref{eq:wishart-density} confirms this:
\[
  f_{\mat{S}}(s) \;=\; \frac{s^{(n - 2)/2}\, e^{-s/2}}{2^{n/2}\, \Gamma(n/2)}, \qquad s > 0,
\]
which is exactly the \(\chi_n^2\) density.
\end{example}

\paragraph{Eigenvalue repulsion in two dimensions.} For \(\Sigma = I_2\), the joint distribution of the two ordered eigenvalues \(0 \le \lambda_1 \le \lambda_2\) of \(\mat{S} \sim \Wishart{2}{n, I_2}\) admits an explicit density,
\begin{equation}
  f(\lambda_1, \lambda_2) \;=\; C\, (\lambda_1 \lambda_2)^{(n - 3)/2}\, e^{-(\lambda_1 + \lambda_2) / 2}\, (\lambda_2 - \lambda_1)\, \indic_{\{0 \le \lambda_1 \le \lambda_2\}},
  \label{eq:wishart-2d-density}
\end{equation}
where \(C > 0\) is a normalizing constant; a derivation via a Jacobian computation on the spectral decomposition \(\mat{S} = \mat{Q} \diag(\lambda_1, \lambda_2) \mat{Q}\transpose\) is given in \citet[][Ch.~3]{YaoZhengBai2015}. The key feature of~\eqref{eq:wishart-2d-density} is the factor \((\lambda_2 - \lambda_1)\), which forces the joint density to vanish on the diagonal \(\lambda_1 = \lambda_2\). This is the simplest case of \vocab{eigenvalue repulsion}: the two eigenvalues of a random symmetric matrix avoid each other. Figure~\ref{fig:wishart-density} visualizes the theoretical density, and Figure~\ref{fig:wishart-2d} confirms repulsion in simulation.

\paragraph{The Gaussian Orthogonal Ensemble.} The eigenvalue repulsion that appears in the Wishart density~\eqref{eq:wishart-2d-density} also appears, with the same \((\lambda_2 - \lambda_1)\) factor, in a different random matrix model: the Gaussian Orthogonal Ensemble. We introduce it here because it is the limit object in the matrix-valued CLT below.

\begin{definition}[Gaussian Orthogonal Ensemble, \(2 \times 2\) case]\label{def:goe}
A \(2 \times 2\) random matrix
\[
  \mat{G} \;=\; \begin{pmatrix} G_{11} & G_{12} \\ G_{12} & G_{22} \end{pmatrix}
\]
is said to belong to the \vocab{Gaussian Orthogonal Ensemble} (\(\GOE_2\)) if its three independent entries are Gaussian with
\[
  G_{11},\, G_{22} \;\sim\; \Normal{0,\, 2}, \qquad G_{12} \;\sim\; \Normal{0,\, 1},
\]
and the three entries are mutually independent. More generally, a \(p \times p\) GOE matrix has independent Gaussian entries on and above the diagonal with diagonal variance \(2\) and off-diagonal variance \(1\).
\end{definition}

The variances \(2\) on the diagonal and \(1\) off the diagonal are chosen exactly so that the matrix is invariant in distribution under orthogonal conjugation \(\mat{G} \mapsto \mat{O} \mat{G} \mat{O}\transpose\); this is the eponymous orthogonal invariance. The top eigenvalue of \(\GOE_2\) is a non-Gaussian random variable, with a density slightly right-skewed by the repulsion that mirrors~\eqref{eq:wishart-2d-density}.

\paragraph{The matrix-valued central limit theorem.} The next step transports the multivariate CLT of Section~\ref{subsec:rvecs} to a statement about the entire fluctuation matrix \(\sqrt{n}(\Sigmahat_n - \Sigma)\). For the null case \(\Sigma = I_2\), the limit is exactly the GOE.

\begin{proposition}[Matrix-valued CLT for \(\Sigma = I_2\)]\label{prop:matrix-clt-I2}
Let \(\vect{X}_i \sim \Normal{\vect{0}, I_2}\) be iid. Then \(\sqrt{n}\,(\Sigmahat_n - I_2)\) converges in distribution, as \(n \to \infty\), to a \(\GOE_2\) matrix.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
The three independent entries of the symmetric matrix \(\sqrt{n}\,(\Sigmahat_n - I_2)\) are
\[
  \sqrt{n}\,((\Sigmahat_n)_{11} - 1), \quad \sqrt{n}\,((\Sigmahat_n)_{22} - 1), \quad \sqrt{n}\,(\Sigmahat_n)_{12},
\]
each of which is a sum of iid centered random variables, so the multivariate central limit theorem of Section~\ref{subsec:rvecs} applies. We compute the limiting variances and verify that the cross-covariances vanish.

For the diagonal entries, write \((\Sigmahat_n)_{jj} - 1 = n^{-1} \sum_i (X_{ij}^2 - 1)\). Each summand has mean zero and
\[
  \Var(X_{ij}^2) \;=\; \E[X_{ij}^4] \;-\; (\E[X_{ij}^2])^2 \;=\; 3 \;-\; 1 \;=\; 2,
\]
using \(\E[Z^4] = 3\) for \(Z \sim \Normal{0, 1}\). Hence the limiting variance of \(\sqrt{n}\,((\Sigmahat_n)_{jj} - 1)\) is \(2\).

For the off-diagonal entry, write \((\Sigmahat_n)_{12} = n^{-1} \sum_i X_{i1} X_{i2}\). Each summand has mean zero (independence) and variance
\[
  \Var(X_{i1} X_{i2}) \;=\; \E[X_{i1}^2]\, \E[X_{i2}^2] \;=\; 1,
\]
again by independence. Hence the limiting variance of \(\sqrt{n}\,(\Sigmahat_n)_{12}\) is \(1\).

The cross-covariances vanish:
\[
  \Cov(X_{i1}^2,\, X_{i1} X_{i2}) \;=\; \E[X_{i1}^3]\, \E[X_{i2}] \;=\; 0,
\]
because \(\E[X_{i2}] = 0\), and analogously for the other pairs.

The three limiting variances are therefore \(2\), \(2\), \(1\) and the three limits are uncorrelated. By the multivariate CLT, the joint limit is \(\Normal{\vect{0},\, \diag(2, 2, 1)}\), which is exactly the entrywise distribution of a \(\GOE_2\) matrix from Definition~\ref{def:goe}.
\end{proof}

\paragraph{First-order eigenvalue perturbation.} Proposition~\ref{prop:matrix-clt-I2} describes the fluctuation of the \emph{matrix} \(\Sigmahat_n\), not of its individual eigenvalues. To translate matrix-level convergence into eigenvalue-level convergence, we need to know how eigenvalues respond to small perturbations of a symmetric matrix. The cleanest such statement, sufficient for our needs, is a multivariate mean-value theorem for simple eigenvalues.

\begin{lemma}[First-order perturbation for simple eigenvalues]\label{lem:first-order-perturbation}
Let \(\mat{A}\) be a real symmetric \(p \times p\) matrix with a simple eigenvalue \(\lambda\) and unit eigenvector \(\vect{u}\). For any real symmetric \(\mat{H}\) with sufficiently small \(\norm{\mat{H}}\), the eigenvalue \(\lambda(\mat{A} + \mat{H})\) of \(\mat{A} + \mat{H}\) closest to \(\lambda\) satisfies
\[
  \lambda(\mat{A} + \mat{H}) \;=\; \lambda \;+\; \vect{u}\transpose \mat{H}\, \vect{u} \;+\; O(\norm{\mat{H}}^2).
\]
\end{lemma}

A self-contained proof requires basic implicit-function-theorem machinery; we give a direct \(2 \times 2\) verification below, and refer to \citet[][Lem.~B.10]{AbryBoniecedidierWendt2024} for the general statement.

\begin{example}[Verification in the \(2 \times 2\) case]\label{ex:perturbation-2x2}
Take \(\mat{A} = \diag(1, 2)\), so the top eigenvalue is \(\lambda = 2\) with eigenvector \(\vect{u} = \vect{e}_2 = (0, 1)\transpose\). For a symmetric perturbation \(\mat{H} = \begin{pmatrix} h_{11} & h_{12} \\ h_{12} & h_{22} \end{pmatrix}\), the \(2 \times 2\) eigenvalue formula~\eqref{eq:2x2-eigenvalues} applied to \(\mat{A} + \mat{H}\) gives
\[
  \lambda_+(\mat{A} + \mat{H}) \;=\; \frac{(1 + h_{11}) + (2 + h_{22})}{2} \;+\; \frac{1}{2}\sqrt{(1 + h_{11} - 2 - h_{22})^2 + 4 h_{12}^2}.
\]
For small \(\mat{H}\), Taylor-expanding the square root,
\[
  \sqrt{(-1 + h_{11} - h_{22})^2 + 4 h_{12}^2} \;=\; \sqrt{1 + 2(h_{22} - h_{11}) + O(\norm{\mat{H}}^2)} \;=\; 1 + (h_{22} - h_{11}) + O(\norm{\mat{H}}^2).
\]
Substituting back,
\[
  \lambda_+(\mat{A} + \mat{H}) \;=\; \frac{3 + h_{11} + h_{22}}{2} \;+\; \frac{1 + h_{22} - h_{11}}{2} \;+\; O(\norm{\mat{H}}^2) \;=\; 2 \;+\; h_{22} \;+\; O(\norm{\mat{H}}^2).
\]
This is exactly Lemma~\ref{lem:first-order-perturbation} with \(\vect{u}\transpose \mat{H} \vect{u} = \vect{e}_2\transpose \mat{H} \vect{e}_2 = h_{22}\).
\end{example}

\paragraph{Top-eigenvalue fluctuations: repeated vs.\ distinct.}
With Proposition~\ref{prop:matrix-clt-I2} and Lemma~\ref{lem:first-order-perturbation} in hand, the dichotomy between repeated and distinct population eigenvalues is straightforward.

\smallskip
\noindent\emph{Repeated case: \(\Sigma = I_2\).} Lemma~\ref{lem:first-order-perturbation} does \emph{not} apply, because both eigenvalues equal \(1\). Instead, the top-eigenvalue map \(\mat{A} \mapsto \lambda_{\max}(\mat{A})\) is a continuous function from symmetric matrices to \(\R\) (this is the eigenvalue-continuity statement of Proposition~\ref{prop:eigen-convergence}(1)). By the continuous mapping theorem (Proposition~\ref{prop:cmt}), applied to Proposition~\ref{prop:matrix-clt-I2},
\[
  \sqrt{n}\,(\lambda_{\max}(\Sigmahat_n) - 1) \;\convd\; \lambda_{\max}(\mat{G}),
\]
where \(\mat{G} \sim \GOE_2\). The right side is the top eigenvalue of a \(\GOE_2\) matrix, which is \emph{not} Gaussian; it has the slightly right-skewed density shaped by repulsion.

\smallskip
\noindent\emph{Distinct case: \(\Sigma = \diag(1, 2)\).} The population eigenvalues are simple. Lemma~\ref{lem:first-order-perturbation} applies with \(\vect{u} = \vect{e}_2\), giving
\[
  \lambda_2(\Sigmahat_n) - 2 \;=\; (\Sigmahat_n)_{22} - 2 \;+\; O_p(\norm{\Sigmahat_n - \Sigma}^2).
\]
Multiplying by \(\sqrt{n}\) and using \((\Sigmahat_n - \Sigma)_{22} = O_p(n^{-1/2})\) (so the quadratic remainder is \(O_p(n^{-1/2})\) and vanishes in the limit),
\begin{equation}
  \sqrt{n}\,(\lambda_2(\Sigmahat_n) - 2) \;=\; \sqrt{n}\,((\Sigmahat_n)_{22} - 2) \;+\; o_p(1).
  \label{eq:distinct-perturbation}
\end{equation}
The entry \((\Sigmahat_n)_{22} = n^{-1} \sum_i X_{i2}^2\) with \(X_{i2} \sim \Normal{0, 2}\) has, by the univariate CLT, \(\sqrt{n}\,((\Sigmahat_n)_{22} - 2) \convd \Normal{0,\, \Var(X_{i2}^2)}\), and
\[
  \Var(X_{i2}^2) \;=\; \E[X_{i2}^4] \;-\; 4 \;=\; 12 \;-\; 4 \;=\; 8,
\]
using \(\E[Z^4] = 3 \sigma^4 = 3 \cdot 4 = 12\) for \(Z \sim \Normal{0, 2}\). Combining with~\eqref{eq:distinct-perturbation},
\begin{equation}
  \sqrt{n}\,(\lambda_2(\Sigmahat_n) - 2) \;\convd\; \Normal{0,\, 8}.
  \label{eq:distinct-eigen-clt}
\end{equation}
The limit is now an ordinary Gaussian. An identical argument applied to the smaller eigenvalue gives \(\sqrt{n}\,(\lambda_1(\Sigmahat_n) - 1) \convd \Normal{0, 2}\), and the two limits are independent because the relevant entries of \(\Sigmahat_n\) are uncorrelated.

Figure~\ref{fig:lowdim-qq} confirms this dichotomy with normal Q-Q plots from a Monte Carlo simulation.

\begin{figure}[h]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{figures/low_dim_qq_plots.png}
  \caption{Normal Q-Q plots for the standardized top sample eigenvalue \(\sqrt{n}\,(\lambda_{\max}(\Sigmahat_n) - \lambda_{\max}(\Sigma))\), 5000 Monte Carlo trials at \(n = 500\). \emph{Left:} \(\Sigma = I_2\) (repeated population eigenvalues). The visible curvature away from the dashed reference line, especially at the right tail, is the signature of the non-Gaussian \(\GOE_2\) top-eigenvalue limit. \emph{Right:} \(\Sigma = \diag(1, 2)\) (distinct population eigenvalues). \paragraph{Eigenvector convergence.} A parallel dichotomy applies to eigenvectors. In the distinct case \(\Sigma = \diag(1, 2)\), the population eigenvectors are \(\vect{e}_1\) and \(\vect{e}_2\), uniquely determined up to sign. The same first-order perturbation that produced~\eqref{eq:distinct-perturbation} also produces, after a sign convention \(\inner{\widehat{\vect{u}}_\ell}{\vect{e}_\ell} \ge 0\),
\[
  \widehat{\vect{u}}_\ell \;\convas\; \vect{e}_\ell \qquad (\ell = 1, 2).
\]
The general statement is in \citet[][Lem.~B.2]{DidierPipiras2012}.

In the repeated case \(\Sigma = I_2\) there is no canonical limiting eigenbasis: every direction in \(\R^2\) is a population eigenvector. The sample eigenvectors do not converge, even though the sample matrix itself does, as the following deterministic counterexample makes vivid.

\begin{example}[Sample eigenvectors can fail to converge under repeated eigenvalues]\label{ex:eigenvec-fail}
Define a sequence of \(2 \times 2\) symmetric matrices
\[
  \mat{S}_n \;=\; \mat{O}_n\, \begin{pmatrix} 1 + \tfrac{1}{n} & 0 \\ 0 & 1 - \tfrac{1}{n} \end{pmatrix}\, \mat{O}_n\transpose,
  \qquad
  \mat{O}_n \;=\; \begin{pmatrix} \cos\theta_n & -\sin\theta_n \\ \sin\theta_n & \cos\theta_n \end{pmatrix}, \quad \theta_n = \frac{n \pi}{2}.
\]
Then \(\mat{S}_n \to I_2\) in operator norm because \(\norm{\mat{S}_n - I_2}_{\mathrm{op}} = 1/n \to 0\). The eigenvectors of \(\mat{S}_n\), however, are the columns of \(\mat{O}_n\); they rotate by \(\pi/2\) at every step and never settle. The matrix converges; its eigenvectors do not.
\end{example}

The lesson of Example~\ref{ex:eigenvec-fail} is that eigenvectors require \emph{spectral separation}: without a gap between population eigenvalues, the eigenvectors are not stable under perturbation. This theme returns in Section~\ref{sec:spiked}, where only above a critical signal level do sample eigenvectors carry reliable information about the population spike direction.

\subsection{High Dimensions: Marchenko-Pastur Law and the Tracy-Widom Edge}\label{subsec:scm-highdim}

The picture of Section~\ref{subsec:scm-lowdim} breaks down qualitatively when \(p\) is allowed to grow with \(n\). The basic phenomenon was already visible in Figure~\ref{fig:intro-regimes} of the introduction: with \(p = 500\) and \(n = 1000\), the sample eigenvalues spread across the interval \([0.086, 2.914]\) even when \(\Sigma = I_p\). They no longer converge to \(\lambda_i(\Sigma) = 1\). The relevant observable is no longer an individual eigenvalue but the entire eigenvalue distribution, and the natural framework is the \emph{empirical spectral distribution}.

The setup throughout is the \vocab{high-dimensional regime}: \(p, n \to \infty\) with
\[
  \frac{p}{n} \;\longrightarrow\; y \;\in\; (0, \infty).
\]
The ratio \(y\) is the \emph{aspect ratio} of the data.

\begin{definition}[Empirical spectral distribution]\label{def:esd}
The \emph{empirical spectral distribution} (ESD) of a \(p \times p\) symmetric matrix \(A\) with eigenvalues \(\lambda_1, \ldots, \lambda_p\) is the probability measure
\[
  F_A \;=\; \frac{1}{p} \sum_{i = 1}^p \delta_{\lambda_i},
\]
placing equal mass \(1/p\) at each eigenvalue.
\end{definition}

\paragraph{The Marchenko-Pastur law.} The fundamental high-dimensional fact is that the ESD of \(\Sigmahat_n\) has a non-trivial limit, even in the null case \(\Sigma = I_p\). Figure~\ref{fig:mp-bulk} shows this empirically: the histogram of all \(p = 500\) eigenvalues from a single realization closely matches a specific theoretical density on the interval \([(1 - \sqrt{y})^2, (1 + \sqrt{y})^2]\).

\begin{figure}[h]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.85\textwidth]{figures/mp_bulk_with_density.png}
  \caption{The ESD of \(\Sigmahat_n = (1/n)\, \mat{X} \mat{X}\transpose\) with iid Gaussian \(\mat{X}\) at \(p = 500\), \(n = 1000\) (so \(y = 1/2\)). The histogram of all 500 eigenvalues from one realization closely matches the Marchenko-Pastur density~\eqref{eq:mp-density} (solid curve). The dotted lines mark the support edges \(\lambda_\pm = (1 \pm \sqrt{y})^2\), with \(\lambda_- \approx 0.086\) and \(\lambda_+ \approx 2.914\). Generated by \texttt{notebooks/03\_section3\_demos.py}.}
  \label{fig:mp-bulk}
\end{figure}

The theoretical statement is the Marchenko-Pastur law.

\begin{theorem}[Marchenko-Pastur law for the null covariance \citep{MarchenkoPastur1967}]\label{thm:mp}
Let \(\mat{X} \in \R^{p \times n}\) be a Gaussian matrix with iid \(\Normal{0, 1}\) entries, and let \(\Sigmahat_n = (1/n)\, \mat{X} \mat{X}\transpose\). As \(p, n \to \infty\) with \(p / n \to y \in (0, \infty)\), the ESD \(F_{\Sigmahat_n}\) converges weakly almost surely to the \vocab{Marchenko-Pastur law} \(\MP_y\), the probability distribution on \([0, \infty)\) with density
\begin{equation}
  f_{\MP_y}(\lambda) \;=\; \frac{1}{2 \pi y \lambda}\, \sqrt{(\lambda_+ - \lambda)(\lambda - \lambda_-)}, \qquad \lambda \in [\lambda_-, \lambda_+],
  \label{eq:mp-density}
\end{equation}
where \(\lambda_\pm = (1 \pm \sqrt{y})^2\). If \(y > 1\), the law has an additional atom at zero of mass \(1 - 1/y\).
\end{theorem}

A complete proof passes through the Stieltjes transform of \(F_{\Sigmahat_n}\) and is beyond the scope of this report; see \citet[][Thm.~2.5]{YaoZhengBai2015} and \citet[][Thm.~3.6]{Vershynin2018}. We use the result as a black box.

Three features of the MP law play a role in later sections, and each is visible in Figure~\ref{fig:mp-bulk}.
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*,itemsep=0.3em]
  \item The support is the interval \([(1 - \sqrt{y})^2, (1 + \sqrt{y})^2]\), of width \(4 \sqrt{y}\). As \(y \to 0\) the support collapses to the point \(\lambda = 1\), recovering the low-dimensional consistency of Section~\ref{subsec:scm-lowdim}.
  \item Near the upper edge \(\lambda_+\), the density vanishes like a square root: \(f_{\MP_y}(\lambda) \sim C(\lambda_+ - \lambda)^{1/2}\). This square-root edge is the input to the Tracy-Widom heuristic below.
  \item When \(y > 1\) the atom at zero has mass \(1 - 1/y\); the matrix \(\mat{X} \mat{X}\transpose\) has rank at most \(n < p\), so \(p - n\) of its eigenvalues are exactly zero.
\end{itemize}

The largest sample eigenvalue exits the bulk at the upper edge.

\begin{theorem}[Convergence of the top eigenvalue \citep{BaikSilverstein2006}]\label{thm:lambda-max-edge}
Under the hypotheses of Theorem~\ref{thm:mp},
\[
  \lambda_{\max}(\Sigmahat_n) \;\convas\; \lambda_+ \;=\; (1 + \sqrt{y})^2.
\]
\end{theorem}

\paragraph{The Tracy-Widom edge.} Theorem~\ref{thm:lambda-max-edge} settles where \(\lambda_{\max}(\Sigmahat_n)\) lives in the limit but says nothing about the fluctuations. Those fluctuations are not Gaussian, and the natural scale is not \(\sqrt{n}\) but \(p^{-2/3}\). Before stating the precise theorem, we explain the scale via a short heuristic that uses only the square-root edge of the MP density.

\smallskip
\noindent\emph{Heuristic for the \(p^{-2/3}\) edge scale.} Near the upper edge,
\[
  f_{\MP_y}(\lambda) \;\sim\; C\,(\lambda_+ - \lambda)^{1/2} \qquad (\lambda \uparrow \lambda_+).
\]
The largest sample eigenvalue is the rightmost of \(p\) draws from this density. Heuristically, the mass to the right of \(\lambda_{\max}\) should be of order \(1/p\):
\[
  \frac{1}{p} \;\sim\; \int_{\lambda_{\max}}^{\lambda_+} f_{\MP_y}(\lambda)\, d\lambda \;\sim\; C \int_{\lambda_{\max}}^{\lambda_+} (\lambda_+ - \lambda)^{1/2}\, d\lambda \;=\; \tfrac{2 C}{3}\, (\lambda_+ - \lambda_{\max})^{3/2}.
\]
Solving for the gap,
\[
  \lambda_+ - \lambda_{\max} \;\sim\; p^{-2/3}.
\]
The exponent \(3/2\) in the integrated square-root density inverts to the exponent \(2/3\) in the fluctuation scale. The same exponent arises in several other random matrix problems; the family of limit laws it generates is the \emph{Tracy-Widom universality class}.

\smallskip
The precise theorem comes with explicit centering and scaling constants from \citet{Johnstone2001}.

\begin{theorem}[Tracy-Widom edge for the null sample covariance matrix \citep{Johnstone2001}]\label{thm:tw}
Under the hypotheses of Theorem~\ref{thm:mp}, define
\begin{equation}
  \mu_{n p} \;=\; \frac{(\sqrt{n - 1} + \sqrt{p})^2}{n}, \qquad
  \sigma_{n p} \;=\; \frac{\sqrt{n - 1} + \sqrt{p}}{n}\, \left(\frac{1}{\sqrt{n - 1}} + \frac{1}{\sqrt{p}}\right)^{1/3}.
  \label{eq:tw-constants}
\end{equation}
Then
\[
  \frac{\lambda_{\max}(\Sigmahat_n) - \mu_{n p}}{\sigma_{n p}} \;\convd\; \mathrm{TW}_1,
\]
where \(\mathrm{TW}_1\) is the (real) Tracy-Widom distribution.
\end{theorem}

The Tracy-Widom \(\mathrm{TW}_1\) law was first identified by \citet{TracyWidom1996} as the limit of the largest eigenvalue of the GOE; it has approximate mean \(-1.21\) and standard deviation \(1.27\), and is asymmetric, with a heavier left tail. The centering \(\mu_{n p}\) and scaling \(\sigma_{n p}\) converge to \((1 + \sqrt{y})^2\) and an \(O(p^{-2/3})\) quantity respectively, matching the heuristic above. Figure~\ref{fig:tw-edge} shows the convergence in simulation.

\paragraph{The picture so far.} Combining Sections~\ref{subsec:scm-lowdim} and~\ref{subsec:scm-highdim}, the two regimes look very different.
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*,itemsep=0.3em]
  \item \emph{Low dimensions.} Sample eigenvalues are consistent estimators of population eigenvalues. Fluctuations are Gaussian on the \(\sqrt{n}\) scale at a simple eigenvalue (via Lemma~\ref{lem:first-order-perturbation}) and \(\GOE\)-shaped at a repeated one (via the continuous mapping theorem applied to Proposition~\ref{prop:matrix-clt-I2}).
  \item \emph{High dimensions, null covariance.} The entire empirical spectrum is described by the Marchenko-Pastur bulk on \([\lambda_-, \lambda_+]\), and the top eigenvalue sits at the right edge \(\lambda_+\) with Tracy-Widom \(\mathrm{TW}_1\) fluctuations on the \(p^{-2/3}\) scale.
\end{itemize}

This is the \emph{null} picture: \(\Sigma = I_p\) carries no structure. The remaining question is what happens when \(\Sigma\) does carry structure, in particular when a small number of eigenvalues stand above an otherwise flat unit baseline. That setting is the spiked covariance model of \citet{Johnstone2001}, and it is the subject of Section~\ref{sec:spiked}.

\section{Sample Covariance Matrices}\label{sec:scm}


The covariance matrix \(\Sigma\) describes the true variability of a random vector, but in data analysis it is unknown. Given observations \(\vect{X}_1, \ldots, \vect{X}_n\), the natural replacement is the empirical average of the outer products \(\vect{X}_i \vect{X}_i\transpose\). In fixed dimensions, this empirical version behaves like a classical sample average: as the number of observations grows, it converges entry by entry to the true covariance, exactly as a scalar sample mean converges to its expectation.\gdmark{meaning} When the dimension grows with the sample size (the so-called \emph{high-dimensional regime}\gdmark{hdr-name}), its spectral behavior changes qualitatively, and both regimes are unpacked below.

%====================================================================
\subsection{Definition and Geometry of the Sample Covariance Matrix}\label{subsec:scm-foundations}

\begin{definition}[Sample covariance matrix (SCM)]\label{def:scm}
Let \(\vect{X}_1, \ldots, \vect{X}_n\) be iid copies of a random vector \(\vect{X} \in \R^p\). In the \emph{mean-zero} case (\(\E[\vect{X}] = \vect{0}\) known), the \vocab{sample covariance matrix} is the \(p \times p\) random matrix\gdmark{scm-abbr}
\begin{equation}
  \Sigmahat_n \;=\; \frac{1}{n} \sum_{i = 1}^n \vect{X}_i \vect{X}_i\transpose.
  \label{eq:scm-outer}
\end{equation}
In the \emph{mean-unknown} case, the (unbiased) sample covariance matrix is
\[
  \Sigmahat_n \;=\; \frac{1}{n - 1} \sum_{i = 1}^n (\vect{X}_i - \bar{\vect{X}}_n)(\vect{X}_i - \bar{\vect{X}}_n)\transpose, \qquad \bar{\vect{X}}_n \;=\; \frac{1}{n} \sum_{i = 1}^n \vect{X}_i.
\]
\end{definition}

We work in the mean-zero case throughout the rest of the report; the Gaussian models in Sections~\ref{sec:scm} and~\ref{sec:spiked} all have \(\vect{\mu} = \vect{0}\) by convention, and the matrix algebra is cleaner. The asymptotic results below carry over to the mean-unknown version with no substantive change.

Note that each entry of a SCM is a sample average.\gdmark{each-entry} In fact, writing \(\vect{X}_i = (X_{i1}, \ldots, X_{ip})\transpose\), the \((j, k)\) entry of \(\Sigmahat_n\) is
\begin{equation}
  (\Sigmahat_n)_{jk} \;=\; \frac{1}{n} \sum_{i = 1}^n X_{ij}\, X_{ik}, \qquad j, k = 1, \ldots, p.
  \label{eq:scm-entrywise}
\end{equation}
Each entry of~\eqref{eq:scm-entrywise}\gdmark{eqref} is therefore the sample average of the iid scalar products \(W_i^{(j,k)} = X_{ij} X_{ik}\), whose common mean (since \(\vect{X}_1\) has mean zero) is exactly
\[
  \E\!\bigl[X_{1j}\, X_{1k}\bigr] \;=\; \Cov(X_j, X_k) \;=\; \Sigma_{jk}.
\]
By the strong law of large numbers (Theorem~\ref{thm:slln}), every entry converges almost surely to its population counterpart, \((\Sigmahat_n)_{jk} \convas \Sigma_{jk}\) as \(n \to \infty\). This entrywise LLN is the mechanism behind the matrix-level convergence proved in Section~\ref{subsec:scm-lowdim}. The diagonal entries \((\Sigmahat_n)_{jj} = n^{-1} \sum_i X_{ij}^2\) are sample variances of individual coordinates; the off-diagonal entries are sample covariances between pairs of coordinates.

As it turns out, the entrywise formula~\eqref{eq:scm-entrywise} can be conveniently repackaged as a single matrix identity.\gdmark{matrix-form} Stack the \(n\) observations as the columns of the \emph{data matrix}
\[
  \mat{X} \;=\; (\vect{X}_1 \mid \vect{X}_2 \mid \cdots \mid \vect{X}_n) \;\in\; \R^{p \times n}.
\]
This is a \(p \times n\) matrix in which the rows correspond to features (the coordinates of \(\vect{X}\)) and the columns correspond to observations: row \(j\) holds the values \(X_{1j}, X_{2j}, \ldots, X_{nj}\) of the \(j\)\textsuperscript{th} feature across all \(n\) observations. The product \(\mat{X} \mat{X}\transpose\) is then a \(p \times p\) matrix whose \((j, k)\) entry, by the usual rules of matrix multiplication, is the dot product of the \(j\)\textsuperscript{th} and \(k\)\textsuperscript{th} feature rows of \(\mat{X}\):
\[
  (\mat{X} \mat{X}\transpose)_{jk} \;=\; \sum_{i = 1}^n X_{ij}\, X_{ik}.
\]
Dividing by \(n\) recovers equation~\eqref{eq:scm-entrywise}, so we obtain the compact form
\begin{equation}
  \Sigmahat_n \;=\; \frac{1}{n}\, \mat{X} \mat{X}\transpose.
  \label{eq:scm-matrix-form}
\end{equation}
This is the working form of the sample covariance matrix used throughout the rest of the report. Relation~\eqref{eq:scm-matrix-form} is very useful in establishing the following proposition.\gdmark{psd-leadin}
\begin{proposition}[\(\Sigmahat_n\) is symmetric and PSD]\label{prop:scm-psd}
The sample covariance matrix \(\Sigmahat_n\) is symmetric and positive semidefinite. All of its eigenvalues are therefore nonnegative (Theorem~\ref{thm:pd-psd-eigen}).
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
For the mean-zero version~\eqref{eq:scm-matrix-form}, symmetry is immediate: \((\mat{X} \mat{X}\transpose)\transpose = (\mat{X}\transpose)\transpose \mat{X}\transpose = \mat{X} \mat{X}\transpose\). For positive semidefiniteness, fix \(\vect{v} \in \R^p\):
\begin{equation}
  \vect{v}\transpose \Sigmahat_n \vect{v}
  \;=\; \frac{1}{n}\, \vect{v}\transpose \mat{X} \mat{X}\transpose \vect{v}
  \;=\; \frac{1}{n}\, (\mat{X}\transpose \vect{v})\transpose (\mat{X}\transpose \vect{v})
  \;=\; \frac{1}{n}\, \norm{\mat{X}\transpose \vect{v}}_2^2
  \;\ge\; 0.
  \label{eq:scm-psd-identity}
\end{equation}
The mean-unknown version uses the centered data matrix in place of \(\mat{X}\); the same identity gives \(\vect{v}\transpose \Sigmahat_n \vect{v} = (n - 1)\inv \norm{\mat{X}_c\transpose \vect{v}}_2^2 \ge 0\).
\end{proof}

The identity~\eqref{eq:scm-psd-identity} has a clean statistical reading. The vector \(\mat{X}\transpose \vect{v} \in \R^n\) has \(i\)\textsuperscript{th} entry \(\vect{v}\transpose \vect{X}_i\), the projection of the \(i\)\textsuperscript{th} observation onto the direction \(\vect{v}\); dividing the squared norm of these projections by \(n\) gives the empirical second moment of the scalar sample \(\vect{v}\transpose \vect{X}_1, \ldots, \vect{v}\transpose \vect{X}_n\). In the mean-zero case this is exactly the empirical variance of the data in the direction \(\vect{v}\):
\begin{equation}
  \vect{v}\transpose \Sigmahat_n \vect{v} \;=\; \frac{1}{n} \sum_{i = 1}^n (\vect{v}\transpose \vect{X}_i)^2.
  \label{eq:scm-empirical-var}
\end{equation}
Proposition~\ref{prop:scm-psd} therefore reads, in plain English, as the statement that the empirical variance in every direction is nonnegative. This is the sample analogue of the identity \(\vect{v}\transpose \Sigma \vect{v} = \Var(\vect{v}\transpose \vect{X})\) from Proposition~\ref{prop:cov-psd}.

\paragraph{Covariance matrix as geometry.} Among all unit directions \(\vect{v} \in \R^p\), some have larger empirical variance than others. The spectral theorem (Theorem~\ref{thm:spectral}) factors \(\Sigmahat_n = \mat{Q} \mat{\Lambda} \mat{Q}\transpose\) with \(\mat{Q}\) orthogonal and \(\mat{\Lambda} = \diag(\lambda_1, \ldots, \lambda_p)\), and the Rayleigh quotient (Proposition~\ref{prop:rayleigh}) identifies the special directions that diagonalize the directional variance: the maximum of \(\vect{v}\transpose \Sigmahat_n \vect{v}\) over unit \(\vect{v}\) is the largest eigenvalue \(\lambda_1\), attained on the top eigenvector \(\vect{q}_1\). The eigenvectors of \(\Sigmahat_n\) are therefore the perpendicular \emph{principal directions} of variation in the data, and the eigenvalues record the empirical variance in each principal direction. Figure~\ref{fig:scm-geom} shows the population-level picture in two dimensions, with the covariance ellipse and the eigenvector directions overlaid on a data cloud.

  \caption{Geometry of the covariance matrix. The figure shows \(n = 500\) iid samples from \(\Normal{\vect{0}, \Sigma}\) with \(\Sigma = \begin{pmatrix} 4 & 1.5 \\ 1.5 & 1 \end{pmatrix}\). In fixed dimension, this matrix behaves like an ordinary average and its eigenstructure tracks that of \(\Sigma\). When the dimension grows with the sample size, the eigenvalues spread away from the population eigenvalues and need separate analysis.

\subsection{Low Dimensions: Consistency, Wishart, and Fluctuations}\label{subsec:scm-lowdim}

In the low-dimensional regime the dimension \(p\) is fixed and the sample size \(n \to \infty\). This is the classical setting of multivariate statistics, in which the limit theorems of Section~\ref{sec:foundations} apply directly to the entries of \(\Sigmahat_n\). We unpack four nested questions: (i) does \(\Sigmahat_n\) converge to \(\Sigma\), (ii) what is the exact distribution of \(\Sigmahat_n\) for Gaussian data, (iii) what is the limiting distribution of the fluctuation \(\sqrt{n}(\Sigmahat_n - \Sigma)\), and (iv) what does that say about individual eigenvalues and eigenvectors of \(\Sigmahat_n\)?

\paragraph{Consistency of the matrix and its eigenvalues.} The first question has the cleanest answer. Each entry of \(\Sigmahat_n\) is a sample average of iid scalar products with mean \(\Sigma_{jk}\), so the strong law of large numbers (Theorem~\ref{thm:slln}) gives entrywise convergence at once. The intro figure (Figure~\ref{fig:intro-regimes}, left panel) already shows the spectral consequence: with \(p = 10\) and \(n = 1000\), the sample eigenvalues of \(\Sigmahat_n\) concentrate tightly around the population value \(1\).

\begin{theorem}[Entrywise consistency of \(\Sigmahat_n\)]\label{thm:scm-consistency}
Let \(\vect{X}_1, \vect{X}_2, \ldots\) be iid copies of \(\vect{X} \in \R^p\) with mean \(\vect{0}\) and finite second moments \(\E[X_j X_k] < \infty\). Then
\[
  \Sigmahat_n \;\convas\; \Sigma \qquad \text{entrywise, as } n \to \infty.
\]
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
The \((j, k)\) entry of \(\Sigmahat_n\) is
\[
  (\Sigmahat_n)_{jk} \;=\; \frac{1}{n} \sum_{i = 1}^n X_{ij}\, X_{ik}.
\]
The random variables \(\{X_{ij} X_{ik}\}_{i \ge 1}\) are iid with mean
\[
  \E[X_{1j} X_{1k}] \;=\; \Sigma_{jk}
\]
(using \(\vect{\mu} = \vect{0}\)) and finite first moment by hypothesis. Kolmogorov's strong law of large numbers (Theorem~\ref{thm:slln}) applied to this sequence yields
\[
  (\Sigmahat_n)_{jk} \;\convas\; \Sigma_{jk}.
\]
Doing this for each pair \((j, k)\) gives entrywise almost-sure convergence \(\Sigmahat_n \convas \Sigma\).
\end{proof}

Combined with the eigenvalue-convergence machinery developed in Section~\ref{subsec:linalg}, entrywise convergence forces convergence of the eigenvalues themselves.

\begin{corollary}[Sample eigenvalues converge to population eigenvalues]\label{cor:scm-eigen-convergence}
Under the hypotheses of Theorem~\ref{thm:scm-consistency}, \(\lambda_i(\Sigmahat_n) \convas \lambda_i(\Sigma)\) for every \(i = 1, \ldots, p\).
\end{corollary}

\begin{proof}
Apply Proposition~\ref{prop:eigen-convergence}(2) (the almost-sure version of eigenvalue convergence) to the random matrix sequence \(\Sigmahat_n \convas \Sigma\).
\end{proof}

Theorem~\ref{thm:scm-consistency} and Corollary~\ref{cor:scm-eigen-convergence} together give the qualitative low-dimensional picture: \(\Sigmahat_n\) is a consistent estimator of \(\Sigma\), and the sample eigenvalues track the population eigenvalues. The remaining questions concern the size and shape of the fluctuations.

\paragraph{Gaussian sample covariance and the Wishart law.} For Gaussian observations, \(\Sigmahat_n\) (more precisely, \(n \Sigmahat_n\)) has an exact distribution. This makes it a tractable starting point for finer questions than entrywise consistency.

\begin{definition}[Wishart distribution]\label{def:wishart}
Let \(\vect{X}_1, \ldots, \vect{X}_n\) be iid \(\Normal{\vect{0}, \Sigma}\) random vectors in \(\R^p\), and set
\[
  \mat{S} \;=\; \sum_{i = 1}^n \vect{X}_i \vect{X}_i\transpose \;=\; n\, \Sigmahat_n.
\]
The distribution of \(\mat{S}\) is the \vocab{Wishart distribution} with \(n\) degrees of freedom and scale matrix \(\Sigma\), written \(\mat{S} \sim \Wishart{p}{n, \Sigma}\). When \(n \ge p\) and \(\Sigma\) is positive definite, \(\mat{S}\) admits the density (on the cone of positive-definite matrices)
\begin{equation}
  f_{\mat{S}}(\mat{S}) \;=\; \frac{\abs{\mat{S}}^{(n - p - 1)/2}\, \exp\!\bigl(-\tfrac{1}{2}\, \tr(\Sigma\inv \mat{S})\bigr)}{2^{n p / 2}\, \abs{\Sigma}^{n/2}\, \Gamma_p(n / 2)},
  \label{eq:wishart-density}
\end{equation}
where \(\Gamma_p\) is the multivariate gamma function; see \citet[][\S 3.2]{YaoZhengBai2015} for a derivation.
\end{definition}

The Wishart distribution is the multivariate analogue of the chi-square distribution, and recovers it in dimension one.

\begin{example}[Wishart in dimension one]\label{ex:wishart-p1}
Take \(p = 1\) and \(\Sigma = 1\). Then each \(\vect{X}_i\) is a scalar \(X_i \sim \Normal{0, 1}\), so \(\mat{S} = \sum_{i = 1}^n X_i^2\). By the definition of the chi-square distribution, \(\mat{S} \sim \chi_n^2\). Substituting \(p = 1\), \(\Sigma = 1\), and \(\tr(\Sigma\inv \mat{S}) = \mat{S}\) into~\eqref{eq:wishart-density} confirms this:
\[
  f_{\mat{S}}(s) \;=\; \frac{s^{(n - 2)/2}\, e^{-s/2}}{2^{n/2}\, \Gamma(n/2)}, \qquad s > 0,
\]
which is exactly the \(\chi_n^2\) density.
\end{example}

For \(\Sigma = I_2\), the joint distribution of the two ordered eigenvalues \(0 \le \lambda_1 \le \lambda_2\) of \(\mat{S} \sim \Wishart{2}{n, I_2}\) admits an explicit density that reveals a striking phenomenon, namely \emph{eigenvalue repulsion}:
\begin{equation}
  f(\lambda_1, \lambda_2) \;=\; C\, (\lambda_1 \lambda_2)^{(n - 3)/2}\, e^{-(\lambda_1 + \lambda_2) / 2}\, (\lambda_2 - \lambda_1)\, \indic_{\{0 \le \lambda_1 \le \lambda_2\}},
  \label{eq:wishart-2d-density}
\end{equation}
where \(C > 0\) is a normalizing constant; a derivation via a Jacobian computation on the spectral decomposition \(\mat{S} = \mat{Q} \diag(\lambda_1, \lambda_2) \mat{Q}\transpose\) is given in \citet[][Ch.~3]{YaoZhengBai2015}. The key feature of~\eqref{eq:wishart-2d-density} is the factor \((\lambda_2 - \lambda_1)\), which forces the joint density to vanish on the diagonal \(\lambda_1 = \lambda_2\). This is the simplest case of \vocab{eigenvalue repulsion}: the two eigenvalues of a random symmetric matrix avoid each other. Because the density is zero when \(\lambda_1 = \lambda_2\) and small when they are close, an outcome with the two eigenvalues nearly equal is very unlikely, so in repeated draws the pair tends to stay apart. Figure~\ref{fig:wishart-2d} confirms repulsion in simulation.

\begin{figure}[H]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{figures/wishart_2d_eigenvalues.png}
  \caption{Eigenvalues of \(\Wishart{2}{100, I_2}\) computed from \(5000\) independent Monte Carlo trials. \emph{Left:} scatter plot of \((\lambda_1, \lambda_2)\) with the line \(\lambda_2 = \lambda_1\) shown for reference. The cloud is visibly bounded away from the diagonal, the geometric signature of the \((\lambda_2 - \lambda_1)\) factor in~\eqref{eq:wishart-2d-density}. \emph{Right:} histogram of the gap \(\lambda_2 - \lambda_1\); the density vanishes at zero, the empirical signature of repulsion. Generated by \texttt{notebooks/03\_section3\_demos.py}.}
  \label{fig:wishart-2d}
\end{figure}

The factor \((\lambda_2 - \lambda_1)\) in~\eqref{eq:wishart-2d-density} is the simplest instance of a structure that appears in every classical random matrix ensemble: the \emph{Vandermonde determinant}.

\begin{definition}[Vandermonde determinant]\label{def:vandermonde}
For real numbers \(\lambda_1, \ldots, \lambda_p\), the \vocab{Vandermonde determinant} is
\begin{equation}
  V(\lambda_1, \ldots, \lambda_p)
  \;=\;
  \det\!\begin{pmatrix}
    1               & 1               & \cdots & 1               \\
    \lambda_1       & \lambda_2       & \cdots & \lambda_p       \\
    \lambda_1^2     & \lambda_2^2     & \cdots & \lambda_p^2     \\
    \vdots          & \vdots          &        & \vdots          \\
    \lambda_1^{p-1} & \lambda_2^{p-1} & \cdots & \lambda_p^{p-1}
  \end{pmatrix}
  \;=\;
  \prod_{1 \le i < j \le p} (\lambda_j - \lambda_i).
  \label{eq:vandermonde}
\end{equation}
\end{definition}

The closed-form on the right is a classical identity, proved by row reduction: subtracting \(\lambda_1\) times each row from the row below introduces a factor of \((\lambda_j - \lambda_1)\) in every column \(j \ge 2\), the first column collapses, and the rest is induction on \(p\). For \(p = 2\) the product reduces to \(V = \lambda_2 - \lambda_1\); for \(p = 3\), \(V = (\lambda_2 - \lambda_1)(\lambda_3 - \lambda_1)(\lambda_3 - \lambda_2)\); for \(p = 4\) it is a product of six gaps; and so on. The Vandermonde is therefore the product of all signed gaps between the \(p\) eigenvalues.

The relevance for the Wishart distribution is geometric. To pass from the density~\eqref{eq:wishart-density} on the cone of positive-definite matrices to a density on the ordered eigenvalues, one diagonalizes \(\mat{S} = \mat{Q} \diag(\lambda_1, \ldots, \lambda_p) \mat{Q}\transpose\) and integrates out the orthogonal factor \(\mat{Q}\). The Jacobian of this change of variables turns out to be exactly \(\abs{V(\lambda_1, \ldots, \lambda_p)}\) (the calculation is in \citet[][\S~3.2]{YaoZhengBai2015}). The full joint eigenvalue density of \(\Wishart{p}{n, I_p}\) for \(n \ge p\) is therefore
\begin{equation}
  f(\lambda_1, \ldots, \lambda_p)
  \;=\;
  C_{n,p}\, \abs{V(\lambda_1, \ldots, \lambda_p)}\, \prod_{i=1}^p \lambda_i^{(n-p-1)/2}\, e^{-\lambda_i/2},
  \qquad 0 \le \lambda_1 \le \cdots \le \lambda_p,
  \label{eq:wishart-joint-density}
\end{equation}
with a normalizing constant \(C_{n,p}\) depending only on \(n\) and \(p\). Setting \(p = 2\) in~\eqref{eq:wishart-joint-density} gives \(\abs{V} = \lambda_2 - \lambda_1\), the prefactor \(\lambda_1^{(n-3)/2} \lambda_2^{(n-3)/2} = (\lambda_1 \lambda_2)^{(n-3)/2}\), and the exponential \(e^{-(\lambda_1 + \lambda_2)/2}\) — exactly~\eqref{eq:wishart-2d-density}, with the indicator collapsing into the ordering constraint.

The Vandermonde factor vanishes on every hyperplane \(\lambda_i = \lambda_j\), so configurations with near-equal eigenvalues are systematically suppressed in every dimension. This is the algebraic mechanism behind eigenvalue repulsion: the gap visible in Figure~\ref{fig:wishart-2d} for \(p = 2\) generalizes to a product of \(\binom{p}{2}\) pairwise gaps in higher dimensions, each contributing its own zero on the corresponding diagonal. The same Vandermonde factor appears in the joint eigenvalue density of the Gaussian Orthogonal Ensemble defined below; only the prefactor and the exponential change. Repulsion is a universal feature of the spectra of random symmetric matrices, not a quirk of the Wishart family.

\paragraph{Fluctuations and the role of eigenvalue multiplicity.} Beyond consistency, we want to know what \(\sqrt{n}(\Sigmahat_n - \Sigma)\) looks like. The limit is a Gaussian symmetric matrix whose variance structure has a standard name. The variances \(2\) on the diagonal and \(1\) off the diagonal appear so often in symmetric Gaussian matrix limits that they define a canonical ensemble.

\begin{definition}[Gaussian Orthogonal Ensemble, \(2 \times 2\) case]\label{def:goe}
A \(2 \times 2\) random matrix
\[
  \mat{G} \;=\; \begin{pmatrix} G_{11} & G_{12} \\ G_{12} & G_{22} \end{pmatrix}
\]
is said to belong to the \vocab{Gaussian Orthogonal Ensemble} (\(\GOE_2\)) if its three independent entries are Gaussian with
\[
  G_{11},\, G_{22} \;\sim\; \Normal{0,\, 2}, \qquad G_{12} \;\sim\; \Normal{0,\, 1},
\]
and the three entries are mutually independent. More generally, a \(p \times p\) GOE matrix has independent Gaussian entries on and above the diagonal with diagonal variance \(2\) and off-diagonal variance \(1\).
\end{definition}

The variances \(2\) on the diagonal and \(1\) off the diagonal are chosen exactly so that the matrix looks statistically the same in every orthonormal basis: for any orthogonal matrix \(\mat{O}\), the conjugate \(\mat{O} \mat{G} \mat{O}\transpose\) has the same distribution as \(\mat{G}\). This rotation-invariance is what the word ``orthogonal'' in the name refers to. The top eigenvalue of \(\GOE_2\) is a non-Gaussian random variable, with a density slightly right-skewed by the repulsion that mirrors~\eqref{eq:wishart-2d-density}.

The multivariate CLT applied entry-by-entry to \(\sqrt{n}(\Sigmahat_n - \Sigma)\) yields the matrix-valued statement. In the null case the limit is exactly the GOE just defined.

\begin{proposition}[Matrix-valued CLT for \(\Sigma = I_2\)]\label{prop:matrix-clt-I2}
Let \(\vect{X}_i \sim \Normal{\vect{0}, I_2}\) be iid. Then \(\sqrt{n}\,(\Sigmahat_n - I_2)\) converges in distribution, as \(n \to \infty\), to a \(\GOE_2\) matrix.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
The three independent entries of the symmetric matrix \(\sqrt{n}\,(\Sigmahat_n - I_2)\) are
\[
  \sqrt{n}\,((\Sigmahat_n)_{11} - 1), \quad \sqrt{n}\,((\Sigmahat_n)_{22} - 1), \quad \sqrt{n}\,(\Sigmahat_n)_{12},
\]
each of which is a sum of iid centered random variables, so the multivariate central limit theorem of Section~\ref{subsec:rvecs} applies. We compute the limiting variances and verify that the cross-covariances vanish.

For the diagonal entries, write \((\Sigmahat_n)_{jj} - 1 = n^{-1} \sum_i (X_{ij}^2 - 1)\). Each summand has mean zero and
\[
  \Var(X_{ij}^2) \;=\; \E[X_{ij}^4] \;-\; (\E[X_{ij}^2])^2 \;=\; 3 \;-\; 1 \;=\; 2,
\]
using \(\E[Z^4] = 3\) for \(Z \sim \Normal{0, 1}\). Hence the limiting variance of \(\sqrt{n}\,((\Sigmahat_n)_{jj} - 1)\) is \(2\).

For the off-diagonal entry, write \((\Sigmahat_n)_{12} = n^{-1} \sum_i X_{i1} X_{i2}\). Each summand has mean zero (independence) and variance
\[
  \Var(X_{i1} X_{i2}) \;=\; \E[X_{i1}^2]\, \E[X_{i2}^2] \;=\; 1,
\]
again by independence. Hence the limiting variance of \(\sqrt{n}\,(\Sigmahat_n)_{12}\) is \(1\).

The cross-covariances vanish:
\[
  \Cov(X_{i1}^2,\, X_{i1} X_{i2}) \;=\; \E[X_{i1}^3]\, \E[X_{i2}] \;=\; 0,
\]
because \(\E[X_{i2}] = 0\), and analogously for the other pairs.

The three limiting variances are therefore \(2\), \(2\), \(1\) and the three limits are uncorrelated. By the multivariate CLT, the joint limit is \(\Normal{\vect{0},\, \diag(2, 2, 1)}\), which is exactly the entrywise distribution of a \(\GOE_2\) matrix from Definition~\ref{def:goe}.
\end{proof}

Proposition~\ref{prop:matrix-clt-I2} describes the fluctuation of the \emph{matrix} \(\Sigmahat_n\), not of its individual eigenvalues. The link from matrix to eigenvalues is a first-order perturbation lemma, the multivariate mean-value theorem for simple eigenvalues.

\begin{lemma}[First-order perturbation for simple eigenvalues]\label{lem:first-order-perturbation}
Let \(\mat{A}\) be a real symmetric \(p \times p\) matrix with a simple eigenvalue \(\lambda\) and unit eigenvector \(\vect{u}\). For any real symmetric \(\mat{H}\) with sufficiently small \(\norm{\mat{H}}\), the eigenvalue \(\lambda(\mat{A} + \mat{H})\) of \(\mat{A} + \mat{H}\) closest to \(\lambda\) satisfies
\[
  \lambda(\mat{A} + \mat{H}) \;=\; \lambda \;+\; \vect{u}\transpose \mat{H}\, \vect{u} \;+\; O(\norm{\mat{H}}^2).
\]
\end{lemma}

A self-contained proof requires basic implicit-function-theorem machinery; we give a direct \(2 \times 2\) verification below, and refer to \citet[][Lem.~B.10]{AbryBoniecedidierWendt2024} for the general statement.

\begin{example}[Verification in the \(2 \times 2\) case]\label{ex:perturbation-2x2}
Take \(\mat{A} = \diag(1, 2)\), so the top eigenvalue is \(\lambda = 2\) with eigenvector \(\vect{u} = \vect{e}_2 = (0, 1)\transpose\). For a symmetric perturbation \(\mat{H} = \begin{pmatrix} h_{11} & h_{12} \\ h_{12} & h_{22} \end{pmatrix}\), the \(2 \times 2\) eigenvalue formula~\eqref{eq:2x2-eigenvalues} applied to \(\mat{A} + \mat{H}\) gives
\[
  \lambda_+(\mat{A} + \mat{H}) \;=\; \frac{(1 + h_{11}) + (2 + h_{22})}{2} \;+\; \frac{1}{2}\sqrt{(1 + h_{11} - 2 - h_{22})^2 + 4 h_{12}^2}.
\]
For small \(\mat{H}\), Taylor-expanding the square root,
\[
  \sqrt{(-1 + h_{11} - h_{22})^2 + 4 h_{12}^2} \;=\; \sqrt{1 + 2(h_{22} - h_{11}) + O(\norm{\mat{H}}^2)} \;=\; 1 + (h_{22} - h_{11}) + O(\norm{\mat{H}}^2).
\]
Substituting back,
\[
  \lambda_+(\mat{A} + \mat{H}) \;=\; \frac{3 + h_{11} + h_{22}}{2} \;+\; \frac{1 + h_{22} - h_{11}}{2} \;+\; O(\norm{\mat{H}}^2) \;=\; 2 \;+\; h_{22} \;+\; O(\norm{\mat{H}}^2).
\]
This is exactly Lemma~\ref{lem:first-order-perturbation} with \(\vect{u}\transpose \mat{H} \vect{u} = \vect{e}_2\transpose \mat{H} \vect{e}_2 = h_{22}\).
\end{example}

With Proposition~\ref{prop:matrix-clt-I2} and Lemma~\ref{lem:first-order-perturbation} in hand, the dichotomy between repeated and distinct population eigenvalues is straightforward.

\smallskip
\noindent\emph{Repeated case: \(\Sigma = I_2\).} Lemma~\ref{lem:first-order-perturbation} does \emph{not} apply, because both eigenvalues equal \(1\). Instead, the top-eigenvalue map \(\mat{A} \mapsto \lambda_{\max}(\mat{A})\) is a continuous function from symmetric matrices to \(\R\) (this is the eigenvalue-continuity statement of Proposition~\ref{prop:eigen-convergence}(1)). By the continuous mapping theorem (Proposition~\ref{prop:cmt}), applied to Proposition~\ref{prop:matrix-clt-I2},
\[
  \sqrt{n}\,(\lambda_{\max}(\Sigmahat_n) - 1) \;\convd\; \lambda_{\max}(\mat{G}),
\]
where \(\mat{G} \sim \GOE_2\). The right side is the top eigenvalue of a \(\GOE_2\) matrix, which is \emph{not} Gaussian; it has the slightly right-skewed density shaped by repulsion.

\smallskip
\noindent\emph{Distinct case: \(\Sigma = \diag(1, 2)\).} The population eigenvalues are simple. Lemma~\ref{lem:first-order-perturbation} applies with \(\vect{u} = \vect{e}_2\), giving
\[
  \lambda_2(\Sigmahat_n) - 2 \;=\; (\Sigmahat_n)_{22} - 2 \;+\; O_p(\norm{\Sigmahat_n - \Sigma}^2).
\]
Here \(O_p(a_n)\) denotes a random quantity bounded in probability by \(a_n\), and \(o_p(1)\) one that tends to \(0\) in probability; they read like the ordinary \(O\) and \(o\), but ``with high probability''.
Multiplying by \(\sqrt{n}\) and using \((\Sigmahat_n - \Sigma)_{22} = O_p(n^{-1/2})\) (so the quadratic remainder is \(O_p(n^{-1/2})\) and vanishes in the limit),
\begin{equation}
  \sqrt{n}\,(\lambda_2(\Sigmahat_n) - 2) \;=\; \sqrt{n}\,((\Sigmahat_n)_{22} - 2) \;+\; o_p(1).
  \label{eq:distinct-perturbation}
\end{equation}
The entry \((\Sigmahat_n)_{22} = n^{-1} \sum_i X_{i2}^2\) with \(X_{i2} \sim \Normal{0, 2}\) has, by the univariate CLT, \(\sqrt{n}\,((\Sigmahat_n)_{22} - 2) \convd \Normal{0,\, \Var(X_{i2}^2)}\), and
\[
  \Var(X_{i2}^2) \;=\; \E[X_{i2}^4] \;-\; 4 \;=\; 12 \;-\; 4 \;=\; 8,
\]
using \(\E[Z^4] = 3 \sigma^4 = 3 \cdot 4 = 12\) for \(Z \sim \Normal{0, 2}\). Combining with~\eqref{eq:distinct-perturbation},
\begin{equation}
  \sqrt{n}\,(\lambda_2(\Sigmahat_n) - 2) \;\convd\; \Normal{0,\, 8}.
  \label{eq:distinct-eigen-clt}
\end{equation}
The limit is now an ordinary Gaussian. An identical argument applied to the smaller eigenvalue gives \(\sqrt{n}\,(\lambda_1(\Sigmahat_n) - 1) \convd \Normal{0, 2}\), and the two limits are independent because the relevant entries of \(\Sigmahat_n\) are uncorrelated.

Figure~\ref{fig:lowdim-qq} confirms this dichotomy with normal Q-Q plots from a Monte Carlo simulation.

  \caption{Normal Q-Q plots for the standardized top sample eigenvalue \(\sqrt{n}\,(\lambda_{\max}(\Sigmahat_n) - \lambda_{\max}(\Sigma))\), 5000 Monte Carlo trials at \(n = 500\). \emph{Left:} \(\Sigma = I_2\) (repeated population eigenvalues). The visible curvature away from the dashed reference line, especially at the right tail, is the signature of the non-Gaussian \(\GOE_2\) top-eigenvalue limit. \emph{Right:} \(\Sigma = \diag(1, 2)\) (distinct population eigenvalues).   \label{fig:lowdim-qq}

\paragraph{Eigenvectors and spectral separation.} The same dichotomy applies to eigenvectors. In the distinct case \(\Sigma = \diag(1, 2)\), the population eigenvectors are \(\vect{e}_1\) and \(\vect{e}_2\), uniquely determined up to sign. The same first-order perturbation that produced~\eqref{eq:distinct-perturbation} also produces, after a sign convention \(\inner{\widehat{\vect{u}}_\ell}{\vect{e}_\ell} \ge 0\),
\[
  \widehat{\vect{u}}_\ell \;\convas\; \vect{e}_\ell \qquad (\ell = 1, 2).
\]
The general statement is in \citet[][Lem.~B.2]{DidierPipiras2012}.

In the repeated case \(\Sigma = I_2\) there is no canonical limiting eigenbasis: every direction in \(\R^2\) is a population eigenvector. The sample eigenvectors may not converge, even when the sample matrix itself does. This is illustrated in the following deterministic counterexample.\gdmark{eigvec-mayfail}

\begin{example}[Sample eigenvectors can fail to converge under repeated eigenvalues]\label{ex:eigenvec-fail}
Define a sequence of \(2 \times 2\) symmetric matrices
\[
  \mat{S}_n \;=\; \mat{O}_n\, \begin{pmatrix} 1 + \tfrac{1}{n} & 0 \\ 0 & 1 - \tfrac{1}{n} \end{pmatrix}\, \mat{O}_n\transpose,
  \qquad
  \mat{O}_n \;=\; \begin{pmatrix} \cos\theta_n & -\sin\theta_n \\ \sin\theta_n & \cos\theta_n \end{pmatrix}, \quad \theta_n = \frac{n \pi}{2}.
\]
Then \(\mat{S}_n \to I_2\) in operator norm because \(\norm{\mat{S}_n - I_2}_{\mathrm{op}} = 1/n \to 0\). The eigenvectors of \(\mat{S}_n\), however, are the columns of \(\mat{O}_n\); they rotate by \(\pi/2\) at every step and never settle. The matrix converges; its eigenvectors do not.
\end{example}

The lesson of Example~\ref{ex:eigenvec-fail} is that eigenvectors require \emph{spectral separation}: without a gap between population eigenvalues, the eigenvectors are not stable under perturbation. This theme returns in Section~\ref{sec:spiked}, where only above a critical signal level do sample eigenvectors carry reliable information about the population spike direction.

\subsection{High Dimensions: Marchenko-Pastur Law and the Tracy-Widom Edge}\label{subsec:scm-highdim}

\gdmark{streamline}The \vocab{high-dimensional regime} is the situation in which the SCM parameters satisfy
\[
  \frac{p}{n} \;\longrightarrow\; y \;\in\; (0, \infty), \qquad \text{as } n \to \infty.
\]
The ratio \(y\) is called the \emph{aspect ratio} of the data; roughly, it is the number of features per observation, held fixed instead of becoming negligible. (Since \(n \to \infty\) and \(p/n \to y > 0\), the dimension \(p\) grows along with \(n\).)\gdmark{hdr-def}

In high dimensions, the picture of Section~\ref{subsec:scm-lowdim} breaks down qualitatively. The basic phenomenon was already visible in Figure~\ref{fig:intro-regimes} of the introduction: with \(p = 500\) and \(n = 1000\), the sample eigenvalues spread across the interval \([0.086, 2.914]\) even when \(\Sigma = I_p\). In other words, by comparison to the low-dimensional regime, they no longer converge to \(\lambda_i(\Sigma) = 1\).\gdmark{breakdown}

In high dimensions, the relevant observable may no longer be an individual eigenvalue, but the entire range of them, also called the \vocab{bulk}.\gdmark{bulk} Individual eigenvalues nonetheless remain of interest: in the second half of this section the largest one, \(\lambda_{\max}(\Sigmahat_n)\), takes center stage.\gdmark{individual-eig} To study the eigenvalues in the bulk, we first introduce the \emph{empirical spectral distribution}.


\begin{definition}[Empirical spectral distribution]\label{def:esd}
The \emph{empirical spectral distribution} (ESD) of a \(p \times p\) symmetric matrix \(A\) with eigenvalues \(\lambda_1, \ldots, \lambda_p\) is the probability measure
\[
  F_A \;=\; \frac{1}{p} \sum_{i = 1}^p \delta_{\lambda_i},
\]
placing equal mass \(1/p\) at each eigenvalue.
\end{definition}

\paragraph{The Marchenko-Pastur law.}
The shape of the limiting ESD depends only on the aspect ratio \(y\) of the data, and is the same across a wide range of entry distributions.\gdmark{mp-repeat}

\begin{theorem}[Marchenko-Pastur law \citep{MarchenkoPastur1967}]\label{thm:mp}
Let \(\mat{X} \in \R^{p \times n}\) have iid entries with mean zero, unit variance, and finite fourth moment, and let \(\Sigmahat_n = (1/n)\, \mat{X} \mat{X}\transpose\). As \(p, n \to \infty\) with \(p / n \to y \in (0, \infty)\), the random eigenvalue histogram \(F_{\Sigmahat_n}\) settles down to a fixed deterministic shape, the \vocab{Marchenko-Pastur law} \(\MP_y\): for essentially every realization, the histogram matches \(\MP_y\) as \(p, n\) grow. When \(0 < y < 1\), this distribution is given by the p.d.f.
\begin{equation}
  f_{\MP_y}(\lambda) \;=\; \frac{1}{2 \pi y \lambda}\, \sqrt{(\lambda_+ - \lambda)(\lambda - \lambda_-)}, \qquad \lambda \in [\lambda_-, \lambda_+],
  \label{eq:mp-density}
\end{equation}
where \(\lambda_\pm = (1 \pm \sqrt{y})^2\). If \(y > 1\), the continuous part still follows~\eqref{eq:mp-density} on \([\lambda_-, \lambda_+]\) with the same \(\lambda_\pm\); the only change is an extra point mass \(1 - 1/y\) at \(\lambda = 0\), from the forced zero eigenvalues.\gdmark{mp-pdf-range}
\end{theorem}

A complete proof passes through the Stieltjes transform of \(F_{\Sigmahat_n}\) and is beyond the scope of this report; see \citet[][Thm.~2.5]{YaoZhengBai2015} or \citet[][Thm.~3.6]{Vershynin2018}. The hypothesis covers Gaussian entries (the main case we simulate) and many other distributions with finite fourth moment.\gdmark{mp-redundant}

Figure~\ref{fig:mp-varying-c} shows the law for four aspect ratios. The shape changes dramatically with \(y\): for \(y\) small the support is a narrow band around \(\lambda = 1\) (the low-dimensional regime is recovered as \(y \to 0\)); for \(y\) near one the density piles up at zero; for \(y > 1\) the matrix \(\Sigmahat_n\) becomes rank-deficient and \(F_{\Sigmahat_n}\) carries an explicit Dirac mass at zero on top of the continuous bulk. A high-resolution single-realization companion plot is included in Appendix~\ref{app:simulation}, Figure~\ref{fig:mp-bulk-single}.

  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{figures/mp_varying_c.png}
  \caption{The Marchenko-Pastur law for four aspect ratios \(y \in \{0.1,\, 0.5,\, 1,\, 2\}\). Each panel pools eigenvalues from many independent realizations of \(\Sigmahat_n = (1/n)\, \mat{X} \mat{X}\transpose\) with iid \(\Normal{0, 1}\) entries (at least \(10{,}000\) eigenvalues per panel), overlaid on the theoretical \(\MP_y\) density~\eqref{eq:mp-density} (solid curve).   \label{fig:mp-varying-c}

Three features of the MP law are worth recording.
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*,itemsep=0.3em]
  \item For \(0 < y < 1\),\gdmark{support-range} the support is the interval \([(1 - \sqrt{y})^2, (1 + \sqrt{y})^2]\), of width \(4 \sqrt{y}\). As \(y \to 0\) the support collapses to the point \(\lambda = 1\), recovering the low-dimensional consistency of Section~\ref{subsec:scm-lowdim}.
  \item Near the upper edge \(\lambda_+\), the density vanishes like a square root: \(f_{\MP_y}(\lambda) \sim C(\lambda_+ - \lambda)^{1/2}\). This square-root edge is the input to the Tracy-Widom heuristic below.\gdmark{nice}
  \item When \(y > 1\) the atom at zero has mass \(1 - 1/y\); the matrix \(\mat{X} \mat{X}\transpose\) has rank at most \(n < p\), so \(p - n\) of its eigenvalues are deterministically zero.
\end{itemize}

The largest sample eigenvalue converges to the upper edge of the bulk.\gdmark{exits} In the following theorem, we describe this convergence for the top eigenvalue of SCMs in the high-dimensional regime.\gdmark{topeig-leadin}
\begin{theorem}[Convergence of the top eigenvalue \citep{BaikSilverstein2006}]\label{thm:lambda-max-edge}
Under the hypotheses of Theorem~\ref{thm:mp},
\[
  \lambda_{\max}(\Sigmahat_n) \;\convas\; \lambda_+ \;=\; (1 + \sqrt{y})^2, \qquad n \to \infty.
\]
\end{theorem}

Its importance notwithstanding, Theorem~\ref{thm:lambda-max-edge} settles \emph{where} \(\lambda_{\max}(\Sigmahat_n)\) lives in the limit but not \emph{how} it fluctuates around \(\lambda_+\). Unlike in low dimensions, the fluctuations of \(\lambda_{\max}(\Sigmahat_n)\) in high dimensions cannot be Gaussian. In fact, the geometry on the two sides of \(\lambda_{\max}\) is fundamentally different, which can be explained schematically as follows.\gdmark{where-how}

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*,itemsep=0.25em]
  \item \emph{To the left,} the other \(p - 1\) sample eigenvalues are densely packed and repel each other (in the Gaussian case through the Vandermonde factor of~\eqref{eq:vandermonde}; the same crowding holds more generally), exerting an upward ``pressure'' that resists \(\lambda_{\max}\) drifting back into the bulk.
  \item \emph{To the right,} the support of the MP density ends at \(\lambda_+\); past the edge there is nothing, so excursions above \(\lambda_+\) are unconstrained by neighbouring eigenvalues.
\end{itemize}
Figure~\ref{fig:edge-repulsion} makes this geometry tangible: across hundreds of realizations, the second- and third-largest eigenvalues sit packed tightly against \(\lambda_+\) from below, while \(\lambda_{\max}\) itself scatters substantially on both sides of the edge. The increasing width of the rows as we move from \(\lambda_{\max-5}\) up to \(\lambda_{\max}\) is the Vandermonde repulsion in action, holding consecutive eigenvalues apart while pushing the top one outward.

  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{figures/edge_repulsion.png}
  \caption{Top six eigenvalues of \(\Sigmahat_n\) across \(R = 500\) realizations at \(n = 600\), \(p = 300\) (\(y = 1/2\)). Each row shows the empirical locations of the eigenvalue of that rank; the rows are vertically jittered for visibility. The dashed line marks the MP upper edge \(\lambda_+\). The rows below \(\lambda_{\max}\) cluster against \(\lambda_+\) from below and never overshoot it; only \(\lambda_{\max}\) scatters past the edge. This asymmetric ``one-sided pressure'' is the geometric source of the TW\(_1\) right skew. Generated by \texttt{notebooks/03\_section3\_demos.py}.}
  \label{fig:edge-repulsion}
\end{figure}

Two regimes, two different restoring mechanisms: the limit is right-skewed, with mean \(\approx -1.207\) and standard deviation \(\approx 1.268\). It is the \emph{Tracy-Widom Type 1 distribution}, discovered by \citet{TracyWidom1996} as the top-eigenvalue limit of the GOE; \citet{Wolchover2014} is an accessible non-technical introduction. The mean is negative, not zero, because the standardized top eigenvalue usually sits a little \emph{below} the centering \(\mu_{n p}\): the bulk pushes \(\lambda_{\max}\) up toward the edge from one side only, so it typically lands just inside the edge, and the right skew comes from the rarer excursions past it.

We record the formal definition for completeness.\gdmark{tw1-leadin} It is not needed to follow anything below: the reader can treat \(\mathrm{TW}_1\) as a named distribution whose shape is the one plotted in Figure~\ref{fig:tw-universality}, and read on.
\begin{definition}[Tracy-Widom Type 1 distribution]\label{def:tw1}
The \vocab{Tracy-Widom Type 1} law \(\mathrm{TW}_1\) is the probability distribution on \(\R\) with cumulative distribution function
\begin{equation}
  F_1(s) \;=\; \exp\!\left(\!-\frac{1}{2}\int_s^\infty \!\Bigl[ q(x) \;+\; (x - s)\, q(x)^2 \Bigr]\, dx \right), \qquad s \in \R,
  \label{eq:tw1-cdf}
\end{equation}
where \(q\) is the unique \emph{Hastings-McLeod solution} \citep{HastingsMcLeod1980} of the Painlev\'e II equation
\begin{equation}
  q''(x) \;=\; x\, q(x) \;+\; 2\, q(x)^3,
  \qquad q(x) \;\sim\; \mathrm{Ai}(x) \;\text{ as } x \to +\infty,
  \label{eq:painleve-II}
\end{equation}
with \(\mathrm{Ai}\) the Airy function.
\end{definition}

\(\mathrm{TW}_1\) has no closed-form density: the CDF~\eqref{eq:tw1-cdf} is defined indirectly through the Painlev\'e II ODE, and the Hastings-McLeod boundary condition pins down the unique solution that decays correctly at \(+\infty\). The same distribution arises in problems far from random matrices, the longest increasing subsequence of a random permutation \citep{BaikDeiftJohansson1999} being the canonical example. That one law recurs across so many unrelated models is an instance of \vocab{universality}.\gdmark{universality} We use \(\mathrm{TW}_1\) here only through its name and its empirical density, plotted in Figure~\ref{fig:tw-universality}.


\begin{theorem}[Tracy-Widom edge for the null sample covariance, \citealp{Johnstone2001}]\label{thm:tw}
Under the hypotheses of Theorem~\ref{thm:mp}, define
\begin{equation}
  \mu_{n p} \;=\; \frac{(\sqrt{n - 1} + \sqrt{p})^2}{n}, \qquad
  \sigma_{n p} \;=\; \frac{\sqrt{n - 1} + \sqrt{p}}{n}\, \left(\frac{1}{\sqrt{n - 1}} + \frac{1}{\sqrt{p}}\right)^{1/3}.
  \label{eq:tw-constants}
\end{equation}
Then
\begin{equation}\label{e:lambda-max_Johnstone_TW1}
  \frac{\lambda_{\max}(\Sigmahat_n) - \mu_{n p}}{\sigma_{n p}} \;\convd\; \mathrm{TW}_1,
\end{equation}
where \(\mathrm{TW}_1\) is the distribution of Definition~\ref{def:tw1}.
\end{theorem}

The two constants have transparent limits: \(\mu_{n p} \to (1 + \sqrt{y})^2 = \lambda_+\), so the centering locks onto the MP upper edge, while \(\sigma_{n p}\) is of order \(n^{-2/3}\), the edge scale explained heuristically below.\gdmark{heuristic-reorder} The same limit can be written with the rate factored out, as in \citet[][Prop.~3.9]{Genzer2025}:
\begin{equation}
  n^{2/3}\,\frac{\lambda_{\max}(\Sigmahat_n) - \mu_{n p}}{\tilde\sigma} \;\convd\; \mathrm{TW}_1, \qquad \tilde\sigma \to (1 + \sqrt{y})\bigl(1 + 1/\sqrt{y}\bigr)^{1/3},
  \label{e:Genzer2025_lambda-max}
\end{equation}
and the two forms~\eqref{e:lambda-max_Johnstone_TW1} and~\eqref{e:Genzer2025_lambda-max} are algebraically identical: the only difference is bookkeeping, with Genzer pulling the \(n^{2/3}\) rate out in front while Johnstone folds it into \(\sigma_{n p}\). Both say the same thing, that \(\lambda_{\max}\) fluctuates on the \(n^{-2/3}\) scale with a \(\mathrm{TW}_1\) shape. These constants, and the first proof that the largest eigenvalue of a null sample covariance matrix has a Tracy-Widom limit, are due to \citet{Johnstone2001} (together with \citealp{Johansson2000}).\gdmark{johnstone-attr} We use Theorem~\ref{thm:tw} as a black box here; the same constants reappear in Section~\ref{sec:spiked}, where we ask which population eigenvalues exceed this null \(\mathrm{TW}_1\) baseline.

\paragraph{Where the edge scale comes from.}
The proof of Theorem~\ref{thm:tw} is technical, but a short heuristic recovers the \(n^{-2/3}\) scale from the square-root shape of the MP density alone.\gdmark{exponent} Near the upper edge the density vanishes like a square root,
\begin{equation}
  f_{\MP_y}(\lambda) \;\sim\; C\,(\lambda_+ - \lambda)^{1/2}, \qquad \lambda \uparrow \lambda_+.
  \label{e:f-MP_near_edge}
\end{equation}
Pretend for a moment that the \(p\) eigenvalues are \(p\) independent draws from the MP law. They are not independent (we saw above that they repel), but this crude picture gets the scale right. Then \(\lambda_{\max}\) is the largest of the \(p\) draws, and on average just one of them lands in the top interval \([\lambda_{\max}, \lambda_+]\), so that interval should hold about a \(1/p\) share of the total mass:
\begin{equation}
  \frac{1}{p} \;\sim\; \int_{\lambda_{\max}}^{\lambda_+} f_{\MP_y}(\lambda)\, d\lambda \;\sim\; \tfrac{2 C}{3}\, (\lambda_+ - \lambda_{\max})^{3/2},
  \label{e:f-MP-near_edge_scaling}
\end{equation}
where the second estimate uses~\eqref{e:f-MP_near_edge}. Solving for the gap gives \(\lambda_+ - \lambda_{\max} \sim p^{-2/3}\): the \(3/2\) power from integrating the square-root edge inverts to the \(2/3\) in the gap. Since \(p / n \to y\) is a fixed positive constant, \(p\) and \(n\) grow in lockstep, so this is the same \(n^{-2/3}\) edge scale that \(\sigma_{n p}\) carries in~\eqref{eq:tw-constants}.

Figure~\ref{fig:tw-edge} shows the empirical distribution of \(z\) collapsing to a common shape as \(p, n\) grow at fixed aspect ratio. Figure~\ref{fig:tw-universality} fixes a single large \((n, p)\) and compares the histogram of \(z\) directly to the TW\(_1\) density, with a Q-Q plot of \(z\) against \(\mathcal{N}(0, 1)\) on the side.

\paragraph{Quantitative tests of normality.}
The visual non-normality in Figures~\ref{fig:tw-edge} and~\ref{fig:tw-universality} can be backed up by classical goodness-of-fit tests. Two natural choices are the \emph{Kolmogorov-Smirnov} test, with statistic
\[
  D_R \;=\; \sup_x \, \abs{F_R(x) - F(x)}
\]
measuring the largest gap between the empirical CDF \(F_R\) of an \(R\)-sample and a reference CDF \(F\), and the \emph{Shapiro-Wilk} test, whose null hypothesis is that the sample comes from \emph{some} normal distribution, without specifying its mean or variance. Applied to the \(R = 5000\) standardized samples \(z\) from Figure~\ref{fig:tw-universality} (further centred to mean \(0\) and scaled to variance \(1\) before the KS comparison against \(\Normal{0,1}\), so that the test is sensitive to shape rather than location), both reject normality decisively:
\[
  D_{5000} = 0.029, \;\; p = 3.5 \times 10^{-4} \qquad\text{(Kolmogorov-Smirnov)},
\]
\[
  W = 0.995, \;\; p = 5.1 \times 10^{-12} \qquad\text{(Shapiro-Wilk)}.
\]
The Shapiro-Wilk statistic \(W\) is close to \(1\) (the value attained by a perfect normal sample), but at \(R = 5000\) trials even a small departure shows up at machine-precision \(p\)-values; the KS statistic is similarly tiny in absolute terms yet far outside the null distribution. For comparison, a control sample of \(5000\) genuine \(\Normal{0, 1}\) draws yields \(D = 0.017\) (\(p \approx 0.12\)) and \(W = 0.9997\) (\(p \approx 0.69\)), neither of which rejects normality. The TW\(_1\)-distributed top eigenvalue is therefore non-Gaussian by every standard diagnostic, visual and quantitative.

The contrast between the two regimes is sharp. In fixed dimension, the sample covariance matrix behaves like an ordinary average: its entries converge to those of \(\Sigma\), and at a simple population eigenvalue the corresponding sample eigenvalue fluctuates Gaussianly on the \(\sqrt{n}\) scale via Lemma~\ref{lem:first-order-perturbation}. At a repeated population eigenvalue the limiting object is instead a finite-dimensional GOE matrix, with the non-Gaussian top-eigenvalue law that reflects eigenvalue repulsion. In high dimensions, by contrast, the sample eigenvalues no longer collapse to the population eigenvalues. Even for \(\Sigma = I_p\), they fill the Marchenko-Pastur interval \([\lambda_-, \lambda_+]\), and the largest eigenvalue sits at the right edge \(\lambda_+\) with Tracy-Widom \(\mathrm{TW}_1\) fluctuations on the \(p^{-2/3}\) scale.

The next section inserts signal into this null picture by allowing a small number of population eigenvalues to rise above the unit background, and asks when the corresponding sample eigenvalue and eigenvector can recover that signal.
